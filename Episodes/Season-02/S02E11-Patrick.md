# S02E11 — "Patrick"
*c. 385–461 AD · The slave who went back*

## The Primary Sources

**Patrick's *Confession*** (c. 450 AD) — one of the most personal documents in Christian history:
> "I was then about sixteen years of age. I did not know the true God. I was taken into captivity in Ireland with many thousands of people... And there the Lord opened my heart to an awareness of my unbelief, in order that, even if late, I might remember my transgressions and turn with all my heart to the Lord my God."

He was enslaved in Ireland for six years, escaped, returned to Britain, trained, and went back:
> "I feel certain in my heart that I am bound by the Spirit of God... I must go to Ireland and spend myself for the sake of strangers."

**The Letter to Coroticus** — a letter of fierce condemnation to a British Christian chieftain who had raided an Irish Christian community and sold the baptized into slavery. The letter is raw anger at Christians treating other Christians as property.

## What Patrick Did
Patrick did not single-handedly convert Ireland — that took centuries. But he:
- Converted the *ard rí* (high kings) of several provincial kingdoms
- Established a church that operated through tribal rather than episcopal structures
- Created a Christianity that was distinctly Irish — incorporating Irish cultural forms
- Left behind a church that produced the great Irish monasteries (Clonmacnoise, Bangor, Iona)

**The Celtic model**: Working within existing social structures; converting leaders who convert communities; using vernacular culture rather than imposing Latin forms. Matteo Ricci in China 1,100 years later used the same approach.

## Celtic Christianity's Legacy
From Ireland, the faith re-evangelized Scotland and northern England:
- **Columba** founds Iona (563 AD)
- Iona trains **Aidan**, who founds Lindisfarne
- From Lindisfarne, Christianity spreads back across northern England
- The **Lindisfarne Gospels** (c. 715 AD) — one of the greatest works of art in Western history — comes from this tradition

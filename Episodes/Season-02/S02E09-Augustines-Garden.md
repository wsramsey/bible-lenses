# S02E09 — "Augustine's Garden"
*354–430 AD · The most influential theologian in Western history*

## The Story
A man spends 32 years looking for truth in all the wrong places — Manichaeism, academic skepticism, Neoplatonism, ambition, a relationship he loves and destroys — and finds it in a garden in Milan, hearing a child's voice say "take up and read."

## The Primary Sources

**The Confessions** (c. 397–400 AD) — the first spiritual autobiography:
> "Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee."

The entire work is addressed to God — a prayer structured as a memoir. It covers:
- His birth in Thagaste, North Africa (354 AD)
- His mother Monica's unceasing prayer
- His 15-year relationship with a concubine he loved and abandoned ("my heart was broken and wounded and shed blood")
- His son Adeodatus ("the son of my sin") who died young
- His career in rhetoric — Milan, the imperial city
- His reading of Cicero at 19: "the book changed my affections and turned my prayers to Thyself"
- His 9 years as a Manichaean
- His reading of Plotinus (the Platonists): they showed him that immaterial reality exists; they could not show him Christ
- The garden, Milan, 386: the voice, the children's game, Romans 13:13-14

**The garden passage:**
> "I heard from a neighboring house a voice, as of boy or girl, I know not, chanting, and oft repeating, 'Take up and read; Take up and read.' ... I seized, opened, and in silence read that section on which my eyes first fell: 'Not in rioting and drunkenness, not in chambering and wantonness, not in strife and envying; but put ye on the Lord Jesus Christ, and make not provision for the flesh, in concupiscence.' No further would I read; nor needed I: for instantly at the end of this sentence, by a light as it were of serenity infused into my heart, all the darkness of doubt vanished away."

## The Intellectual Contribution

**What Augustine gave Western Christianity:**
- The doctrine of **original sin** as inherited corruption (not just imitation) — from Romans 5:12 interpreted through Latin translation (*in quo* = "in whom" rather than Greek "because")
- **Predestination** — God's election is not based on foreseen merit; grace precedes and enables faith
- **The two cities** — the City of God (ordered by love of God) and the City of Man (ordered by love of self); the pilgrim church in the earthly city
- **Just war theory** — the conditions under which violence is justified; the foundational Christian political theology

## The Fall of Rome (410 AD)
Alaric's Visigoths sack Rome on August 24, 410. The psychological earthquake: the eternal city, no longer eternal. Pagans blame Christians — their God failed them. Augustine begins *The City of God* (413–426) — 13 years of writing — partly as a response. He dies in 430 with the Vandals besieging Hippo.

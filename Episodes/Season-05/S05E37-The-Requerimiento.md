# S05E37 — "The Requerimiento"
*1492–1560 AD · The document that is read to people who can't hear it*

## The Story
The Spanish conquistadors carry a legal document called the Requerimiento. Before attacking an indigenous community, a notary reads it aloud — in Spanish — to people who speak no Spanish and have never heard of the Pope. The document explains that the Pope has given their lands to Spain. If they submit, they will be treated well. If they don't, any harm done to them is their fault.

Bartolomé de las Casas, who witnessed this, wrote: "I don't know whether to laugh or cry."

## The Primary Sources

**The Requerimiento** (1513, Juan López de Palacios Rubios):
> "We ask and require... that you consider what we have told you; that you take the time that shall be necessary to understand and deliberate upon it, and that you acknowledge the Church as the Ruler and Superior of the whole world... If you do so, you will do well... and we shall receive you in all love and charity... But, if you do not do this, and maliciously make delay in it... we protest that the deaths and losses which shall accrue from this are your fault, and not ours."

**Las Casas's account** (*A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies*, 1542):
> "The reason the Christians have murdered on such a vast scale and killed anyone and everyone in their way is purely and simply greed."

Las Casas estimated 12–15 million indigenous deaths in the Caribbean alone in the first 50 years. Modern scholars estimate 90% population collapse in the Caribbean by 1550 — primarily through disease, but accelerated enormously by violence, enslavement, and disruption of food systems.

**The Valladolid Debate** (1550–1551):
The only formal debate in history over whether it was legal to conquer an indigenous people. Las Casas vs. Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda. Sepúlveda: the indigenous people are natural slaves (Aristotle's argument); they commit human sacrifice; they need to be civilized. Las Casas: they are fully human; their humanity cannot be used to justify enslaving them; the Gospel is not spread by violence.

No winner was declared. The debate continued. The killing continued.

**Francis Xavier** (1506–1552) — the other side:
The Jesuit who traveled to India, Sri Lanka, Malacca, Japan, and died trying to reach China. He *asked* for the Inquisition in Goa; he worked within Portuguese colonial structures; he also lived among the poor, learned local languages, and reportedly baptized hundreds of thousands. The same tradition, simultaneously.

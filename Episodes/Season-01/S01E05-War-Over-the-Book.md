# S01E05 — "The War Over the Book"
*140–400 AD · Which writings are authoritative?*

## The Story
Marcion arrives in Rome c. 140 with a radical proposal: Jesus has nothing to do with the Jewish God; the OT is the record of an inferior deity; Paul (edited) and Luke are the only true Scriptures. The church's response — we need a *better* canon, not no canon — launches the process that gives us the Bible.

## The Historical Evidence

**The Marcionite Crisis:**
Marcion donated 200,000 sesterces to the Roman church when he arrived — a wealthy shipping merchant. He was excommunicated within two years. His canon: an edited Luke (Marcion removed what he saw as Jewish interpolations) and 10 Pauline letters (no Pastorals). The church returned his money and kept his challenge: it forced the question of which writings were authoritative.

**The Muratorian Fragment** (c. 170–200 AD, discovered 1740):
The oldest surviving canonical list. It names four Gospels, Acts, 13 Pauline letters, Jude, 1-2 John, Revelation, the Wisdom of Solomon. It explicitly excludes the Shepherd of Hermas ("too recent") and the Epistle of Paul to the Alexandrians ("a forgery"). The disputed books are discussed.

**Athanasius's Easter Letter** (367 AD):
The first known document listing exactly our 27-book NT. Athanasius — after 50 years of exile and return — writes an annual Easter letter to Egyptian churches. In 367 he lists the books and says: "these are the fountains of salvation."

**Key data points:**
- The NT canon was not decided by a single council
- Regional councils (Hippo 393, Carthage 397) ratified an existing consensus
- The Deuterocanonical books (Tobit, Judith, Maccabees, Sirach, Wisdom, Baruch) were in the Greek OT used by the early church; Jerome noted their secondary status; the Reformation removed them; Trent (1546) defined them as canonical for Catholics
- The Ethiopian canon still includes 1 Enoch and Jubilees — books quoted in the NT (Jude 14) but excluded everywhere else

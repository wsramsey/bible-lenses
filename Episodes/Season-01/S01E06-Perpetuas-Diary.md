# S01E06 — "Perpetua's Diary"
*203 AD · The oldest surviving Christian writing by a woman*

## The Primary Source
The *Passion of Perpetua and Felicity* is one of the most extraordinary documents in Christian history. Part of it — the prison diary — was written by Perpetua herself; part by her companion Saturus; the account of their deaths by an eyewitness editor. Tertullian may have been the editor.

**Perpetua** was 22, a noblewoman, nursing a baby, arrested in Carthage (North Africa) with several companions during the persecution of Septimius Severus.

## Key Passages

**Her father's visit:**
> "While I was still with the police authorities, my father out of love for me tried to dissuade me from my purpose... 'Father,' said I, 'do you see, let us say, that vessel lying there, that little pitcher, or whatever it may be?' And he said, 'I see it.' And I said to him, 'Can it be called by any other name than what it is?' And he answered, 'No.' 'So also I cannot call myself anything else than what I am, a Christian.'"

**Her vision of the ladder:**
She dreams of a narrow bronze ladder stretching to heaven, with swords and spears attached to its sides. A dragon at the foot. She steps on the dragon's head and climbs. At the top: a white-haired shepherd milking sheep in a vast garden. "Welcome, child." She wakes knowing she will die.

**On the nursing child taken from her:**
The baby was taken; Perpetua asked for it; was refused; miraculously the baby no longer needed feeding and Perpetua's milk dried up without pain. She interpreted this as a sign: the child would be cared for.

**The arena:**
She and Felicity (who had just given birth) were "tossed and fell to the ground." The crowd demanded more. Perpetua was led back. The gladiator who was to kill her was nervous; his sword stroke was not clean; she guided his hand to her own throat.

The editor writes: "Perhaps so great a woman could not else have been slain... had she not herself willed it."

## Why This Episode Matters
- The oldest Christian document written by a woman
- A primary-source account of what martyrdom actually felt like from the inside
- A woman with real power making a real choice — not a passive victim
- The nursing mother as martyr: the most complete human selflessness
- The Roman context: the arena as entertainment, the crowd's expectations, the bureaucratic machinery of execution

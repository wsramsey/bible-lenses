# S01E02 — "The Road to Rome"
*46–68 AD · 10,000 miles and 13 letters*

## The Story
A man who watched people die for this faith now risks his life for it. Three missionary journeys. 10,000 miles. Letters written to communities he loves and worries about. The first Christian theology produced not in a library but on the road, in prison, in the middle of arguments about whether Gentiles need to be circumcised.

## The Historical Evidence

**Paul's letters — the oldest Christian documents:**
- Galatians (c. 48–49 AD): The first surviving Christian document; Paul arguing with Peter face-to-face about the Gentile question
- 1 Thessalonians (c. 50 AD): The oldest complete Pauline letter; Paul writing to a community he founded six months earlier
- 1 Corinthians (c. 54 AD): The messiest letter — sex, lawsuits, spiritual gifts, the Lord's Supper, resurrection; the most human picture of an early church

**Archaeological confirmation:**
- **The Gallio Inscription** (Delphi, 1905): Confirms Gallio as proconsul of Achaia "during the 26th acclamation of Emperor Claudius" (c. 51–52 AD) — precisely when Acts 18:12 places Paul in Corinth before Gallio. The tightest chronological anchor for Paul's career.
- **The Erastus Inscription** (Corinth, 1929): A stone inscription naming "Erastus, curator of public buildings" — almost certainly the Erastus of Romans 16:23 ("Erastus, who is the city's director of public works, sends his greetings")
- **The Areopagus** (Athens): Paul's sermon location; still standing; you can stand where he stood

**The Council of Jerusalem (49 AD):**
Paul and Barnabas travel to Jerusalem to settle whether Gentile converts need circumcision. The decision — no — is the most consequential in Christian history. Galatians 2 gives Paul's account; Acts 15 gives Luke's. They don't fully agree. The disagreement is itself evidence: this is history, not legend.

## The Narrative Arc
Paul arrives in Rome under house arrest, c. 60 AD. He writes Philippians — "I have learned to be content whatever the circumstances." Nero burns Rome in 64 AD. The Christians are blamed. The tradition says Peter and Paul both die in Rome in this persecution. The movement loses its two greatest leaders within years of each other. And grows faster than ever.

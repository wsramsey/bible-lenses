# The Complete Visual Arc — What You See

*The full journey from Pentecost to now, told through paintings, artifacts, and faces*

---

## Era 1: Origins (30–200 AD)
**Visual palette**: Stone. Sand. Papyrus. The colors of the ancient Mediterranean.

**Opening image**: The Catacombs of Priscilla, Rome. The Good Shepherd fresco. C. 250 AD. The oldest surviving image of Christ. A young man with a sheep on his shoulders. No halo. No cross. Anonymous. *Why didn't they paint him the way we paint him?*

**Sequence**:
- The fish symbol scratched in stone → the Chi-Rho on a soldier's shield → the first baptismal font at Dura-Europos (c. 240 AD)
- Pliny's letter to Trajan (manuscript page) → the Roman arena → Perpetua's face (reconstructed)
- Paul's letter to the Corinthians (papyrus fragment) → the Areopagus in Athens → the Gallio Inscription

**Closing image**: The Pantheon in Rome, interior dome. Still standing. Built 126 AD. The most preserved building from antiquity. *This building was here when Paul was arrested 100 meters away. It's still here. The churches that replaced it are mostly gone.*

---

## Era 2: Councils and Creeds (300–600 AD)
**Visual palette**: Gold. Mosaic. Byzantine richness emerging.

**Opening image**: Constantine's colossal head, Musei Capitolini, Rome. Just the head — 2.6 meters tall. One eye slightly larger than the other. The gaze that was meant to be divine. *What does power do to a face?*

**Sequence**:
- The Nicaea council icon (standard Byzantine type) → Athanasius's exile routes mapped → the Arian baptistery in Ravenna (the mosaics the losing side left)
- Augustine's garden, Milan → the Confessions manuscript → Philippe de Champaigne's Augustine portrait
- The Hagia Sophia exterior → interior dome → the gold mosaic of Christ Pantocrator in the apse

**The Hagia Sophia sequence** (Ken Burns, 90 seconds):
Slow pan from the exterior through the narthex into the nave. As the dome comes into view, the sound changes — the Cappella Romana recording of Byzantine liturgy in the Hagia Sophia acoustic. The reverb tail. *When the liturgy was sung here, the dome made it sound like the voice of God.*

**Closing image**: The mosaic of Justinian in San Vitale, Ravenna. The emperor holding the Eucharist. His feet not quite touching the ground. *He didn't ask permission to be shown this way.*

---

## Era 3: The Medieval World (600–1200 AD)
**Visual palette**: Vellum. Stone. Candle-light illumination.

**Opening image**: The Book of Kells, Chi-Rho page. Full screen. Zoom in on the detail — at maximum zoom, there are tiny human faces hidden in the interlace. *Irish monks hid themselves in the art. Find them.*

**Sequence**:
- Charlemagne's equestrian statuette (Louvre) → the Carolingian minuscule script → a page of Alcuin's Bible
- The Romanesque portal at Autun — Gislebertus's Last Judgment tympanum — zoom into the faces of the damned
- Bernard of Clairvaux's portrait → a Cistercian abbey cloister → a page from his Sermons on the Song of Songs

**The Cathedral Sequence** (Ken Burns, 2 minutes):
Chartres exterior at dawn → the rose window from outside → entering through the Royal Portal → the nave, looking up → the light through the stained glass. The Gregorian chant building under it. No narration. Just the experience.

*Then, cut: the sound of construction. Hammering. Men on scaffolding 50 meters up, knowing they'll never see it finished.*

**Thinker window**: Thomas Aquinas — Fra Angelico's fresco, Cell 7, San Marco. The hunched figure in prayer. Not triumphant. Working.

**Closing image**: The Bayeux Tapestry — the section showing the comet of 1066. The figures below it, pointing up, afraid. *The comet meant something was ending. It was right.*

---

## Era 4: Crisis and Renewal (1200–1400 AD)
**Visual palette**: Darker. The Black Death palette — grey, brown, flashes of gold.

**Opening image**: Grünewald's Isenheim Altarpiece, crucifixion panel. Slowly revealed — you see the hands first, clawing at nothing. *This was painted for a hospital. The patients were dying. This was what they saw every day.*

**Sequence**:
- Duccio's Entry into Jerusalem → Giotto's Lamentation → the shift from Byzantine flat to proto-Renaissance depth
- A page from Julian of Norwich's Revelations → her anchorite cell (exterior, Norwich Cathedral) → close on her face
- The Plague sequence: a 14th-century illustration of the flagellants → a town register with death dates → Machaut's *Messe de Nostre Dame* (the only Mass composed during the Black Death itself)

**The Black Death sound sequence** (Radiolab, 90 seconds):
A single tolling bell. Then another. Then they overlap. Then so many bells they become noise. Then silence. *In some towns, there was no one left to ring the bells.*

**Thinker window**: Meister Eckhart — the carved relief at Erfurt Cathedral. The face of a man thinking something dangerous. *He was tried for heresy. He died before the verdict. His books were condemned. They survived.*

**Closing image**: The Ghent Altarpiece interior, revealed very slowly. The Lamb at the center, blood flowing into a chalice. The processions of all the saints converging from every direction. *This was painted 12 years after the Black Death ended. It's the most elaborate vision of heaven ever painted. After everything that had just happened, someone needed to paint this.*

---

## Era 5: The Reformation (1450–1620 AD)
**Visual palette**: Stark. Black and white emerging. The printing press aesthetic.

**Opening image**: A page of the Gutenberg Bible. Both columns. The illuminated capital letter. The meticulous type. Zoom in until individual letters fill the screen. *Every letter was set by hand. Five days of work for one page. Within 20 years: 1,000 editions in circulation.*

**The Printing Press Sequence** (Radiolab, 60 seconds):
The sound of a press: rhythmic, mechanical, relentless. Under it: images of pamphlets multiplying, pages flying, maps of distribution. Fast cut. The information revolution.

**Sequence**:
- Lucas Cranach's Luther portrait (Uffizi) → the 95 Theses manuscript → Worms Cathedral interior
- The Diet of Worms room → the Wartburg room where Luther translated the NT → a page of his German Bible
- Raphael's School of Athens → the Vatican rooms → the Sistine ceiling → Julius II's tomb (Michelangelo's Moses)

**Thinker window**: Calvin — the Geneva portrait. Severe. Unsmiling. *He said: 'One of the clearest proofs of our corruption is the fact that we find pleasure in elaborate ceremonies.' He designed a church that was all white walls and the spoken Word. The building was the theology.*

**Counter-voice**: El Greco's Burial of the Count of Orgaz — the Counter-Reformation answer. Heaven and earth meeting. Silk and color and gold. *The other side's answer to Calvinist simplicity. Which one is more Christian? Both traditions are still arguing.*

**Closing image**: Zurbarán's *Agnus Dei* — the lamb alone on the stone slab. Nothing else in the frame. *After 150 years of theological argument, here is what it comes down to: a lamb. Bound. About to be sacrificed. This is the thing everyone was fighting about.*

---

## Era 6: New Worlds (1492–1800 AD)
**Visual palette**: Tropical green bleeding into dark ocean.

**Opening image**: The Theodor de Bry engravings of the indigenous Americas. European eyes on a world they'd never seen. *These images shaped how Europe imagined the New World for 200 years. They were made by a man who had never been there.*

**The Ocean Sequence** (Ken Burns, 60 seconds):
A painting of the Atlantic. Slow pan. Nothing but water. The Radiolab move: under the ocean sound, very faintly, the beginning of "Go Down Moses." Then it stops. The ocean continues.

**Sequence**:
- The Requerimiento document (manuscript) → a map of the conquests → Las Casas's portrait
- The Jesuit Reductions of Paraguay — the ruins today → the restored instruments → the Guaraní Baroque music recording
- Rembrandt's Sacrifice of Isaac → his Jewish Amsterdam neighbors' faces → the 1600 Amsterdam Jewish Quarter

**Thinker window**: Bartolomé de las Casas — no portrait survives. A painting of the Valladolid Debate instead. *He argued that indigenous peoples were fully human. He argued this in 1550. It should not have needed arguing.*

**Closing image**: Rembrandt's Return of the Prodigal Son. Very slow reveal — just the hands first. The father's hands on the son's shoulders. *The greatest painting of grace in Western art was made by a Protestant painter in a city that had been liberated from Catholic Spain. He was bankrupt when he painted it. He understood coming home with nothing.*

---

## Era 7: Awakenings and Abolition (1700–1865 AD)
**Visual palette**: American. Wide sky. Revival tent canvas. Firelight.

**Opening image**: Jonathan Edwards's portrait. The face of a man who has seen something. *He was the greatest theologian America has produced. He preached the most famous sermon in American history. He read it in a flat monotone. People screamed.*

**Sequence**:
- A revival camp meeting illustration → the circuit preacher on horseback → Wesley's portrait
- Harriet Tubman's photograph (c. 1885) → the Underground Railroad routes mapped → "Go Down Moses" (Marian Anderson recording)
- William Wilberforce's portrait → the House of Commons painting → the slave ship *Brooks* diagram

**The Spiritual Sequence** (Radiolab, 90 seconds):
The *Brooks* diagram fills the screen — the slave ship cross-section, every human being reduced to geometry. Silence. Then: Marian Anderson's "Were You There" begins. No transition. No explanation. The juxtaposition is the argument.

**Thinker window**: Frederick Douglass — his photograph (c. 1847, National Portrait Gallery). The most photographed American of the 19th century. He understood that images were arguments. *He wrote three autobiographies. Each one described the same events. The details changed as his understanding of them deepened.*

**Closing image**: The Second Great Awakening camp meeting — a contemporary illustration. Thousands of people in a field. Firelight. The chaos of the Spirit. *This is where America's religious DNA was made. Not in the churches. In a field. With everyone.*

---

## Era 8: The Modern Crucible (1860–1945 AD)
**Visual palette**: Photographic. Black and white. Then color bursting in.

**Opening image**: The Isenheim Altarpiece again — but now we know it was hanging above Barth's desk when he wrote his *Romans* commentary. *The same painting. A different century. What does it mean to choose this as the image you work under?*

**The WWI Sequence** (Radiolab, 60 seconds):
A soldier's photograph → the Western Front aerial photograph (just mud) → a phonograph in a trench playing a hymn, badly → the 93 intellectuals' manifesto (single line shown) → silence.

**Sequence**:
- Barth's portrait (dark, intense) → the manuscript of his *Romans* commentary → the Barmen Declaration
- Bonhoeffer's prison photograph → his handwriting in the margin of his Bible → Flossenbürg today
- The March on Washington photograph → MLK's face → the Lincoln Memorial steps

**Thinker window**: Dietrich Bonhoeffer — his prison photograph. Thirty-nine years old. He looks directly at the camera. *He had been offered safety. He chose to return to Germany. He wrote his most important books in a cell. He was executed three weeks before the war ended.*

**The Holocaust sequence** (Ken Burns + Radiolab, 2 minutes):
Chagall's stained glass window at Reims Cathedral — made 1974, for the cathedral rebuilt after WWI. The colors are extraordinary. Slowly revealed. Under it: Górecki's Symphony No. 3, movement 1 — a mother's lament. No narration. *A Jewish painter was asked to create Christian stained glass for a French cathedral rebuilt after two world wars. This is what he made.*

**Closing image**: Rouault's *Head of Christ* (c. 1905, Houston Museum of Fine Arts). The most battered, most human Christ-face in 20th-century art. *Rouault painted clowns. He said the clown was the image of Christ: someone who entertains while suffering, who wears a mask over pain, who is not what they appear to be.*

---

## Era 9: The World Turns (1945–Now)
**Visual palette**: Color. Global. Multiple simultaneous worlds.

**Opening image**: The Vatican II opening session photograph — John XXIII and 2,500 bishops. *The most powerful religious institution in the world, trying to have a conversation with the world it had been afraid of for 200 years.*

**The Global South Sequence** (Radiolab, 90 seconds):
Sound first — a Nigerian Pentecostal service. Then: a Korean congregation of 50,000 singing. Then: a Brazilian favela church. Then: an Ethiopian Orthodox procession. Then: back to silence. The images: each church. Each face. Each world. *700 million people. None of them asking permission.*

**Sequence**:
- The Marc Chagall Jerusalem windows → the Rothko Chapel interior → Matisse's Chapel of the Rosary (the light on the tiles)
- The Civil Rights March photograph → the Ebenezer Baptist Church interior → MLK's Bible (still in the church, open to Isaiah)
- The Boston Globe Spotlight front page → the Church of the Holy Sepulchre → Pope Francis in Canada, 2022

**Thinker window**: Simone Weil — no formal portrait. A photograph, 1941. The thinnest, most intense face. *She never joined the church. She said she felt called to stay outside, on the threshold, where the people who don't belong stand. She died at 34, refusing to eat more than the ration of occupied France. She wrote some of the most profound theology of the 20th century.*

**Closing image**: The series ends on the Rublev Trinity Icon. Slow zoom toward the empty space at the table — the space facing the viewer. The invitation that has been there for 2,000 years.

*"There's still a place at the table. There has always been a place at the table. The question the whole story is asking, from the upper room to this room, is: will you sit down?"*

Silence. The icon. Fade.

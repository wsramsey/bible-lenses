# Bible Lenses — Visual Documentary Format

## The Core Metaphor
**A painting is a window. You look through it into the world that made it.**

The experience is this:
- A painting appears. Full screen. Slowly revealed — Ken Burns pan across the canvas.
- A voice describes what you're seeing. Simple. Direct. Not art criticism — *what is this moment?*
- The painting dissolves into the historical world it depicts.
- Snippets. Fragments. Other paintings. A manuscript page. A piece of stone. A face.
- Then: the thinker who shaped this moment. Or was shaped by it. Their words.
- Then: the connection to today. One question. Silence.
- Back to the painting. Zoom out. It hangs in its museum. Context restored.

## The Format Rules

### 1. The Painting Does the Work
The narration never explains what you can see. If Caravaggio shows darkness and a shaft of light, the narrator doesn't say "notice the darkness." The visual does that. The narrator asks *why*.

### 2. Snippets, Not Lectures
Maximum 45 seconds of continuous narration before something visual happens. This is not a lecture with pictures. It's a visual experience with occasional words.

### 3. Every Painting Connects to 3 Things
- **The historical moment** it depicts or was made in
- **A thinker** whose thought it embodies or challenges
- **A question** alive in the present moment

### 4. The Sound Design Is the Third Voice
The narrator is voice 1. The primary source quotes are voice 2. The sound design — ambient, music, silence — is voice 3. All three carry equal weight.

### 5. Scroll Architecture
The experience is designed like scrolling:
- **Entry point**: A single painting, full-screen, in silence
- **Slow reveal**: Ken Burns pan, ambient sound building
- **The moment**: The painting's specific historical instant
- **The corridor**: A sequence of related images — manuscripts, faces, places, other paintings
- **The connection**: The thinker's words
- **The return**: Back to the painting, now seen differently
- **The portal**: A highlight appears on one element of the painting — click/tap to go deeper

## The Three Visual Rhythms

### Slow (Ken Burns)
Extended pan over a single painting or artifact. Used for:
- Opening a new era
- A death
- A moment of singular beauty
- When the silence needs to breathe

*Duration: 30–90 seconds*

### Medium (Radiolab)
A sequence of images, each 3–8 seconds. Cut on the beat of the music or narration.
Used for:
- Showing how an idea spread
- Parallel lives
- Before/after contrasts
- The spread of a movement

*Duration: 30 seconds – 2 minutes*

### Fast (MTV-edit, but for history)
Quick cuts, almost subliminal. Images flash. Used *very sparingly* for:
- The chaos of a war
- The explosion of the Reformation (pamphlets multiplying)
- The pandemic spread
- Azusa Street

*Duration: 5–15 seconds maximum*

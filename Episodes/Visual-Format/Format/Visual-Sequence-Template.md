# Visual Sequence Template

## The Standard Segment (8–12 minutes)

### Beat 1: THE PAINTING (60–90 seconds)
*Slow pan. Ambient sound. No narration for first 15 seconds.*

The painting fills the screen. The Ken Burns effect — slow, deliberate pan revealing the composition gradually. The ambient sound of the era it was painted in begins low.

Then a single sentence from the narrator — not describing the painting, but placing it:

> *"Caravaggio painted this in 1601. He was 30 years old, already famous, already violent — he would kill a man five years later. He was painting in Rome, two blocks from where the Apostle Paul had stood trial 1,500 years earlier. And he was painting this: the moment Saul of Tarsus falls off his horse."*

### Beat 2: THE MOMENT (2–3 minutes)
*Medium sequence. Related images cut together.*

The narration takes you inside the historical moment. Not the painting's history — the event depicted.

While talking about Paul's conversion: other images of Damascus; the road; archaeological finds from 1st-century Syria; Paul's actual letter to the Galatians where he describes the event in one breathless sentence.

The images cut on the narration's rhythm. Fast enough to feel alive, slow enough to register.

### Beat 3: THE THINKER CARD (90 seconds)
*Single portrait. Still. Just the face.*

The thinker whose life or thought connects to this moment appears. A painted portrait or photograph, full screen.

The narrator does not introduce them. Their words appear as text, read aloud:

> *"I resolved to know nothing while I was with you except Jesus Christ — and him crucified."*

Then: their name, dates, and a single phrase describing their contribution:

**Paul of Tarsus, c. 5–67 AD — "The Apostle who gave Christianity its theology"**

### Beat 4: THE CONNECTION (45 seconds)
*The painting returns. A single element highlighted.*

The painting comes back. But now one element is subtly highlighted — a hand, a face, a shadow.

The narrator asks one question. Present tense. Direct address.

> *"Paul says his conversion happened in a flash of light — like being thrown from a horse, though he never mentions the horse. Caravaggio shows him flat on his back, arms reaching up toward a light we can't see. The horse is standing calmly over him. Nobody else in the painting seems to notice anything happened.*

> *When did something change in you? Did anyone else notice?"*

### Beat 5: THE PORTAL (10 seconds)
*Highlighted area pulses. An invitation.*

The highlighted element in the painting glows slightly. A label appears: *"→ Paul the Apostle"* or *"→ The Road to Damascus"* or *"→ Conversion Experiences."*

In the app: this is the node link. Click it. You go deeper.
In the video: it's the transition to the next segment.

## The Era Opening (3–4 minutes)

At the start of each era (1st century, medieval, Reformation, etc.), a special sequence:

### The Map Sequence
A historical map of the era, very slowly revealed. Major cities appearing as glowing points. Trade routes. Political boundaries. Then: the specific places that matter for the era.

### The Sound Texture
The ambient soundscape of the era establishes. For 30 seconds, no narration — just the world coming to life.

### The Single Sentence
One sentence that captures the era:

> *"This is 30 AD. A province on the eastern edge of the Roman Empire. Nobody in Rome knows what is about to happen."*

## The Thinker Sequence (4–6 minutes)

When a major thinker gets their own dedicated segment:

**Portrait → Key Work → Key Idea → Contemporary Echo**

1. **Portrait**: Their face, full screen, slow reveal. Music from their era.
2. **Key Work**: The title of their most important book or idea, and where they wrote it.
3. **Key Idea**: One paragraph. Their central claim, in their own words.
4. **The Contribution Line**: One sentence describing what they added to the stream.
5. **The Contemporary Echo**: Where their idea shows up in a conversation happening right now.

Example: **Thomas Aquinas**
- *Portrait*: Fra Angelico's fresco, Cell 7, San Marco
- *Key Work*: The Summa Theologiae — written in Naples and Paris, 1265–1274
- *Key Idea* (his own words): *"Grace does not destroy nature but perfects it."*
- *Contribution*: The synthesis that made faith and reason friends instead of enemies
- *Contemporary Echo*: Every time a scientist who is also a Christian says "I don't see a conflict" — they're standing on Aquinas's argument

## The Connection Web (1–2 minutes)

Periodically — perhaps once per era — the visual zooms out and shows the connections.

A visual web: faces connected by lines. Lines labeled with the relationship. The camera moves through the web like a drone through a city.

*"By the year 400 AD, look at how many conversations are happening simultaneously. Augustine in Carthage, Jerome in Bethlehem, Chrysostom in Constantinople — all writing letters to each other. All arguing. All building the same cathedral from different angles."*

The web pulses. Lines glow as connections are named.

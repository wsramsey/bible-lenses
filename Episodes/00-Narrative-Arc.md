# The Narrative Arc of Christianity
*From a fishing village in Roman Palestine to 2.5 billion people on every continent*

## The Shape of the Story

### Act One: The Impossible Beginning (1st–4th c.)
A movement begins with 12 people in a borrowed room. Within 300 years — without armies, without political power, without money — it has spread from Ireland to India. It grows fastest when it is persecuted. Its central claim: a man was executed and came back. Its central practice: a meal. Its central ethic: care for people no one else will care for.

### Act Two: The Dangerous Gift of Power (4th–10th c.)
Constantine makes it legal. Theodosius makes it mandatory. The movement that survived by dying is now the official religion of the most powerful empire on earth. What does power do to a faith built on a cross? The answers take 1,700 years to arrive and are still arriving.

### Act Three: The Building of a World (10th–14th c.)
Medieval Christendom: cathedrals, universities, hospitals, the first human rights arguments, the Crusades, the Inquisition, the Black Death. Christianity builds Western civilization and simultaneously builds its worst atrocities. Both are true. The question is whether they are separable.

### Act Four: The Great Fracture (15th–17th c.)
Printing press. Luther. The Reformation. The counter-Reformation. 150 years of religious wars. The Thirty Years' War kills 8 million. The Peace of Westphalia creates the secular state as the solution to Christian violence. Christianity produces its own undoing — and then keeps going.

### Act Five: The Global Spread (17th–19th c.)
Missionaries follow colonizers. Christianity becomes the first global religion. The Enlightenment attacks it from within the West. Revivals respond. Abolition — Christianity's greatest moral achievement — is led by Christians. Slavery — Christianity's greatest moral failure — is defended by Christians. The same book. The same God. Opposite conclusions.

### Act Six: The 20th Century Crucible (1900–2000)
Two world wars. The Holocaust — the endpoint of 1,900 years of Christian antisemitism. The death of liberal optimism. Barth's bomb in the playground. Bonhoeffer's hanging. Azusa Street and 700 million Pentecostals. The Global South explosion. The sex abuse crisis. Vatican II. The Civil Rights Movement. The most violent and most generative century in Christianity's history.

### Act Seven: Now (2000–present)
The West is losing it. The rest of the world is gaining it. The center has moved from Geneva and Rome to Lagos and Seoul. The average Christian in 2023 is a 30-year-old African woman who has never heard of Karl Barth. The faith that started among 12 people in a borrowed room is simultaneously dying and exploding. The story is not over.

## The Central Tension
Every century returns to the same question: **What does it cost to follow this?**

When the cost is death: the faith grows.
When the cost is nothing: it hollows out.
When it holds political power: it tends toward violence.
When it loses power: it tends toward authenticity.

This pattern does not prove the faith is true. But it is the pattern.

## The Primary Sources
Every episode in this series is backed by primary documents:
- The letters of Paul (written 48–65 AD — within 15 years of the resurrection)
- The Didache (c. 50–120 AD — the oldest Christian manual of practice)
- Pliny's letter to Trajan (c. 112 AD — a pagan governor's account of Christian worship)
- Perpetua's prison diary (203 AD — a woman's account of martyrdom)
- Augustine's Confessions (c. 397 AD)
- The Rule of Benedict (c. 529 AD)
- Bernard of Clairvaux's letters (1090–1153)
- The Magna Carta (1215) — shaped by Innocent III's England
- Julian of Norwich's Revelations (1373)
- Luther's letters and table talk
- The Trial of Jan Hus (1415)
- The Requerimiento (1513)
- Letters of Perpetua, Cortés, Las Casas, Wilberforce, Douglass, Lincoln, Bonhoeffer, King

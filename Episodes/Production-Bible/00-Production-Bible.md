# Bible Lenses Podcast — Production Bible
*Dan Carlin meets Ken Burns with Radiolab sound design*

## The Concept
A long-form audio documentary series tracing the full history of Christianity — not as a religious broadcast but as the most consequential story in Western civilization, told with the rigor of a historian, the intimacy of a storyteller, and the honesty to say "this is where it went wrong."

**63 episodes across 8 seasons.** Each episode: 60–90 minutes. No ads. No filler. Every minute earns its place.

## The Audience
People who:
- Loved *Hardcore History* but want the religious dimension taken seriously
- Grew up in the church and left, wondering what they actually believed
- Never grew up in the church and are genuinely curious what this thing is
- Are currently in the church and want to understand it more deeply than Sunday school allows

**Not** a Christian podcast. A podcast *about* Christianity that Christians and non-Christians can both listen to.

## The Voice and Tone

### The Three Pillars

**Dan Carlin** — stakes and humanity
- Make the listener feel the physical reality of the past
- Never pretend we know more than we do
- Direct address: "Here's why this matters right now"
- The uncomfortable question at the end of every episode

**Ken Burns** — primary sources and silence
- Let the documents speak
- Read the actual words of the people who were there
- Trust the listener to sit with something difficult
- Don't over-explain what the silence already says

**Radiolab** — sound as meaning
- The sound design IS the argument
- Unexpected audio bridges that reframe everything
- Science and mystery held together without resolving the tension
- Moments where the medium itself becomes the message

## The Structure of Every Episode

### The Template (flexible, not rigid)

```
00:00 — COLD OPEN
       A primary source read cold, no context
       60–90 seconds
       The listener doesn't know yet what they're hearing

02:00 — THE STAKES
       Narrator sets the historical moment
       "Here's what's about to happen — and here's why it matters"
       The Carlin move: make the stakes visceral

12:00 — THE VOICES
       Primary sources woven into narration
       2–3 key figures from the era
       Their actual words, slightly distinguished in delivery

35:00 — THE TURN
       The thing the listener didn't know
       The fact that reframes everything before it
       Sometimes a single sentence

50:00 — THE LESSON
       Direct address to the listener
       Not an answer — a question
       The Dan Carlin ending: "So here's what I keep coming back to..."

60:00 — THE MUSIC
       One piece from the era
       Heard differently now than it would have been at the start
       Fade out on it
```

## The Narrator's Rules

1. **Never pretend certainty you don't have.** "We think," "the sources suggest," "historians argue" — always.

2. **Make the dead feel alive.** Not with dramatic re-enactment. With specific physical detail. Not "he walked to his death" but "it was a Tuesday morning, early April, still cold, and he walked to the gallows in his prison clothes, and he knelt down to pray."

3. **Read the primary sources slowly.** Slower than feels comfortable. Let the words land.

4. **Say the hard things.** When Christianity committed atrocities, say it plainly. No defensive hedging. "This was wrong. Here is how it was justified. Here is how it continued for 300 years. Here is how it ended."

5. **End with a question, not an answer.** The listener should feel slightly unsettled. Not convinced of anything — prompted to think.

6. **Trust silence.** After a primary source passage. After a significant revelation. Count to three before continuing. The silence is not dead air — it's processing time.

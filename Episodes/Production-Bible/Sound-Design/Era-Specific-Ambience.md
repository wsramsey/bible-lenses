# Era-Specific Sound Sources

## Recordable / Available Sounds for Each Era

### 1st Century Jerusalem
**Field recording locations**:
- The Western Wall plaza on a weekday morning — stone, crowd murmur, liturgical prayer
- The Cardo Maximus area of the Old City — approximate ancient street scale
- The Garden of Gethsemane — olive trees, birds, specific quietness

**Archaeological acoustics**:
- The Cardo Maximus in Jerusalem has been reconstructed — stone colonnaded street
- The Pool of Bethesda excavation gives a sense of underground stone acoustic

**Reconstruction**:
- Aramaic spoken aloud (multiple linguists have reconstructed 1st-century Galilean Aramaic)
- Period instruments: kinnor (lyre), halil (flute), tof (frame drum) — recordings exist

### Byzantine Constantinople
**The Hagia Sophia acoustic** (UNESCO documented):
- The dome creates an 11-second reverb tail — one of the longest in any building
- The acoustic was deliberately designed to make the liturgy sound supernatural
- Recordings made inside the Hagia Sophia before 2020 (now a mosque — acoustic somewhat changed by carpets)
- The Cappella Romana's recording *Lost Voices of Hagia Sophia* (2010) reconstructed the specific acoustic using impulse response technology — available

### Medieval Monastery
**Field recording locations**:
- Fontgombault Abbey, France — Benedictine; Gregorian chant sung in a 12th-century church; recordings commercially available
- Abbey of Solesmes — the center of Gregorian chant restoration; recording archive
- Any surviving medieval stone church for the ambient acoustic

**The canonical hours**:
- The Liturgy of the Hours in Latin — multiple recordings of authentic Gregorian chant
- The sound of a monastery at 3am — bell, then silence, then distant chanting beginning

### The Reformation
**The printing press sound**:
- Working Gutenberg-style presses still operate at the Gutenberg Museum, Mainz, Germany
- Several museums have working reproduction presses — recordable
- The specific sound of type being set, inked, pressed is distinct and powerful

**German town square**:
- Market sounds from a modern German medieval town (Rothenburg ob der Tauber, Nördlingen) approximate the soundscape

### The Atlantic World
**The ocean**:
- North Atlantic recordings — not tropical; the specific sound of cold, gray water
- Port sounds: ropes, wood, water — available from maritime museums

**African musical traditions**:
- Field recordings of West African drumming (Ghana, Benin) — the traditions that came on the slave ships
- Comparison with early African American spiritual recordings shows direct continuation

### 20th Century
**Actual recordings available**:
- WWI: actual recordings of artillery, soldiers' voices (preserved in British Library Sound Archive)
- Azusa Street: no recording exists of the revival itself (1906), but contemporaneous African American gospel recordings from 1908–1915 exist (Victor, Columbia records)
- MLK: every major speech recorded; "I Have a Dream" (August 28, 1963) — 8-track recording exists
- Vatican II: Italian radio recordings of sessions
- Bonhoeffer's prison: no recording, but Tegel Prison Berlin still exists and is recordable

### Global South
**Available today**:
- Yoido Full Gospel Church, Seoul: services recorded and broadcast
- RCCG, Nigeria: services recorded and broadcast
- Brazilian Pentecostal: multiple available recordings
- Ethiopian Orthodox liturgy: Ge'ez chant recordings from the 1970s onward (UNESCO archive)
- Coptic liturgy: multiple recordings from Cairo churches

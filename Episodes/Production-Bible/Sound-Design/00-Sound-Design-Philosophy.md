# Sound Design Philosophy

## The Core Principle
**Every sound is chosen for theological reason, not atmospheric decoration.**

The sound design is not wallpaper. It is argument. When the listener hears the sound of a printing press under Luther's Diet of Worms speech, they are hearing the *reason* the speech had consequences. When they hear the ocean under Frederick Douglass's words, they are being reminded what that ocean carried.

If a sound doesn't add meaning, it shouldn't be there.

## The Three Layers

### Layer 1: The World (always present)
The ambient sonic world of the episode. Present throughout, very low. Establishes place and time without calling attention to itself.

### Layer 2: The Moment (significant scenes)
Specific sounds that mark key turning points. These come up in the mix at crucial moments then recede.

### Layer 3: The Bridge (episode transitions)
The Radiolab move — an unexpected audio connection between past and present that reframes what the listener just heard.

## Era Sound Palettes

### 1st Century Mediterranean
**Baseline**: Stone on stone. Distant Aramaic murmur. Occasional animal sounds (sheep, donkeys — this is the Temple Mount). Wind.
**Key sounds**: The slap of sandals on the Cardo Maximus. Water poured from a clay vessel (baptism scenes). The hiss of a scroll being unrolled.
**Sources**: Field recordings from Jerusalem's Old City; recordings of Bedouin markets; modern recordings of the Temple Mount soundscape.

### Roman Persecution Era (64–313 AD)
**Baseline**: The sounds of an urban Roman city — crowds, Latin, the occasional trumpet of the military.
**Key sounds**: The roar of an arena crowd (distant, ominous). The sound of a stylus on wax (letters being written). The crackle of fire.
**The pivot sound**: A single voice beginning to sing in the darkness — the *Oxyrhynchus Hymn* (the oldest surviving Christian hymn with musical notation, c. 260 AD). It sounds ancient and strange and alive.

### Byzantine/Medieval (330–1000 AD)
**Baseline**: Church bells. Distant liturgical chanting. Stone reverb — everything in these episodes should feel like it's happening inside a cathedral.
**Key sounds**: The scratch of quill on vellum (monastery scriptorium). Rain on stone. The bell that marks the canonical hours.
**The pivot sound**: The Hagia Sophia in full Byzantine liturgy — this specific acoustic is documented and recreatable; the dome's 11-second reverb tail is one of the most extraordinary sounds ever made.

### The Reformation (1517–1648)
**Baseline**: The printing press. This is the era's defining technology and its defining sound. The rhythmic mechanical clatter should be just barely present throughout every Reformation episode.
**Key sounds**: The crinkle of a pamphlet being unfolded. A crowd in a German town square. Luther's voice in the Wartburg — isolated, echo, the stone room where he translated the Bible in 11 weeks.
**The pivot sound**: Ein feste Burg on a single lute — then, 45 minutes later in the episode, Bach's chorale setting of the same tune. The distance between those two versions *is* the Reformation's century of development.

### Atlantic/Colonial Era (1492–1800)
**Baseline**: The ocean. Always. Every episode in this era lives under the sound of water — it carried enslaved people, it carried missionaries, it carried the faith and its compromises simultaneously.
**Key sounds**: Jungle birds (South American missions). The sound of a quipu (Inca recording device — the technology the missionaries didn't understand). Drums that survived the Middle Passage encoded in the spirituals.
**The pivot sound**: "Go Down Moses" — a cappella, single voice, no production. Played after the description of the Requerimiento. The contrast is devastating and requires no explanation.

### 19th Century
**Baseline**: Industrial sounds beginning to enter — trains, the telegraph, the mechanized world. Underneath: hymns from tent revivals.
**Key sounds**: A telegraph clicking out Morse code (the information revolution before the internet). The sound of a printing press — faster now, more urgent. Steam.
**The pivot sound**: Mahalia Jackson's "Take My Hand, Precious Lord" (1956 recording) played after the description of the abolitionist movement's theological arguments. The connection between those arguments and that voice is the argument.

### 20th Century
**Baseline**: Radio static. The 20th century is the first century with recorded sound — use it. Real recordings. Real voices. Real ambience.
**Key sounds**: Artillery (WWI trenches). A phonograph playing badly (the incongruity of beauty in hell). The sound of a typewriter (Bonhoeffer's letters written in prison). A crowd of 250,000 at the March on Washington.
**The pivot sound**: Bonhoeffer's episode ends with the actual recording of "A Mighty Fortress Is Our God" being sung by German prisoners of war in a British camp, 1945. Not performed for effect — recorded in a real place, by real people, who sang it because they needed to.

### Global South / Contemporary
**Baseline**: Urban sounds from Lagos, Seoul, São Paulo — the megacities that are the centers of contemporary Christianity. Traffic. Heat. Crowd density. The world Christianity actually lives in.
**Key sounds**: A Korean congregation of 50,000 singing in unison. A Nigerian Pentecostal service — the specific chaos of simultaneous worship, prayer, tongues, healing prayer. The call to prayer from a mosque in Cairo — and then, 30 seconds later, the bells of a Coptic church.
**The pivot sound**: The series finale ends on the sound of a single woman praying in Yoruba. No translation provided. You don't need to understand the words. You understand the sound.

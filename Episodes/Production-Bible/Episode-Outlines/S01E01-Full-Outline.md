# S01E01 — "The Upper Room"
**Runtime: 75 minutes**

---

## 00:00 — COLD OPEN (2 min)
*No music. No ambient. Just the voice.*

Read 1 Corinthians 15:3-8 slowly. Then:

*"Paul of Tarsus. Writing approximately 54 AD. Within 20 years of the events he's describing. He's writing to a community in Corinth — a city he founded two years earlier — and they're having arguments about whether the resurrection is real. And he writes this. What strikes me every time I read it is that phrase: 'most of whom are still alive.' He's not appealing to tradition. He's not appealing to scripture. He's saying: if you don't believe me, go ask them. They're still here."*

[SOUND: Stone. Distant crowd. Aramaic. Jerusalem ambience begins, very low.]

---

## 02:00 — THE STAKES (10 min)
*Register 1: The Wide Shot*

Set the scene: Roman Palestine, 30 AD. A province on the eastern edge of an empire that controlled everything from Scotland to Mesopotamia. A religion — Judaism — that had survived Babylonian exile, Hellenization, and Roman occupation. A man from Nazareth, a city no one had heard of, who has been executed as a criminal.

*"In the Roman world, crucifixion was reserved for two categories of people: slaves and political rebels. It was designed to be maximally humiliating. You died slowly. In public. Naked. The message was clear: this is what happens to people who challenge the order of things. Rome crucified thousands of people. Most of their names we don't know."*

*"And then something happened that we can't fully explain. The followers of this particular crucified man didn't scatter. They didn't go back to their fishing boats. They started telling people that he had come back."*

---

## 12:00 — THE 40 DAYS (15 min)
*Register 2: The Close-Up*

Walk through the resurrection appearances. Not arguing for their historicity — presenting them as they were experienced by the people who reported them.

The appearance to Mary Magdalene (John 20) — the "criterion of embarrassment": no one in 1st-century Judaism would fabricate a resurrection account with a woman as the first witness.

The appearance to Thomas (John 20) — the physical evidence demanded; the finger in the wounds. What Thomas represents: the person who needs to touch it before they'll believe. Why this story was preserved.

Peter's restoration (John 21) — the three questions, one for each denial. The same firepit. The same question. "Do you love me?" The psychological precision of it.

*"I'm not going to argue with you about whether the resurrection happened. That's a different podcast. What I want to do is show you what it looked like from the inside — what these particular people experienced and reported in the days and weeks after the crucifixion. Because whatever you think about the metaphysics, the historical record is this: a group of people who had every reason to scatter and hide instead became convinced something had happened and began telling everyone they could find."*

---

## 27:00 — PENTECOST (10 min)
*Sound design: The Oxyrhynchus Hymn enters under narration*

Acts 2 described. The linguistic diversity of the crowd — pilgrims from across the diaspora. The speaking in tongues as the reverse of Babel — not confusion but understanding.

Peter's speech: the first Christian sermon, preserved in Acts 2:14-41. Quote extensively. Note what he says: not primarily a theological argument but a historical claim. He points across the city to the tomb. "This Jesus whom you crucified." He's not speaking in abstractions.

*"3,000 baptized in one day. That's the number Acts gives us. I have no way to verify it. But here's what we can verify: within 20 years, there were Christian communities in every major city of the Mediterranean world. That growth rate — without armies, without money, without political power — requires explanation."*

---

## 37:00 — THE GROWTH (12 min)
*Register 1: Back to the Wide Shot*

Rodney Stark's demographic modeling. The 40% per decade growth rate. Show the math: 1,000 members in 40 AD → 31 million by 300 AD. Compare to Mormon growth rate. The spread is not miraculous in the sociological sense — it follows the patterns of effective social movements.

The mechanisms: trade networks, social care, epidemic response. The plague scenes. Christians staying while pagans fled. The death rate differential.

Quote Dionysius of Alexandria on the plague of 250 AD:
*"Most of our brother Christians showed unbounded love and loyalty, never sparing themselves and thinking only of one another. Heedless of danger, they took charge of the sick, attending to their every need... Drawing on themselves the sickness of their neighbors and cheerfully accepting their pains, many, in nursing and caring for others, transferred their death to themselves and died in their stead."*

*"That is not theology. That is a description of behavior. And behavior is what spreads a movement."*

---

## 49:00 — THE VOICES (12 min)
*Register 2: Primary sources*

Read Paul's letter to the Philippians 4:11-13 as if it's the last letter of a man who doesn't know what's going to happen:
> "I have learned, in whatsoever state I am, therewith to be content. I know both how to be abased, and I know how to abound... I can do all things through Christ which strengtheneth me."

*"He wrote this from prison. Awaiting trial. He didn't know if he'd live or die. He says: I've learned to be content. Not that he was content. Not that contentment came naturally. That he'd learned it. There's a difference."*

Brief excerpt from Ignatius's letter to the Romans, written on his way to martyrdom:
> "I am writing to all the Churches and I enjoin all, that I am dying willingly for God's sake... I am God's wheat and shall be ground by their teeth so that I may become God's pure bread."

*"He was asking the Roman church not to intervene to save him. He wanted to die. Not from despair. From something else — something that looked to his contemporaries either like madness or like the most perfect courage they had ever seen."*

---

## 61:00 — THE LESSON (8 min)
*Register 3: Direct Address*

*"Here's what I keep coming back to. The movement that became Christianity started among people who had no power. No money. No political influence. No military. They were, by Roman standards, nobodies — fishermen, tax collectors, women, slaves. And within 300 years, they had changed the world more thoroughly than any empire.*

*The normal explanation is: the message was compelling. And it was. But I think there's something else. I think what spread it was not primarily argument but demonstration. The argument for Christianity in the 1st and 2nd centuries was not primarily theological. It was behavioral. People watched how Christians lived — how they cared for their sick, how they treated their slaves, how they welcomed strangers, how they faced death — and they concluded: these people have something real.*

*Which raises an uncomfortable question for 2025. If you stripped away the buildings and the institutions and the cultural inheritance and asked: what does the actual behavior of Christians in your city look like? What would the Roman observers conclude? Would they see something that required explanation? Or would they see something indistinguishable from any other reasonably decent set of people?*

*That's not a rhetorical question. I'm genuinely asking."*

---

## 69:00 — THE CLOSE (6 min)
*Sound: the Oxyrhynchus Hymn, slightly louder now*

The episode ends on Acts 28:30-31 — the last two verses of the book of Acts. Paul in Rome, in rented accommodation, "preaching the kingdom of God and teaching about the Lord Jesus Christ with all boldness and without hindrance."

*"Luke ends his story there. Mid-sentence, essentially. Paul's alive. He's teaching. The authorities haven't stopped him yet. The story is still going.*

*That's not an accident. Acts ends there because the story doesn't end. The next episode of Acts is your life.*

*Or it's this podcast."*

[MUSIC: The Oxyrhynchus Hymn in full — 90 seconds — then fade]

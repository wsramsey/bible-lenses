# Music Cues — Master Index

## Philosophy
Every piece of music used should be:
1. **From the era depicted OR directly about it** — no anachronistic scoring for emotional manipulation
2. **Heard as the people of that era heard it** — not as classical concert music but as living practice
3. **Theologically meaningful** — the music makes an argument the words can't

## By Era

### 1st Century
- **Psalm 22 in ancient Hebrew cantillation** — this is the psalm Jesus quotes from the cross; hearing it in its original form recontextualizes everything
- **The Oxyrhynchus Hymn** (c. 260 AD) — oldest surviving Christian hymn with notation; eerie, modal, nothing like modern Christian music

### Byzantine / Early Medieval
- **Cappella Romana — *Lost Voices of Hagia Sophia*** (2010) — reconstructed using the actual acoustic of Hagia Sophia; available commercially
- **Gregorian chant — Solesmes Abbey recordings** — the standard; available
- **The Kontakion of Romanos the Melodist** (6th c.) — the greatest Byzantine hymnographer; settings available

### Medieval
- **Hildegard of Bingen — *O Virtus Sapientiae*** — her own composition; multiple recordings; strange and beautiful
- **Machaut — *Messe de Nostre Dame*** (1364) — the oldest complete polyphonic Mass setting; written during the Black Death
- **Anonymous — *L'homme armé*** (c. 1460) — the most famous medieval tune; used as the basis for dozens of Renaissance Mass settings; connects the secular and sacred

### Reformation
- **Luther — *Ein feste Burg*** on single lute, unharmonized — as Luther's congregation would have sung it
- **Bach — *Ein feste Burg* Cantata BWV 80** — the same tune 150 years later
- **Tallis — *Spem in alium*** (c. 1570) — 40-voice motet; Catholic music written for Elizabeth I's Protestant England; the *via media* in sound
- **The Genevan Psalter** (1562) — Calvinist; stark; congregational; the opposite of Tallis

### Colonial / Atlantic
- **"Go Down Moses"** — a cappella, recorded c. 1940s (Library of Congress Lomax recordings)
- **"Were You There"** — single voice, Marian Anderson version (1941)
- **Bach — *St. Matthew Passion, Erbarme dich*** — Peter's denial and weeping; for the episode on betrayal within the church

### 19th Century
- **"Amazing Grace"** — in its original form; then in its prison camp form; then Judy Collins's version; the journey of a single hymn through American history
- **Brahms — *Ein deutsches Requiem*** (1868) — written after his mother's death; secular text from Psalms and Isaiah; the 19th century wrestling with death and comfort
- **Antonín Dvořák — *Stabat Mater*** (1877) — written after the death of his children; Catholic text; devastating

### 20th Century
- **Mahalia Jackson — "Take My Hand, Precious Lord"** (recorded 1956) — for the Civil Rights episodes
- **Arvo Pärt — *Spiegel im Spiegel*** (1978) — Estonian composer working under Soviet occupation; the sound of faith under oppression
- **Górecki — *Symphony No. 3 (Symphony of Sorrowful Songs)*** (1976) — Holocaust; a mother's lament; for the WWII episodes
- **John Coltrane — "A Love Supreme"** (1964) — for the Civil Rights era; Coltrane as a deeply spiritual musician; the sacred and secular meeting
- **Johnny Cash — "When the Man Comes Around"** (2002) — Revelation imagery in American country music; the eschatological tradition continuing

### Global South / Contemporary
- **Taizé — "Ubi Caritas"** — the ecumenical community; simple, multi-lingual
- **Soweto Gospel Choir** — South African township gospel
- **Hillsong — "Oceans"** (2013) — the most-played Christian song of the 2010s; whatever you think of it, it is the sound of global evangelical Christianity right now
- **Ethiopian Orthodox Liturgy — Zema chant** — the oldest surviving Christian musical tradition; UNESCO archive recordings
- **Korean Congregational singing** — Yoido Full Gospel Church recording; the sheer scale is the argument

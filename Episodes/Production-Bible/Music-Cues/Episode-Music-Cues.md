# Episode Music Cues — Detailed

## Season 1

### E01 — The Upper Room
**Opening**: Psalm 22 in ancient Hebrew cantillation — the psalm Jesus quotes from the cross, heard first in its original context
**Mid-episode**: The Oxyrhynchus Hymn (c. 260 AD) — used when describing the spread of Christian worship; the listener hears what the earliest Christian music sounded like
**Close**: A single voice beginning *Hallelujah* in Aramaic — then cut to silence

### E03 — Fire and the Word
**Under Perpetua's diary**: Nothing. Silence. Her words are the music.
**After the arena description**: A distant, single voice singing — not a hymn anyone knows — just a lone human voice in darkness. Then fade.
**Close**: The Oxyrhynchus Hymn again — but now heard differently; now it's the sound of people who died for it

### E06 — Perpetua's Diary
**The entire episode is nearly musicless** — this is intentional. The only music is the brief refrain of a Carthaginian Jewish chant (North African tradition) played as her father visits for the last time. Music for the world she was leaving. Silence for the world she was entering.

## Season 2

### E07 — In This Sign (Constantine)
**Opening**: Roman military music — the tubicen (bronze horn); the sound of Constantine's army before the Milvian Bridge
**The vision moment**: Sudden silence. Then, very quietly, a Byzantine troparion beginning. The faith that will replace Rome, heard in Rome's moment of triumph.
**Close**: The full Divine Liturgy beginning — the sound of the church Constantine created, heard 1,700 years later. It sounds exactly as it did in 325 AD.

### E09 — Augustine's Garden
**The restless years**: Carthaginian street sounds. Under them, barely audible, Augustine reading aloud — we hear his voice but not the words.
**The conversion moment**: Total silence for 10 seconds. Then: Bach's *Erbarme dich* from the St. Matthew Passion — Peter's weeping after the denial. Not exact — Augustine is not Peter — but the sound of a man whose heart breaks open.
**Close**: Ambrose's *Te Deum* — the hymn of thanksgiving — as Augustine would have heard it in Milan

### E10 — The Desert
**Baseline**: Desert silence. Not empty — the silence of deep desert, which has its own sound: wind, sand, the specific quietness of absence.
**The sayings**: Read against total silence. No ambient. Each saying given space.
**Close**: Evagrius Ponticus set to a modern Orthodox setting — the theology of the desert fathers, still sung

## Season 4

### E25 — Here I Stand (Luther)
**Opening**: A single voice singing *Ein feste Burg* — unaccompanied, in German, as a congregation would have sung it
**The printing press**: The press sound builds under the narration about the spread of the Theses — at first barely audible, then louder as the narration describes the copies multiplying
**Diet of Worms**: The press sound cuts abruptly as Luther begins to speak. Silence except for crowd ambience.
**Close**: Bach's *Ein feste Burg* Cantata BWV 80 — the same tune, 150 years later, harmonically developed, orchestrally complex. The distance between the opening single voice and this close is the story of what Luther started.

### E26 — The Anabaptist Experiment
**Felix Manz's drowning**: The sound of the Limmat River, Zürich. Under it, very faintly, a Hutterite hymn (the Ausbund, 1564 — the oldest Protestant hymnal still in use today, sung by the Amish). The tradition that was drowned is still singing.

## Season 5

### E37 — The Requerimiento
**Opening**: Ocean. Just ocean. 90 seconds. Nothing else.
**The Requerimiento read aloud**: The full text, in translation, read without music. The absurdity is its own argument.
**Las Casas response**: "Go Down Moses" — a cappella, single voice — enters immediately after Las Casas's most damning quote. No transition. The juxtaposition is the argument.
**Close**: A Guaraní-Jesuit Baroque piece from the Reductions (recordings exist from the reconstructed musical tradition) — the sound of what was possible before it was destroyed

## Season 7

### E52 — The Bomb in the Playground
**Opening**: Radio static. Then a voice — Woodrow Wilson's actual 1918 recording. The world before Barth's bomb.
**WWI trenches**: A phonograph recording playing *Ein feste Burg* — badly, tinny, in a trench — actual recordings from this era exist
**Barth's Romans**: The music stops entirely. Narration only. The silence is the theological point — Barth himself stopped the comfortable music.
**The Holocaust sequence**: Górecki's *Symphony of Sorrowful Songs*, movement 1 — a mother's lament, written 30 years after the Holocaust, for it
**Bonhoeffer's death**: The actual recording of German POWs singing *Ein feste Burg* in a British camp, 1945. The same tune from the opening. Heard completely differently now.
**Close**: Arvo Pärt's *Spiegel im Spiegel* — Estonian composer under Soviet occupation; the sound of faith surviving in darkness

## Season 8

### E60 — Azusa to Africa
**Opening**: Dead silence — and then, like an explosion, the sound of the Azusa Street revival reconstructed from contemporaneous gospel recordings. Not clean. Not produced. Ecstatic.
**Global South sequence**: A sound collage — Korean congregation, Nigerian choir, Brazilian worship, Coptic liturgy, Ethiopian chant. Each bleeding into the next. No narration. Just the sound of 700 million people.
**Close**: A single woman's voice in Yoruba. Praying. No translation. No music under it. The last sound in the series.

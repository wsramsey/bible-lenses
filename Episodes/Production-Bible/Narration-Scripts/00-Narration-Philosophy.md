# Narration Philosophy

## The Voice

**Not a celebrity.** Not a news anchor voice. Not the "documentary voice" — the slightly hushed, reverential tone that signals Important Serious Content.

What we want: a voice that sounds like a person talking to another person. Intelligent. Unhurried. Capable of humor and of horror. The voice of someone who has read everything and is trying to tell you what it means.

Reference points:
- David McCullough's warmth without the sentimentality
- Dan Carlin's willingness to say "I don't know" and "this disturbs me"
- Ira Glass's conversational intimacy without the studied casualness
- Robert Krulwich's (Radiolab) joy of discovery

## The Three Registers

### Register 1: The Wide Shot
Setting the historical scene. The narrator is standing at a distance, showing you the full picture. Slightly more formal. Moving quickly. Building context.

> *"By the year 200 AD, Christianity had spread to every major city in the Roman Empire — from Antioch to Alexandria to Rome. It had done this without a single army, without a single emperor's endorsement, without a single cent of government money. It had spread the way ideas spread when they fill a need that nothing else is filling."*

### Register 2: The Close-Up
Zooming into a specific person or moment. The narrator's voice drops slightly. Slows. Gets personal.

> *"Perpetua was twenty-two years old. She was nursing a baby. She wrote this from a prison cell in Carthage, in the year 203 AD, knowing she was going to die. And I want you to hear what she wrote, because she wrote it herself, and it survived, and it is one of the most astonishing documents in the history of Christianity:"*

Then silence. Then the document.

### Register 3: The Direct Address
The Carlin move. The narrator steps out of the history and talks to the listener. First person. Present tense. A question that doesn't have a safe answer.

> *"Here's what I keep coming back to. We look back at the Roman persecutions and we feel the horror of it — the arena, the lions, Perpetua in the sand. And we think: how could they do that? But let me ask you something. What do you believe so deeply that you would refuse to simply say it isn't true, even if saying it isn't true would save your life? Most of us have never had to answer that question. The early Christians had to answer it every time they walked past a Roman altar. What would you have done?"*

## Transitions

### The Temporal Bridge
Moving between eras without jarring:

> *"That letter — the letter Pliny wrote in 112 AD — was sitting in an archive for 1,400 years before anyone paid close attention to it. When scholars finally read it carefully in the 16th century, they were astonished. Here was a Roman governor, describing a Christian worship service, from the inside. He had no idea what he was documenting. He was trying to figure out what to charge them with."*

### The Sound Bridge
The narrator sets up a sound transition:

> *"And here's the thing I want you to hear. I'm going to play you something. This was recorded in the Hagia Sophia — the church Justinian built in 537 AD — just before it was converted back into a mosque. This is what it sounded like when the Byzantine liturgy was sung inside that dome. Listen."*

[MUSIC: Cappella Romana — Hagia Sophia reconstruction]

*"That 11-second reverb. That's not an accident. The architects designed it. They wanted the sound of God's voice to linger."*

### The Contrast Cut
Two things placed next to each other without explanation:

> *"Here is what Juan de Sepúlveda said at the Valladolid Debate in 1550, arguing that the indigenous peoples of the Americas could be enslaved:"*

[QUOTE from Sepúlveda — 30 seconds]

> *"And here is what Bartolomé de las Casas said in response:"*

[QUOTE from Las Casas — 30 seconds]

*"The same church. The same Bible. The same God. Listen to what it produces when it's used to justify power, and what it produces when it's used to protect the powerless."*

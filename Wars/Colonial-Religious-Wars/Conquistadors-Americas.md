# The Conquistadors and the Americas (1492–1600)
*The largest demographic catastrophe in human history — justified in Christian terms*

## The Religious Justification
The **Requerimiento** (1513): A legal document read aloud (in Spanish) to indigenous peoples before attacking them. It explained that the Pope had given their lands to Spain; they could submit peacefully or be conquered and enslaved — their "fault," not the Spanish.

This is one of the most grotesque documents in history — a legal fiction that gave Spanish soldiers a clean conscience for atrocities.

## The Scale
The indigenous population of the Americas fell from an estimated 50–100 million in 1492 to approximately 5–10 million by 1600. The primary cause was disease (smallpox, measles, influenza — epidemics to which indigenous peoples had no immunity). But deliberate violence, enslavement, and forced labor killed millions more.

## The Conquest of the Aztec Empire (1519–1521)
Hernán Cortés with 500 soldiers, indigenous allies, and disease conquered the Aztec Empire (population ~5–25 million).

**The Fall of Tenochtitlán (1521)**: The Aztec capital destroyed. The temple complex demolished; a cathedral built on its ruins. The deliberate symbolic replacement of indigenous religion with Christianity.

## The Conquest of the Inca Empire (1532–1572)
Francisco Pizarro captured the Inca emperor Atahualpa; ransomed him for a room full of gold and silver; then executed him anyway (1533). The Inca Empire collapsed.

## The Theological Defenders of Indigenous Rights
**Bartolomé de las Casas (1484–1566)**: Dominican priest; former slave-owner; became the most passionate defender of indigenous peoples.

His *A Short Account of the Destruction of the Indies* (1542) — a devastating indictment of Spanish colonial violence:

> "The reason the Christians have murdered on such a vast scale and killed anyone and everyone in their way is purely and simply greed."

Las Casas appealed to Emperor Charles V; helped produce the **New Laws (1542)** limiting indigenous enslavement. The laws were largely ignored in the colonies.

**The Valladolid Debate (1550–1551)**: The only formal debate in history over whether it was legal to conquer an indigenous people. Las Casas vs. Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda. Las Casas argued for indigenous humanity and rights. No clear winner; the debate continued.

## The Mission System
The **California missions** (Franciscan, 1769–1833): 21 missions along California's coast. Indigenous peoples were forcibly settled, Christianized, and put to work. Thousands died from disease and mistreatment.

Father Junípero Serra (founder) — **canonized by Pope Francis (2015)** — was one of the most controversial canonizations in history. Indigenous advocates protested vigorously.

## The Legacy
The conquest of the Americas is the central reckoning for any honest account of Christian mission:
- The Gospel arrived with the sword
- The Christianization was genuine — Latin America is the most Catholic region on earth
- The cost in human life was incalculable
- The political structures of colonial Christianity lasted for centuries

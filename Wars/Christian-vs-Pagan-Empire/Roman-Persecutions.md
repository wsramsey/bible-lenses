# Roman Persecutions of Christianity (64–313 AD)
*Not a war in the conventional sense — but Christianity's founding conflict*

## The Major Persecutions

| Emperor | Dates | Nature | Key Victims |
|---------|-------|--------|------------|
| **Nero** | 64–68 | Local, Rome only; blamed Christians for Great Fire | Peter, Paul |
| **Domitian** | 81–96 | Scattered; demanded emperor worship | John exiled to Patmos |
| **Trajan** | 98–117 | Policy: don't seek Christians, prosecute those denounced | Ignatius of Antioch |
| **Hadrian** | 117–138 | Restrained prosecution; required proof of crime | |
| **Marcus Aurelius** | 161–180 | Allowed local persecutions | Polycarp, Martyrs of Lyon |
| **Septimius Severus** | 193–211 | Prohibited conversion to Christianity | Perpetua and Felicity |
| **Maximinus Thrax** | 235–238 | Targeted clergy | |
| **Decius** | 249–251 | First empire-wide persecution; required sacrifice certificates (*libelli*) | Fabian of Rome, Origen tortured |
| **Valerian** | 253–260 | Targeted clergy and property | Sixtus II, Cyprian of Carthage |
| **Diocletian** | 303–311 | The Great Persecution — the worst; four edicts; destroyed churches; required Scripture surrender (*traditores*) | Thousands; created Donatist crisis |

## The Theological Significance
The persecutions produced:
- The **martyr theology** — bearing witness to the resurrection through death
- The **Donatist controversy** — what to do with those who surrendered Scriptures (*traditores*)
- The **development of Christian identity** — persecution clarified who was truly Christian
- **Ironically**: growth — Tertullian: "The blood of martyrs is the seed of the church"

## Constantine's Reversal
The Edict of Milan (313) ended the persecutions — Christianity went from illegal to legal to official within 70 years. The fastest reversal of fortune in ancient history.

# The English Civil War (1642–1651)
*King and Parliament; Anglican and Puritan; the war that executed a king*

## The Religious Dimension
The English Civil War was simultaneously:
- A constitutional conflict (the power of Parliament vs. the Crown)
- A religious conflict (Puritanism vs. Anglicanism)
- A class conflict (rising merchant class vs. aristocracy)

**King Charles I** believed in *jus divinum* — divine right of kings; attempted to impose an Anglican Prayer Book on Presbyterian Scotland; married a French Catholic; his court was perceived as crypto-Catholic.

**The Puritans**: Calvinist reform party within Anglicanism; demanded more thorough Protestant reformation; strong in Parliament and the eastern counties.

## The War
**1642**: Charles I raised his standard at Nottingham. The Royalist army ("Cavaliers") vs. the Parliamentary army ("Roundheads").

**1644–1645**: Oliver Cromwell's New Model Army — a professional force based on merit rather than birth; remarkably disciplined; sang psalms before battle.

**1645**: Battle of Naseby — decisive Parliamentary victory.

**1647**: Charles I surrendered, then negotiated secretly with the Scots, provoking further conflict.

**1649**: **Execution of Charles I** (January 30, 1649) — the first execution of a reigning monarch by his own parliament in European history. Shocked all of Europe. The Pope condemned it. It was both a constitutional and a religious act — Charles died as a martyr of the Church of England (later canonized as King Charles the Martyr).

## Cromwell's Commonwealth
Oliver Cromwell ruled as Lord Protector (1653–1658):
- Puritan governance; abolished Christmas as "popish"; closed theaters
- **Ireland (1649)**: Cromwell's campaign in Ireland — massacre of Drogheda and Wexford; tens of thousands killed; the Irish Catholic population dispossessed; the deepest wound in Irish-English history
- **The Jews**: Cromwell readmitted Jews to England (1656) after their 366-year expulsion — on pragmatic and theological grounds (millennialist expectation that Jews must be present in England before the Second Coming)

## The Restoration (1660)
Charles II restored; the monarchy returned; the Puritans lost. The **Act of Uniformity (1662)** ejected 2,000 Puritan ministers from the Church of England — the birth of English Nonconformism (Baptists, Congregationalists, Quakers, later Methodists).

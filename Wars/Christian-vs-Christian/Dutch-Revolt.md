# The Dutch Revolt / Eighty Years' War (1568–1648)
*Calvinist merchants vs. the Spanish Empire*

## The Conflict
The Low Countries (modern Netherlands and Belgium) revolted against Spanish Habsburg rule. The revolt combined:
- Calvinist resistance to Catholic rule
- Economic grievances against Spanish taxation
- Northern European nationalism against Spanish imperialism

## William the Silent (1533–1584)
William of Orange — leader of the revolt. Converted from Catholicism to Lutheranism to Calvinism as political convenience shifted; but also showed genuine religious conviction.

Assassinated by a Catholic on Philip II's orders in 1584 — the first assassination of a head of state with a handgun.

## The Spanish Terror
The Duke of Alba's "Council of Blood" (1567–1573): systematic suppression; thousands executed; 100,000 fled. This radicalized the Dutch and turned what might have been a political grievance into an existential religious war.

## The Fall of Antwerp (1576)
The "Spanish Fury" — mutinous Spanish soldiers sacked Antwerp; 8,000 civilians killed; Europe's richest city destroyed. Turned moderate Catholic Flemish citizens against Spain.

## The Union of Utrecht (1579)
The seven northern (largely Calvinist) provinces united. The foundation of the Dutch Republic. **The Dutch Republic became the most religiously tolerant state in Europe** — Jews, Arminians, Catholics, and many others found refuge there.

## The End
The Dutch Republic was formally recognized in the Peace of Westphalia (1648) — 80 years after the revolt began.

## The Legacy
- The Dutch Republic's tolerance was directly shaped by its founding conflict
- Spinoza, Descartes (exile), and many other thinkers took refuge in Holland
- Dutch capitalism (VOC, the first multinational corporation) was partly shaped by Calvinist ethic
- The "Dutch model" of religious tolerance influenced Locke and eventually the American First Amendment

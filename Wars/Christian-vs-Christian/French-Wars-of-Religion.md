# The French Wars of Religion (1562–1598)
*Eight civil wars; 3 million dead; the bloodiest intra-Christian conflict*

## The Context
By 1560, Calvinism had spread rapidly through France — the Huguenots (French Protestants) numbered perhaps 10% of the population but included much of the nobility. The Catholic majority and the monarchy felt threatened. The result was eight separate civil wars over 36 years.

## The St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre (August 23–24, 1572)
The defining atrocity of the French Wars of Religion.

King Charles IX, under the influence of his mother Catherine de' Medici, authorized the assassination of Huguenot leaders gathered in Paris for a royal wedding. What began as targeted killings spread into popular mob violence across Paris and then across France.

**Deaths**: 5,000–30,000 Huguenots killed in Paris and the provinces over the following weeks.

**The reactions**:
- Pope Gregory XIII ordered a *Te Deum* sung in Rome and commissioned Giorgio Vasari to paint murals of the massacre in the Vatican
- Philip II of Spain celebrated
- Protestant Europe was horrified; it became the defining example of Catholic brutality in Protestant propaganda for centuries

## Henry of Navarre (Henry IV)
The key figure: a Huguenot prince who inherited the throne of France, converted to Catholicism to end the wars ("Paris is worth a Mass"), and then issued the **Edict of Nantes (1598)** granting Huguenots religious toleration.

**The revocation**: Louis XIV revoked the Edict of Nantes in 1685. 200,000–400,000 Huguenots fled France — to England, the Dutch Republic, Prussia, South Africa, and America. France lost some of its most skilled craftsmen, merchants, and intellectuals. The revocation was one of the great self-inflicted economic wounds in French history.

# The Irish Religious Conflicts (1534–1998)
*Four centuries of violence at the intersection of religion and colonialism*

## Phase 1: The Reformation and Conquest (1534–1603)

**Henry VIII** broke with Rome (1534); Ireland remained Catholic. England's attempt to impose the Protestant Reformation on Ireland was the beginning of the conflict.

The **Nine Years' War (1593–1603)**: The last great Gaelic Irish revolt, led by Hugh O'Neill. Defeated by Elizabeth I. The Flight of the Earls (1607): the Gaelic aristocracy fled to Europe.

## Phase 2: The Ulster Plantation (1610)

The English government confiscated 500,000 acres of Ulster land and settled it with Scottish and English Protestant colonists. This is the root of modern Northern Ireland:
- A Protestant settler community imposed on a Catholic indigenous one
- The division that persists to this day

## Phase 3: The 1641 Rebellion and Cromwell (1641–1652)

**1641 Rebellion**: Irish Catholics rose against Protestant settlers; approximately 4,000 settlers killed; atrocity stories (exaggerated) shaped English attitudes for a century.

**Cromwell's Irish campaign (1649–1653)**:
- Massacre of Drogheda (September 1649): 3,500 soldiers and civilians killed; Cromwell's justification: "the righteous judgment of God upon these barbarous wretches"
- Massacre of Wexford: 2,000 killed
- Mass transplantation of Irish Catholics to Connacht: "To Hell or Connacht"
- Irish land ownership collapsed from 60% to less than 20%

## Phase 4: The Williamite War (1689–1691)

**The Glorious Revolution (1688)**: Protestant William of Orange replaced Catholic King James II.

**The Battle of the Boyne (July 12, 1690)**: William III defeated James II. Still commemorated annually by Protestant Orangemen in Northern Ireland — one of the most politicized historical memories in the world.

**The Treaty of Limerick (1691)**: The Catholic army allowed to leave; the treaty's promises of toleration were immediately broken; the **Penal Laws** stripped Catholics of political and economic rights.

## Phase 5: The Great Famine and Independence (1845–1921)

**The Great Famine (1845–1852)**: 1 million dead; 1 million emigrated; population fell from 8 million to 6 million. The British government's inadequate response (while food continued to be exported from Ireland) shaped Irish nationalist memory.

**1916 Easter Rising** and the Irish War of Independence (1919–1921): Ireland partitioned; the Irish Free State (Catholic majority) and Northern Ireland (Protestant majority) established.

## Phase 6: The Troubles (1969–1998)

**The Troubles**: 30 years of conflict in Northern Ireland between:
- **Nationalist/Republican** (predominantly Catholic): wanted Irish unification
- **Unionist/Loyalist** (predominantly Protestant): wanted to remain in the UK

**Deaths**: approximately 3,500 people killed.

Key events:
- **Bloody Sunday (January 30, 1972)**: British paratroopers shot 26 civil rights marchers in Derry; 14 died; massive escalation
- **The IRA bombing campaigns** in Northern Ireland and England
- **Loyalist paramilitary violence** against Catholics

**The Good Friday Agreement (1998)**: Peace settlement; power-sharing government; most violence ended.

## The Theological Dimension
The conflict in Ireland is often called "religious" but is better described as a conflict in which religious identity became a marker for political and ethnic identity:
- Being Catholic = Irish nationalist
- Being Protestant = British unionist
- The theological differences between Catholics and Protestants were rarely the actual issue

**The role of the Catholic Church**: sometimes nationalist, sometimes cautious; the IRA was condemned by the Church but also operated within Catholic communities

**The role of Protestant churches**: sometimes loyalist, sometimes prophetic peacemakers; the Corrymeela Community (inter-denominational) worked for reconciliation from 1965

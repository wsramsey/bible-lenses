# The Reconquista (711–1492 AD)
*781 years of war to reclaim the Iberian Peninsula*

## The Context
Muslim armies under Tariq ibn Ziyad crossed from North Africa into Spain (711) and within 7 years had conquered nearly all of the Visigothic Christian kingdom. The **Battle of Covadonga (722)** — a small Christian victory in northern Spain under Pelayo — is traditionally considered the beginning of the Reconquista.

## The Phases

### Phase 1: The Long Stalemate (722–1000)
- Small Christian kingdoms in the north (Asturias, León, Castile, Aragon, Navarre) held out
- Al-Andalus (Muslim Spain) was a sophisticated, multicultural civilization
- **The Convivencia ("coexistence")**: Jews, Christians, and Muslims lived in relative tolerance in Al-Andalus; a golden age of Islamic scholarship; Moses Maimonides was born in Córdoba
- Slow Christian expansion southward

### Phase 2: Acceleration (1000–1250)
- The Taifa period: Al-Andalus fragmented into small kingdoms (*taifas*); easier to reconquer
- **Toledo recaptured (1085)** — the first major city; Alfonso VI; a huge Christian victory
- **The Almoravid intervention (1086)** — North African Berber Muslims invited in; halted Christian advance
- **The Almohad Caliphate** — replaced Almoravids; more zealous; ended the Convivencia; expelled or forced conversion of Jews and Christians in Muslim territory
- **Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa (1212)** — crushing defeat of the Almohads by a coalition of Christian kings; turning point

### Phase 3: Completion (1250–1492)
- Major cities fall: Córdoba (1236), Seville (1248), Valencia
- Only the Kingdom of Granada remains as a Muslim enclave
- **Fall of Granada (January 2, 1492)** — Ferdinand and Isabella; the last Muslim kingdom in Spain surrenders

## The 1492 Triple Event
Three events within months of each other that changed history:
1. **January 2**: Fall of Granada — end of Reconquista
2. **March 31**: Edict of Expulsion — 100,000–200,000 Jews expelled from Spain
3. **August 3**: Columbus sails — beginning of the Americas chapter

## The Legacy
- Spanish nationalism is built on the Reconquista narrative
- The Reconquista model influenced the conquistadors in the Americas
- The Inquisition was partly a Reconquista institution — checking the sincerity of Muslim and Jewish converts
- The expulsion of the Jews and Muslims was one of history's most significant ethnic cleansings
- The Convivencia (however idealized) represents one of the few extended examples of Muslim-Christian-Jewish coexistence

## El Cid (c. 1043–1099)
Rodrigo Díaz de Vivar — the great hero of the Reconquista. Fought for both Christian and Muslim rulers depending on political circumstances; conquered Valencia. The ambiguity of the Reconquista in one person.

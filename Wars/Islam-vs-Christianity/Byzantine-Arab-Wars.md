# Byzantine-Arab Wars (630s–1453 AD)
*800 years of warfare ending in the fall of Constantinople*

## The Long War

### Phase 1: Arab Conquest (630s–718)
- Arab armies take Syria, Palestine, Egypt, North Africa from Byzantium
- Two Arab sieges of Constantinople: 674–678 and 717–718 — both failed
- Greek fire (an incendiary naval weapon) helped defend Constantinople

### Phase 2: Frontier Wars (718–1071)
- Byzantine counterattacks; recovery of some territory
- Emperors like Nicephorus Phocas and John I Tzimiskes reconquered parts of Syria
- Long frontier; continuous raiding on both sides

### Phase 3: Seljuk Turkish Advance (1071–1099)
- **Battle of Manzikert (1071)** — Seljuk Turks defeated Byzantium; captured Emperor Romanus IV
- Asia Minor (the heartland of Byzantine Christianity) permanently lost to Turks
- Emperor Alexios I asked Pope Urban II for help → **the First Crusade** (1095)

### Phase 4: Crusader Period (1099–1261)
- Crusaders established Latin states in the Holy Land; Byzantine relations complex and often hostile
- **The Fourth Crusade (1204)** — Crusaders sacked Constantinople; Latin Empire established; Byzantine Empire in exile
- Byzantines recovered Constantinople 1261 (Michael VIII Palaiologos)

### Phase 5: Ottoman Advance (1354–1453)
- Ottoman Turks cross into Europe (1354); rapidly expand in the Balkans
- **Siege of Constantinople (1453)** — Mehmed II's Ottoman army (80,000+) vs. 7,000 defenders
- **May 29, 1453** — Constantinople falls; Emperor Constantine XI dies fighting; the Hagia Sophia becomes a mosque
- End of the Byzantine Empire; end of the eastern Roman Empire; beginning of the Ottoman Empire in Europe

## The Significance
The fall of Constantinople was one of the most significant events in world history:
- End of the Christian Greek East as an independent power
- Thousands of Greek scholars fled to the West — contributing to the Italian Renaissance
- The Orthodox Church survived under Ottoman rule as the *Rum millet*
- The event is still commemorated annually by the Greek Orthodox Church

## The Greek Orthodox Survival
The Ecumenical Patriarch continued in Constantinople under Ottoman rule:
- Mehmed II appointed Gennadius Scholarius as patriarch (1454)
- The church survived but under severe restrictions
- The *dhimmi* system applied to all Christians

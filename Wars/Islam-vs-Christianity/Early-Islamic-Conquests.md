# The Early Islamic Conquests (632–732 AD)
*Christianity loses its heartlands in a century*

## The Scale
In 100 years, Islam conquered:
- The entire Arabian Peninsula (632–633)
- Syria and Palestine — the birthplace of Christianity (636–640)
- Egypt — where Alexandria was the intellectual center of Christianity (641–642)
- Persia (633–651)
- North Africa — Carthage, Augustine's homeland (647–709)
- Spain — the Visigothic Christian kingdom (711–718)

## Key Battles

**Battle of Yarmouk (636)** — Syria
Arab armies under Khalid ibn al-Walid defeated the Byzantine army. Syria and Palestine fell. Jerusalem surrendered to Caliph Umar (638).

**Battle of the Yarmuk / Alexandria (641–642)** — Egypt
Amr ibn al-As conquered Egypt. Alexandria — one of the greatest cities of the ancient world and a center of Christian theology — became Muslim-controlled.

**Battle of Tours/Poitiers (732)** — France
Charles Martel defeated the Umayyad army under Abd al-Rahman al-Ghafiqi. Edward Gibbon famously wrote that if Martel had lost, "the Arabian fleet might have sailed without a naval combat into the mouth of the Thames" and "the interpretation of the Koran would now be taught in the schools of Oxford."

Historians debate whether this battle was as decisive as Gibbon claimed — the Arab advance may have already been at its limit. But it did mark the end of northward expansion.

## The Christian Response
The loss of the Middle East and North Africa was catastrophic:
- The Patriarchates of Jerusalem, Antioch, Alexandria — all under Muslim rule
- The theological schools, libraries, and monasteries of Egypt — gradually in decline
- The split between Eastern (still fighting Byzantium) and Western (Spain) Christianity
- **The Crusades** (1095+) were partly the long-delayed military response to these conquests

## The Treatment of Christians Under Early Islam
The *dhimmi* system:
- Christians and Jews were "People of the Book" — tolerated but subordinate
- Special tax (*jizya*); restrictions on building churches; prohibited from proselytizing
- Relatively protected compared to later periods; many Christian scholars thrived
- Gradual conversion over centuries through social pressure and legal disadvantage

# The Crusades — Complete Military and Religious History
*Nine major campaigns; complex legacy; still unresolved*

## The Call
Pope Urban II at Clermont (1095): "God wills it!" (*Deus vult!*) — called for an armed pilgrimage to recover Jerusalem from the Seljuk Turks, who had recently defeated Byzantium at Manzikert (1071) and restricted Christian access to holy sites.

**The motivations** (all present simultaneously):
- Genuine religious devotion; pilgrimage as an armed act of piety
- The indulgence promise — remission of all penance for participants
- Political — Byzantine Emperor Alexios I's request for mercenaries became something much larger
- Economic — landless younger sons seeking fortune; Italian city-states seeking trade routes
- Social — outlet for the violent military class of feudal Europe

## The Major Crusades

### First Crusade (1096–1099)
**The People's Crusade (1096)**: Peter the Hermit led 40,000 peasants; massacred Jews in the Rhineland en route; destroyed in Anatolia by the Turks before the military campaign began.

**The Military Crusade**: Four great armies of French and Norman knights; total perhaps 100,000. Key leaders: Godfrey of Bouillon, Raymond IV of Toulouse, Bohemund of Taranto, Robert of Normandy.

**Siege of Antioch (1097–1098)**: Eight-month siege; city taken; crusaders then besieged inside; miraculous discovery of the "Holy Lance"; morale restored.

**Fall of Jerusalem (July 15, 1099)**: The city captured after a six-week siege. The massacre that followed was one of the most brutal in medieval history — eyewitness accounts describe blood ankle-deep in the Temple Mount area. Muslims and Jews alike were killed.

**Result**: Four Crusader states established — Kingdom of Jerusalem, County of Tripoli, County of Edessa, Principality of Antioch.

### Second Crusade (1147–1149)
Preached by Bernard of Clairvaux after the fall of Edessa (1144). Led by Louis VII of France and Conrad III of Germany. Besieged Damascus — a Christian-allied city — and failed. Complete disaster.

### Third Crusade (1189–1192) — "The Kings' Crusade"
Triggered by Saladin's reconquest of Jerusalem (1187). Led by Richard I of England ("the Lionheart"), Philip II of France, and Frederick I Barbarossa (drowned crossing a river).

**Saladin (Salah ad-Din Yusuf ibn Ayyub, 1137–1193)**: The Kurdish Muslim general who reunified Muslim Syria and Egypt, reconquered Jerusalem (October 2, 1187), and fought Richard I to a standstill. His chivalrous conduct toward prisoners and non-combatants contrasted sharply with Crusader behavior and made him more admired in medieval Europe than most Crusader leaders.

**Richard vs. Saladin**: A military standoff. Richard could not retake Jerusalem. The **Treaty of Jaffa (1192)**: Christians allowed pilgrimage access to Jerusalem; the city remained Muslim. Richard departed; never returned.

**The Massacre at Ayyadieh (1191)**: Richard executed 2,700 Muslim prisoners when Saladin delayed agreed prisoner exchange. One of the most condemned acts of the Crusades.

### Fourth Crusade (1202–1204) — The Great Betrayal
Originally aimed at Egypt. Diverted to Constantinople.

**The sack of Constantinople (April 12–15, 1204)**: Latin Crusaders looted the greatest Christian city in the world — stealing relics, destroying art, killing Orthodox Christians, installing a Latin patriarch. The Hagia Sophia desecrated. The Byzantine Empire temporarily replaced by a Latin Empire.

This is arguably the most consequential event of the Crusades: **Catholics killing Orthodox Christians and destroying the Christian East's greatest city**. The wound has never fully healed. The Fourth Crusade permanently poisoned Catholic-Orthodox relations and is a major reason Orthodox Christians view Rome with deep suspicion.

Pope Innocent III condemned the sack — after the fact. He had been unable to control the forces he set in motion.

### Fifth Crusade (1217–1221)
Target: Egypt (the strategic key to Palestine). Captured Damietta; then bogged down; total failure.

### Sixth Crusade (1228–1229) — The Diplomatic Crusade
Frederick II, Holy Roman Emperor — excommunicated by the Pope at the time — negotiated a treaty with the Ayyubid Sultan al-Kamil. Jerusalem returned to Crusader control for 15 years through diplomacy. Frederick was condemned by the Pope and by many Crusaders for "making peace with the infidel."

**The lesson**: diplomacy succeeded where warfare had failed. The church preferred a failed military crusade to a successful diplomatic agreement with Muslims.

### Seventh and Eighth Crusades (1248–1270) — Louis IX
King Louis IX of France (Saint Louis) led two crusades. Both failed. He died of dysentery in Tunis (1270).

### Ninth Crusade (1271–1272)
Edward I of England. Achieved little. The last major crusade to the Holy Land.

### Fall of Acre (1291)
The last Crusader stronghold in Palestine fell to the Mamluks. The Crusader period ended.

## The Northern Crusades (12th–15th century)
Often forgotten: crusades against pagan peoples in Northern and Eastern Europe:
- **The Livonian Crusade** — against Baltic peoples (modern Latvia/Estonia); the Teutonic Knights
- **The Albigensian Crusade** — against Cathar heretics in southern France (see Inquisition notes)
- **The Hussite Crusades** — five crusades against Hussites in Bohemia; all failed

## The Spanish Reconquista as Crusade
The Reconquista was officially declared a crusade; participants received the same indulgences.

## The Assessment

### What the Crusades Achieved
- Temporary Crusader states (1099–1291)
- Galvanized European identity around a common religious cause
- Accelerated trade between Europe and the Middle East
- Transferred knowledge from the Islamic world to Europe (mathematics, philosophy, medicine)

### What the Crusades Destroyed
- Christian-Muslim relations — the word "Crusade" is still toxic in the Arab/Muslim world
- Christian-Orthodox relations — the Fourth Crusade's sack of Constantinople
- Christian-Jewish relations — the Rhineland massacres
- The prestige of the papacy — Urban II's promise of divine victory was repeatedly unfulfilled
- **Lives**: hundreds of thousands killed over two centuries

### Modern Assessment
- Tom Holland (*Dominion*): the Crusades were a complex response to genuine loss; framing them as simply aggression ignores the Muslim conquest of Christian territories that preceded them
- Most historians: the Crusades were a catastrophic failure of Christian witness regardless of their political context
- Islamic world: the Crusades are a founding trauma; Osama bin Laden called the US a "Crusader state"

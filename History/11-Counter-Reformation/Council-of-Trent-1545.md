# Council of Trent (1545–1563)
*The Catholic Church responds to the Reformation — and defines itself against it*

## Why It Was Called
Luther (1517), Zwingli (1520s), Calvin (1530s) — Protestantism was spreading rapidly. The Council was the Catholic response: reform genuine abuses while defining Catholic doctrine clearly against Protestant challenges.

## The Major Decisions
**On Scripture**: Scripture AND Tradition (not Scripture alone); the Latin Vulgate is authoritative
**On Justification**: both faith AND works; grace cooperating with free will; against Luther's *sola fide*
**On the Sacraments**: seven sacraments confirmed; transubstantiation defined precisely
**On the Mass**: the Mass is a true sacrifice; Eucharist in one kind for laity
**On Purgatory**: affirmed; indulgences reformed (not abolished)

## The Reforms
- Bishops must reside in their dioceses
- Seminaries established for proper clerical training
- Simony (buying church offices) prohibited
- Catechism produced (*Roman Catechism*, 1566)

## The Permanent Division
Trent's decisions made Protestant-Catholic reunion virtually impossible for 400 years. It defined Catholicism precisely *against* the Reformation. The anathemas (*"If anyone says... let him be anathema"*) created a wall.

## Vatican II's Relationship to Trent
Vatican II (1962–1965) was partly a re-opening of questions Trent had closed — especially on Scripture, ecumenism, and religious liberty. The tension between Trent and Vatican II is the defining conflict of contemporary Catholicism.

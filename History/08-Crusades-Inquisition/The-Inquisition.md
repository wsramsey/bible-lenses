# The Inquisition (1184–1834 AD)
*Six centuries of institutional heresy-hunting*

## The Types
1. **Episcopal Inquisition (1184)** — Bishops required to investigate heresy
2. **Papal Inquisition (1231)** — Dominican friars as inquisitors; systematic procedures
3. **Spanish Inquisition (1478–1834)** — Crown-controlled; targeted conversos and moriscos
4. **Roman Inquisition (1542–1965)** — Index of Forbidden Books; Galileo case

## How It Worked
- Inquisitor arrives; 30-day "grace period" for voluntary confession
- Denunciations accepted anonymously
- Suspects unaware of charges or accusers
- Confession required (with torture permitted after 1252)
- "Relaxation to the secular arm" = death by burning (the church didn't execute directly)

## The Numbers
- Spanish Inquisition: estimated 3,000–5,000 executions over 356 years (not the millions of legend)
- Inquisitorial courts were actually more procedurally fair than secular courts of the era — still deeply unjust
- The torture methods were constrained compared to secular courts — still torture

## The Galileo Case (1633)
Galileo Galilei was tried for heliocentrism (the earth moves around the sun). Forced to recant. The case became the myth of "religion vs. science." The reality: the science was genuinely uncertain; the church overreached; the principle of imprisoning someone for their scientific views is indefensible. John Paul II formally acknowledged the error in 1992.

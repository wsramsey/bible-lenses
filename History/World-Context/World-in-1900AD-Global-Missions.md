# The World of the Great Missionary Era
*1800–1914 · Empire, Abolition, and the Global Spread of Christianity*

## The World Context
The 19th century missionary movement happened in a specific world:
- **The British Empire** at its height — controlling a quarter of the earth's land surface
- **The Industrial Revolution** — steamships made global travel possible; telegraphs made communication possible
- **The Scramble for Africa (1880s)** — European powers divided Africa; missionaries often preceded (or followed) colonizers
- **The collapse of traditional societies** — contact with European civilization was destabilizing indigenous cultures worldwide
- **The abolition of the slave trade (1807)** and slavery (1833) — evangelical Christians led this; it gave missions moral credibility

## The Missionary Movement's Assumptions
**William Carey** (1761–1834), the "Father of Modern Missions," went to India in 1793 with these convictions:
- The Great Commission (Matt 28:19–20) is obligatory for all Christians, not just apostles
- Other cultures have genuine intellectual and spiritual life; engage them
- Education, medicine, and Bible translation are all part of mission
- Christianity and civilization go together (this assumption would cause enormous harm)

**The "civilization and Christianity" linkage** was the era's fatal flaw:
- David Livingstone opened Africa to the Gospel *and* to British commerce
- "Christianity, commerce, and civilization" was Livingstone's formula
- This made missionaries complicit in colonialism, even when they personally opposed it
- The same missionaries who fought slavery often accepted a paternalistic view of "backward" peoples

## The Medical Mission
**Albert Schweitzer** (1875–1965) — theologian, musician, physician; built a hospital in Gabon; won the Nobel Peace Prize. Represented the best of the tradition: genuine service, no conversion requirement.

**Amy Carmichael** (1867–1951) — Ireland to India; Dohnavur Fellowship; rescued children from temple prostitution; never returned to England; *If* is one of the great devotional texts.

## The Cost
The missionary era's costs:
- **Cultural destruction** — indigenous languages, practices, and worldviews were often dismissed as demonic
- **Residential schools** — the worst fruit: Christian-run schools that abused indigenous children
- **Complicity with empire** — too many missionaries benefited from colonial protection
- **But also:** Bible translation preserved hundreds of languages; medical missions saved millions; education created indigenous leaders who eventually led independence movements

## Why This Matters for Reading Missionary Literature
When you read Carey, Taylor, Livingstone, or Carmichael — you're reading people of genuine faith and genuine cultural blindness simultaneously. The same person who sacrificed everything for the Gospel could assume that Western culture was superior to every other.

This is not hypocrisy; it's the limit of anyone shaped by their moment. Understanding their world makes their achievements more remarkable and their failures more forgivable — and more instructive.

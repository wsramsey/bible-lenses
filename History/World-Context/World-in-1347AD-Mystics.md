# The World of the 14th Century Mystics
*1300–1400 AD · The Age of Crisis and Interior Fire*

## The Catastrophes
The 14th century was one of the most catastrophic in European history — and also one of the most mystically fertile. The two facts are connected.

**The Black Death (1347–1351)**
- Killed 30–60% of Europe's population — perhaps 50 million people
- Some regions lost 70–80%
- Returned repeatedly through the rest of the century
- **What it did to religion:** When mass death is random, arbitrary, unstoppable — and when the church's prayers, sacraments, and pilgrimages don't stop it — people either abandon faith or go deeper inside

**The Avignon Papacy (1309–1377)**
- The papacy relocated to Avignon under French control
- Seven French popes; widely seen as corrupt and politically compromised
- **Catherine of Siena** literally wrote letters to the pope demanding he return to Rome — and he eventually did
- The institutional church was visibly failing; people looked elsewhere for God

**The Great Western Schism (1378–1417)**
- After the return to Rome, a rival papacy reestablished in Avignon
- Two (then three) simultaneous popes
- No one knew whose sacraments were valid
- Which pope's excommunication meant anything?

**The Hundred Years' War (1337–1453)**
- France and England at war; the countryside devastated
- **Joan of Arc** (1412–1431) — a mystical visionary who turned the war

## What the Mystics Were Responding To
In this context — mass death, institutional corruption, schism, war — the Rhineland mystics, the English mystics, and the Italian mystics all made the same move:

**If the external church is broken, God must be found within.**

- **Meister Eckhart** — the ground of the soul where God and the soul are one; beyond images, beyond institutions
- **Tauler** — preached to the dying in plague-stricken Strasbourg; mysticism for ordinary people in crisis
- **Julian of Norwich** — received her visions in 1373 *after surviving* a near-death illness; "all shall be well" spoken into a world where nothing was well
- **Catherine of Siena** — nursed plague victims, then reformed the papacy; contemplation and action unified
- **The Cloud of Unknowing** — written to a young man asking for deeper prayer; the institutional church recedes; the naked intent toward God remains

## Julian's World Specifically
Julian was born c. 1342 — **during** the Black Death's first wave. She almost certainly lost family members. She received her visions in May 1373 during a near-fatal illness. She wrote *Revelations of Divine Love* in this context.

"All shall be well, and all shall be well, and all manner of thing shall be well."

This is not optimism. This is faith spoken by a woman who had seen mass death, institutional failure, and her own near-death — and chose, in the face of all of it, to believe in God's love.

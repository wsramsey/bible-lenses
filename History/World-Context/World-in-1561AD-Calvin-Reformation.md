# The World Calvin Wrote In
*1536–1564 · The Reformation at War with Itself and Rome*

## The Continent Calvin Inhabited
By the time Calvin published the first edition of the *Institutes* (1536), the Reformation had already fractured:
- **Luther vs. Zwingli** — they had met at Marburg (1529) and split over the Eucharist; Protestant unity was already broken
- **The Peasants' War (1524–1525)** — 100,000 German peasants died; Luther's endorsement of the princes' brutal suppression haunted Protestantism
- **The Anabaptists** — radical reformers being executed by both Catholics and Lutherans
- **Henry VIII's break with Rome (1534)** — for entirely political reasons; the Reformation was becoming entangled with nationalism

## The Violence of the Age
Calvin's world was saturated with religious violence:
- **1534 — The Affair of the Placards** — radical Protestants posted anti-Catholic placards across France; brutal Catholic reprisals; Calvin fled France
- **1540s-1560s — French Wars of Religion** — 3 million deaths; the Huguenots (Calvin's Reformed) vs. Catholic France
- **1553 — Michael Servetus burned in Geneva** — Calvin approved the execution; the Reformation was capable of the same violence it condemned in Rome
- **1563 — Heidelberg Catechism** — Calvin's theology spreading to Germany, Netherlands, Scotland

## Geneva as Calvin's Laboratory
Geneva was a city of 13,000 people — a small but strategically located city-state in the Alps. Calvin turned it into what John Knox called "the most perfect school of Christ that ever was in the earth since the days of the apostles."
- Weekly sermons; daily biblical lectures (Calvin preached 286 sermons on Acts alone)
- The Consistory enforced moral discipline — and this was genuinely controversial and resented
- The Geneva Academy trained pastors and sent them across Europe as missionaries

## The Catholic Counter-Response
The Council of Trent (1545-1563) ran almost exactly parallel to Calvin's ministry in Geneva. While Calvin was writing theology, the Catholic Church was defining its own positions precisely against his.

## Why This Matters for Reading Calvin
The *Institutes* is not written in a library by a safe academic. Calvin was a French exile who could have been executed for heresy, who watched his theological allies die for their beliefs, who governed a contentious city, who feared Catholic reprisal daily.

His emphasis on God's sovereignty — predestination, providence, the absolute majesty of God — makes psychological sense for a man whose world was chaos. If God is in control, the martyrdoms and wars and exile have meaning.

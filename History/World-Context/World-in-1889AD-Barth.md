# The World Karl Barth Was Born Into
*1886–1968 · The Century That Killed Liberal Optimism*

## The World of Barth's Formation
Barth grew up in the world of **liberal Protestant theology** at its most confident:
- **Darwinism** had been absorbed; evolution was now compatible with faith
- **Historical criticism** had "demythologized" Scripture; scholars knew what Jesus "really" said
- **The Social Gospel** was making the world better through Christian social reform
- **Harnack and Herrmann** — Barth's own professors — believed Christianity's essence was timeless moral and spiritual values; Jesus was the supreme moral teacher
- **Progress** — the 19th century's deep faith; technology, democracy, education were solving humanity's problems
- **The German university** was the intellectual center of the world

## August 1914 — The Earthquake
**The 93 intellectuals' declaration** supporting Kaiser Wilhelm II's war: 93 German academics signed a manifesto supporting Germany's invasion of Belgium. Among them: virtually every theologian Barth had studied under, including Harnack.

Barth wrote later: "An entire world of theological exegesis, ethics, dogmatics and preaching, which up to that point I had accepted as basically credible, was *shattered*."

If liberal theology could produce the Kaiser's chaplains, it was not the Gospel.

## World War I: The Numbers
- **17 million dead** — civilian and military
- **Entire generation of young men** gone from France, Britain, Germany
- **The Ottoman Empire collapses** — the Middle East reshaped (Sykes-Picot; the seeds of modern conflict)
- **Russian Revolution (1917)** — Communism as a messianic political religion
- **The optimism of progress** — dead

Barth wrote his *Epistle to the Romans* (1919) **in this context**. The "infinite qualitative distinction between God and man" — God as wholly Other, not the highest human value — was a direct response to a Christianity that had become too cozy with human civilization.

## The Weimar Republic and the Rise of Hitler
- **1919–1933** — Weimar Germany: inflation, humiliation, chaos
- **1933** — Hitler comes to power; Barth is professor at Bonn
- **1934** — Barth drafts the Barmen Declaration; refuses to begin his lectures with "Heil Hitler"; expelled from Germany
- **1939–1945** — World War II; 60–85 million dead; the Holocaust

## Why Barth's Theology Makes Sense in This World
Liberal theology said: God is the highest human value, the ground of our deepest aspirations, the fulfillment of our moral potential.

After the trenches, after Auschwitz, Barth said: **No.** God is not the highest human value. God is the judge of all human values. The Word of God stands over against all human religion, including Christianity. You cannot get from the human to the divine — only from the divine to the human.

This is not abstract. It's the theology of a man who watched Christianity baptize mass murder and watched German Christians line up behind a demonic regime.

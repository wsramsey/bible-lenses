# The World of Kierkegaard and Newman
*1830–1880 AD · Faith Under the Double Pressure of Industry and Reason*

## The Age of Machinery and Doubt
The 1840s were the decade of:
- **The Irish Famine (1845–1852)** — 1 million dead, 2 million emigrate; the catastrophe of Catholic Europe's most Catholic nation
- **The Communist Manifesto (1848)** — Marx and Engels; history as class struggle; religion as opium
- **The Revolutions of 1848** — across Europe; the old order trembling
- **The Railway Age** — industrial capitalism transforming every aspect of life
- **Darwin's research** — *Origin of Species* (1859) just 15 years away; already rumors circulating

## What Was Happening to Christianity
**From the left** — Feuerbach (*The Essence of Christianity*, 1841): God is a projection of human nature; theology is really anthropology. The most intelligent critique of Christianity since the Greeks.

**From the "center"** — Liberal Protestantism (Schleiermacher, Hegel): religion is about feeling and moral values, not supernatural claims. Christianity is evolution's highest religious form.

**From science** — Geology was already showing the earth was ancient; Darwin was coming; biblical chronology was crumbling.

**From economics** — Industrial capitalism was creating misery; the church was largely aligned with the property-owning classes; working-class men were leaving Christianity.

## Kierkegaard's Response (1840s Copenhagen)
Kierkegaard looked at the Danish state church — comfortable, respectable, socially beneficial, culturally mandatory — and said: **this is not Christianity.**

His context: Copenhagen bourgeois society where everyone was "Christian" by default. His target: the comfortable faith that costs nothing.

The three stages — aesthetic → ethical → religious — describe a path to the authentic faith that requires a "leap," not a rational argument. You cannot argue your way to God; you must choose.

He wrote in *Fear and Trembling* (1843) — the year of revolutions, of industrial misery, of Feuerbach — that Abraham's willingness to sacrifice Isaac represents the scandalous particularity of faith: it cannot be justified by ethics or reason. It is a leap into the absurd.

## Newman's Response (1840s Oxford)
Newman faced a different version of the same crisis: the Church of England was being hollowed out by the state. Parliament (which included non-Anglicans) was legislating church affairs.

His response: **recover the patristic roots**. The Oxford Movement went back to the Church Fathers. If modern liberal Christianity is bankrupt, the answer is the oldest Christianity.

But following the Fathers logically led Newman to Rome (1845) — the most dramatic intellectual conversion of the Victorian age.

His *Essay on Development* (1845) answers the question: how do you account for the difference between early Christianity and 19th century Catholicism? Answer: like a seed growing into a tree, not like an organism becoming diseased.

## Why This Matters
Both Kierkegaard and Newman were responding to **comfortable, socially acceptable Christianity** that had lost its edge. Both demanded a faith that cost something.

- Kierkegaard: the **inward leap**; authenticity; the individual before God
- Newman: the **historical tradition**; the church as teacher; the convert's hard choice

They never read each other but were answering the same question from opposite directions.

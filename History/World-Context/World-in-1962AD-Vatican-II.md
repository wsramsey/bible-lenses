# The World That Produced Vatican II and Postwar Theology
*1945–1970 · Between Hiroshima and Woodstock*

## The World After World War II
The theologians who shaped the mid-20th century church were all living in the aftermath of catastrophe:

**What the Holocaust did to theology:**
- The God of Christian history had allowed (or the church had enabled) the murder of 6 million Jews
- Every Christian theological tradition had to reckon with this
- Auschwitz as theological challenge: where was God? how could a Christian God permit this?
- [[Jürgen Moltmann]] (*Theology of Hope*, 1964): he was a German POW who converted in captivity; the resurrection as protest against the death camps
- **Jewish-Christian dialogue** becomes theologically urgent: [[Franz Rosenzweig]], [[Emmanuel Levinas]], [[Martin Buber]]

**What the Atom Bomb did:**
- Hiroshima (August 6, 1945) — 80,000 dead instantly
- For the first time: humanity has the power to destroy civilization
- **Reinhold Niebuhr's "Christian realism"** — nations are sinful; nuclear deterrence is a tragic necessity; the liberal idealism of the Social Gospel is naïve

**The Cold War (1947–1991):**
- The world divided between US capitalism and Soviet communism
- Both claim to be secular salvation; both are pseudo-religious
- The nuclear threat as permanent background context

## The Civil Rights Movement (1954–1968)
- **Brown v. Board of Education (1954)**
- **Rosa Parks (1955), Montgomery Bus Boycott**
- **Martin Luther King Jr.** — a Baptist pastor using the church as organizational base
- The most significant moral challenge to American Christianity since abolition
- White Southern churches overwhelmingly supporting segregation
- King's "Letter from Birmingham Jail" addressed to *white moderate clergy*

## The Secularization of Western Europe
- **Church attendance collapsing** in France, Germany, Britain after the war
- The educated European working class abandoning Christianity
- **"The death of God" theology (1960s)** — Altizer, Hamilton: maybe God is absent or dead
- **Harvey Cox, *The Secular City* (1965):** secularization as liberation; Christianity must embrace the secular

## Vatican II's Specific Historical Context
Pope John XXIII convened Vatican II (1962) in this world:
- Cold War threat of nuclear annihilation
- Decolonization — Africa and Asia becoming independent; the church there is growing rapidly
- The Holocaust and Jewish-Christian relations
- Communism in Eastern Europe (many bishops attending were from behind the Iron Curtain)
- Western secularization
- The birth control pill (1960) — about to create a crisis John didn't foresee

## Why This Matters
The documents of Vatican II — on the church, on religious liberty, on the Jews, on the modern world — cannot be read without this context. They are not abstract theology. They are responses to:
- How do we talk to a secular world that has seen Auschwitz?
- How do we relate to Jews after the Holocaust?
- How do we speak about religious liberty after Nazism showed the church what happens when it surrenders to the state?
- How do we respond to the explosion of Christianity in the Global South?

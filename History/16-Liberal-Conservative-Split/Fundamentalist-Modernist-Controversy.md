# The Fundamentalist-Modernist Controversy (1890s–1930s)
*The split that shaped American Christianity to this day*

## The Background
By 1900, mainline Protestant seminaries had widely adopted historical-critical methods ([[Wellhausen]]'s JEDP; [[Bultmann]]'s form criticism), evolutionary theory, and progressive theology. The response was the publication of *The Fundamentals* (1910–1915) — 90 essays defending:
- Biblical inerrancy
- Virgin birth
- Physical resurrection
- Miracles
- Substitutionary atonement

## The Fosdick Controversy (1922)
**Harry Emerson Fosdick** (Baptist preacher at a Presbyterian pulpit) preached "Shall the Fundamentalists Win?" — arguing that modern people cannot be required to believe in the Virgin Birth or the Second Coming. **J. Gresham Machen** responded with *Christianity and Liberalism* (1923): liberalism is not Christianity but a different religion entirely.

## The Scopes Trial (1925)
John Scopes, a Tennessee teacher, was tried for teaching evolution. William Jennings Bryan (anti-evolution) vs. Clarence Darrow (ACLU). Scopes found guilty (fine $100). The national media mocked fundamentalism.

The trial drove fundamentalism underground and into separate institutions — Bible colleges, radio ministries, parachurch organizations — creating the infrastructure for the later evangelical resurgence.

## The Presbyterian Schism (1936)
Machen left the Presbyterian Church and founded what became the **Orthodox Presbyterian Church**. The mainline denominations moved left; conservatives left or were marginalized.

## The Long Shadow
This split defines American Christianity:
- **Mainline Protestants** (Methodist, Presbyterian USA, Episcopal, UCC) — liberal trajectory
- **Evangelical/Fundamentalist** — separate institutions; Billy Graham's "neo-evangelicalism" as the irenic middle

# The Crusades (1095–1291 AD)
*Nine major campaigns — the most complex legacy in Christian history*

## The Call
Pope Urban II at the Council of Clermont (1095): "God wills it!" (*Deus vult!*) — called for an armed pilgrimage to recover Jerusalem from the Seljuk Turks. Promised remission of sins to participants.

## The Major Crusades
1. **First (1096–1099)** — Captured Jerusalem; massacred Muslim and Jewish population
2. **Second (1147–1149)** — Failed; precipitated by fall of Edessa
3. **Third (1189–1192)** — Saladin had retaken Jerusalem (1187); Richard I of England vs. Saladin; treaty allowed Christian pilgrims
4. **Fourth (1202–1204)** — Sacked Constantinople; the greatest Christian atrocity against Christians; permanent wound in Catholic-Orthodox relations
5. **Fifth through Ninth** — Increasingly futile; the last Crusader state (Acre) fell in 1291

## The Rhineland Massacres (1096)
As the First Crusade assembled, mobs attacked Jewish communities in the Rhine Valley — Worms, Mainz, Cologne. Thousands of Jews murdered. "Why fight the enemies of Christ far away when the enemies of Christ are here among us?" The Crusades launched the first major European pogroms.

## Saladin
The Muslim commander who retook Jerusalem (1187) became more admired in medieval Europe than most Christian kings — for his chivalry, mercy to prisoners, and honorable conduct. His example embarrassed Crusader brutality.

## Why the Crusades Still Matter
- Catholic-Orthodox relations: the Fourth Crusade's sack of Constantinople (1204) is unhealed
- Christian-Muslim relations: "Crusade" remains a live and toxic word in the Islamic world
- Christian-Jewish relations: the Crusades began the pattern of pogroms that ended in the Holocaust
- Theology of holy war: justified by Augustine's just war theory; the first case of papal authority directing military force

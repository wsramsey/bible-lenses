# The Investiture Controversy (1076–1122 AD)
*Who appoints bishops — the pope or the king?*

## The Issue
**Lay investiture**: kings and emperors had been appointing bishops and abbots (investing them with the symbols of their office). This gave secular rulers enormous power over the church — and enormous revenue, since church offices were often sold (*simony*).

## Pope Gregory VII's Diktat
Gregory VII's *Dictatus Papae* (1075): 27 radical propositions including:
- "The Roman pontiff alone can depose or reinstate bishops"
- "He may depose emperors"
- "He may absolve subjects of unjust men from their allegiance"

## Canossa (1077)
Emperor Henry IV was excommunicated. His subjects were released from their oaths of allegiance. Facing rebellion, Henry walked barefoot in the snow to the castle at Canossa in northern Italy where Gregory was staying — standing three days in penitent's garb until Gregory lifted the excommunication.

## The Long-Term Significance
The Investiture Controversy forced the separation of spiritual and temporal authority — which paradoxically eventually produced the secular state. When pope and emperor fought, a space opened between them where other authorities (law, universities, cities, merchants) could operate.

Harold Berman (*Law and Revolution*) argues modern Western law begins here.

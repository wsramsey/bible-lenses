# Benedict's Rule and Western Monasticism (c. 529 AD)
*The institution that saved Western civilization*

## The Rule
[[Benedict of Nursia]] (480–550) wrote a *Rule* for his monastery at Monte Cassino — 73 chapters organizing monastic life around **ora et labora** (pray and work). Balanced, humane, brilliantly practical. Became the standard for all Western monasticism.

## What Monasteries Did
For 500 years (600–1100), Benedictine monasteries were the primary institutions of Western civilization:
- **Preserved manuscripts** — copied classical texts; without monasteries, most of Greek and Roman literature would be lost
- **Educated** — the only schools in many regions
- **Farmed** — Cistercians in particular were agricultural innovators; they cleared forests, drained swamps, introduced new crops
- **Hospitality** — "receive every guest as Christ" (Rule ch. 53); the medieval hospital system
- **Economic** — monasteries were the most productive economic units of the early medieval world

## The Irony
Benedict intended to flee the world; his followers transformed it.

# First Council of Nicaea (325 AD)
*The council that defined the Trinity — and why it still matters*

## The Crisis: Arianism
**Arius** (c. 256–336), a priest in Alexandria, taught: the Son was the first and greatest creation of the Father — "there was a time when he was not." God the Father alone is truly eternal and divine; the Son is *homoiousios* (of similar substance) but not *homoousios* (of the same substance).

**Alexander and Athanasius** responded: If the Son is not fully God, our salvation is not real. Only God can save us; if Jesus is a lesser divine being, we are not saved.

## The Council
~300 bishops gathered at Constantine's invitation. After debate, **Nicaea condemned Arianism** and affirmed that the Son is *homoousios* (consubstantial, of the same substance) with the Father. The Nicene Creed was produced:

> "We believe in one God, the Father Almighty... and in one Lord Jesus Christ, the only-begotten Son of God, begotten from the Father before all ages, Light from Light, true God from true God, begotten not made, consubstantial (*homoousios*) with the Father..."

## Why It Matters
- **Theological:** Defines the core of Trinitarian Christianity; if Jesus is fully God, the Incarnation and Atonement mean what Christians claim
- **Political:** Sets the precedent that emperors call councils; church and state intertwined for 1,600 years
- **Ongoing:** The Nicene Creed is recited weekly in Catholic, Orthodox, Anglican, Lutheran, and many other churches; it remains the common ground of Christian orthodoxy

## The Aftermath
Arianism didn't die — it kept resurging. Athanasius was exiled five times. "*Athanasius contra mundum*" — Athanasius against the world.

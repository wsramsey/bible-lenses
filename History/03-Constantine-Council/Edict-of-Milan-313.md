# Edict of Milan (313 AD)
*Christianity becomes legal — everything changes*

## What Happened
Constantine and Licinius met in Milan and agreed to grant religious toleration to all — specifically including Christians. Confiscated church property was to be returned.

## Before and After
**Before:** Christianity illegal; owning Scriptures criminal; worship punishable by death
**After:** Legal, then favored, then (under Theodosius, 380) the official state religion

## The Debate: Liberation or Corruption?
- **The positive view:** The church needed legal space; Constantine's patronage enabled the great councils, the building of churches, the preservation of Scripture
- **The Yoder/Hauerwas critique:** "Constantinianism" — the church's identification with state power — was the original sin of institutional Christianity; the persecuted church was more authentic
- **The nuanced view:** Neither pure gain nor pure loss; the church gained power and lost something of its witness; the tension was never resolved

## Immediate Consequences
- Church buildings could be built openly
- Sunday declared a day of rest
- Clergy exempted from civic duties
- Constantine called the Council of Nicaea (325)

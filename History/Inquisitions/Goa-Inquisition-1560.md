# The Goa Inquisition (1560–1812)
*Colonialism and inquisition — the worst combination*

## Context: Francis Xavier
Francis Xavier arrived in Goa in 1542 — 18 years before the Inquisition was established. He wrote to the King of Portugal requesting an inquisition to deal with New Christians he believed were relapsing. He died in 1552, before it was established.

The Inquisition is sometimes blamed on Xavier; the reality is he advocated for it but it was established after his death and was primarily a response to different problems.

## What It Targeted
1. **New Christians from Portugal** — converso families who had settled in Goa; suspected of crypto-Jewish practice
2. **Indian converts** who maintained traditional Hindu, Muslim, or other practices alongside Christianity
3. **"Hindu" customs** classified as idolatry: keeping idols, observing Hindu festivals, wearing a sacred thread, certain marriage customs

## The Methods
Same procedure as the Portuguese Inquisition:
- Informers throughout the colony
- Secret denunciations
- Torture
- Auto-da-fé (public sentencing ceremonies)
- Burning at the stake for the condemned

**The special colonial context**: In Goa, the Inquisition had even less external oversight than in Portugal; the social power over converts was enormous; the line between genuine heresy and traditional cultural practice was often arbitrary.

## The Numbers (Disputed)
Records are incomplete. Estimates of executions range from a few hundred to several thousand. Tens of thousands of trials are confirmed.

**Voltaire** (*Candide*, 1759): used the Goa Inquisition as a symbol of religious violence; helped establish its reputation in the European Enlightenment as a byword for colonial brutality.

## The Prohibition of Hindu Practices
The Goa Inquisition went beyond heresy into cultural destruction:
- Hindu temples destroyed
- Wearing sacred threads prohibited
- Hindu marriage rites prohibited
- Cultivating certain plants associated with Hindu ritual prohibited
- Eventually: simply being suspected of Hindu sympathies was sufficient for investigation

## The Legacy
The Goa Inquisition is the most significant example of the intersection of colonialism and inquisitorial power. It was used not only to police religious orthodoxy but to enforce cultural conversion — the transformation of Indian Christians into European-style Catholics.

Its memory remains vivid in Goa. It is taught in Indian schools as an example of Portuguese colonial brutality.

# The Papal (Medieval) Inquisition (1231–c.1400)
*The systematic Inquisition — friars as professional heresy-hunters*

## Foundation
Pope Gregory IX's *Excommunicamus* (1231) established the Papal Inquisition proper — removing the investigation of heresy from local bishops and entrusting it to Dominican friars as direct papal agents. The Dominicans (*Order of Preachers*, founded 1216 to combat heresy through preaching) were the primary operators; Franciscans also participated.

## Key Features

**The Inquisitors**
Dominican and Franciscan friars trained in theology and law. They traveled in pairs. Their authority was directly from the pope, overriding local episcopal jurisdiction.

**The Procedure** (fully developed by Bernard Gui, c. 1320)
1. Arrival in a town → public announcement of investigation
2. **Grace period** (30 days): voluntary confession = lighter punishment
3. Denunciation: anyone could accuse; accusations were anonymous
4. Summons: the accused had no right to know the charges or their accusers
5. Interrogation: recorded by notaries; suspects questioned repeatedly
6. Torture: permitted from 1252 (*Ad extirpanda*, Innocent IV); could only be applied once but "continued" sessions were common; no blood-shedding (so strappado — suspension by arms tied behind back — was favored)
7. Sentence at the **auto-da-fé**: penance, imprisonment, confiscation of property, or
8. "Relaxation to the secular arm" — handed to civil authority for execution by burning

**The Manuals**
- *Practica Inquisitionis* (Bernard Gui, c. 1323) — the most complete medieval manual
- *Directorium Inquisitorum* (Nicholas Eymerich, 1376) — systematized procedure; widely used

## Major Targets

**Cathars** — the primary target in southern France until the movement was effectively destroyed by the 1290s through a combination of crusade, inquisition, and social pressure

**Waldensians** — the survivors went underground; some communities survived in the Alps; the Waldensians eventually aligned with the Reformation (1532)

**Spiritual Franciscans** — Franciscans who insisted on absolute poverty; condemned under John XXII; some burned as heretics

**Beguines** — lay women's movement; condemned for Quietist theology

**Knights Templar** — technically not a heresy trial; but Inquisition methods used; 1307–1312; burned under Philip IV of France's pressure

**Jews and Magic** — Jews were technically outside the Inquisition's jurisdiction (it prosecuted baptized Christians only) but were often caught up in proceedings

## The Most Notorious: Conrad of Marburg
Conrad of Marburg (d. 1233) — the first papal inquisitor north of the Alps in Germany. His methods were so brutal that he was assassinated by German nobles in 1233. Pope Gregory IX expressed regret.

## The Numbers
Historians estimate a few hundred executions over the life of the medieval Papal Inquisition — far fewer than the Black Legend suggests, but each one represented a terrible failure of Christian witness.

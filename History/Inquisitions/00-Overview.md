# The Inquisitions — Complete Overview
*1184–1965 · Six centuries of institutional heresy prosecution*

> The Inquisitions are one of Christianity's most documented failures — institutional violence in the name of truth. An honest account requires neither minimization nor exaggeration. The numbers were smaller than legend claims; the principle was indefensible.

## The Five Major Inquisitions

| Inquisition | Period | Operated By | Primary Targets |
|-------------|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| [[Episcopal Inquisition]] | 1184–1230s | Local bishops | Cathars, Waldensians |
| [[Papal (Medieval) Inquisition]] | 1231–c.1400 | Dominican/Franciscan friars | Cathars, Waldensians, heretics |
| [[Spanish Inquisition]] | 1478–1834 | Spanish Crown (not Rome) | Conversos, Moriscos, Protestants |
| [[Portuguese Inquisition]] | 1536–1821 | Portuguese Crown | Conversos, heretics |
| [[Roman Inquisition]] | 1542–1965 | Holy Office (Vatican) | Protestants, Galileo, modernists |
| [[Goa Inquisition]] | 1560–1812 | Portuguese colonial | Indian converts, crypto-Jews |

## What All Inquisitions Had in Common
1. **Inquisitor** — a trained theologian with legal authority to investigate heresy
2. **Denunciation** — anonymous accusations accepted
3. **The grace period** — time to voluntarily confess before formal proceedings
4. **Interrogation** — with torture permitted after 1252 (Innocent IV, *Ad extirpanda*)
5. **The sentence** — ranging from penance to imprisonment to "relaxation to the secular arm" (execution by burning)
6. **The auto-da-fé** — public ceremony of sentencing and sometimes execution

## The Numbers (Historians' Estimates)
- **Medieval Inquisition**: hundreds executed over 200 years
- **Spanish Inquisition**: 3,000–5,000 executions over 356 years (not millions as legend claims)
- **Portuguese Inquisition**: approximately 1,175 executions confirmed
- **Roman Inquisition**: dozens of executions; hundreds imprisoned
- **Goa Inquisition**: thousands of trials; exact execution numbers disputed

**Historical note**: Inquisitorial courts were actually more procedurally fair than contemporary secular courts — they required two witnesses, allowed appeals, had rules of evidence. This does not make them just. It contextualizes them.

## The Theological Justification
**Augustine of Hippo** — *compelle intrare* ("compel them to come in," Luke 14:23): used to justify coercion of Donatists; the first Christian theological endorsement of religious coercion
**Thomas Aquinas** — heresy is worse than counterfeiting; the state should execute relapsed heretics; the church hands them over without endorsing the death penalty *per se*
**The medieval synthesis**: the church is responsible for souls; heresy endangers souls; protecting souls justifies temporal coercion

## The Condemnations
- **Pope John Paul II** — *Day of Pardon* (March 12, 2000): asked forgiveness for "the use of violence that some have committed in the service of the truth"
- **Vatican Commission (2004)** — released a 800-page scholarly study: acknowledged genuine abuses while providing historical context
- **Individual condemnations**: by virtually every modern Christian theologian

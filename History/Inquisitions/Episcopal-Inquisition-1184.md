# The Episcopal Inquisition (1184–1230s)
*The first Inquisition — bishops as inquisitors*

## Origin
Pope Lucius III and Emperor Frederick I Barbarossa issued *Ad abolendam* (1184) — the founding document of the Inquisition. Required bishops to visit their dioceses regularly and investigate heresy. Those found guilty were handed to secular authorities.

## Why It Was Created
Southern France (Languedoc) was saturated with **Catharism** — a dualist heresy teaching that the material world is evil, created by a wicked demiurge; the true God is purely spiritual. The Cathars (also called *Albigensians*) rejected the sacraments, the Incarnation, and the authority of the Roman church. They had their own clergy (*perfecti*) and lay followers (*credentes*).

The **Waldensians** were a poverty movement founded by Peter Waldo of Lyon (c. 1170) — not theologically heretical but practiced lay preaching without papal authorization. Initially not targeted as heretics; eventually condemned.

## How It Worked
- Bishops were required to investigate their own dioceses
- Suspects could be named by two witnesses
- Penalties: public penance, fines, imprisonment, exile
- Execution: handed to secular authorities; the church "did not shed blood" (technically)

## Its Failure
The episcopal inquisition was largely ineffective. Bishops were often busy, locally compromised, or reluctant. The Cathar movement continued to grow. This failure led to the papal inquisition.

## The Albigensian Crusade (1209–1229)
Pope Innocent III declared a military crusade against the Cathars. The results were catastrophic:
- The massacre of Béziers (1209): the papal legate allegedly said "Kill them all; God will know his own" — whether he said this exactly is disputed; the massacre happened
- An estimated 200,000–1,000,000 deaths over 20 years
- The destruction of Occitan culture
- This was not the Inquisition per se but preceded and enabled it

# The Theological Justification for the Inquisitions
*How Christian theologians argued that burning heretics honored God*

## The Building Blocks

### Augustine: *Compelle Intrare* (c. 417)
Augustine's treatment of the Donatists in North Africa was the founding theological text for religious coercion.

The Donatists had separated from the Catholic Church; Augustine initially opposed force. After the Donatists committed violence against Catholics, he changed his position.

**His argument**:
1. Luke 14:23 — the master tells the servant: "Go out into the roads and country lanes and *compel* them to come in" (*compelle intrare*)
2. Love sometimes requires coercion for the sinner's own good — as a doctor restrains a patient to prevent self-harm
3. The goal is not punishment but correction and return to the church
4. Better to suffer temporal coercion than eternal damnation

**Why this was dangerous**: it created the template for all subsequent religious coercion. The intention was pastoral; the effect was to justify violence.

### Thomas Aquinas: Heresy as Worse than Counterfeiting (c. 1265)
*Summa Theologiae* II-II, Q. 11, Art. 3:

> "With regard to heretics two points must be observed: one, on their own side; the other, on the side of the Church. On their own side there is the sin, whereby they deserve not only to be separated from the Church by excommunication, but also to be severed from the world by death. For it is a much graver matter to corrupt the faith which quickens the soul, than to forge money, which supports temporal life. Wherefore if forgers of money and other evil-doers are forthwith condemned to death by the secular authority, much more reason is there for heretics, as soon as they are convicted of heresy, to be not only excommunicated but even put to death."

**The logic**:
1. Heresy corrupts souls — the most serious possible harm
2. The state executes counterfeiters (lesser offense)
3. Therefore the state should execute heretics (greater offense)
4. The church excommunicates; the state executes; the church "does not shed blood"

**The distinction**: the church only excommunicates and hands over to the secular arm; it doesn't formally execute. This distinction was always theologically strained.

### The Inquisitors' Manual Perspective (Bernard Gui, c. 1323)
The practical theology of the professional inquisitor:
- Heresy is a contagious spiritual disease; the infected must be isolated
- Mercy to the unrepentant heretic is cruelty to the thousands they will infect
- Execution is the last resort, but a genuine one; the heretic who refuses to recant chooses death
- The auto-da-fé is an act of mercy — a last chance to recant before the sentence is carried out

## The Counter-Arguments (Made at the Time)

**Erasmus** — coercion produces only outward compliance, not genuine faith; the methods of Christ were persuasion and example, not the sword

**Albigensian Crusade critics** — including Alcuin of York (against Charlemagne's forced baptisms): "Faith is a free act of the will; you can baptize a body but not a soul"

**Sebastian Castellio** — *Concerning Heretics* (1554, response to Calvin's execution of Servetus): "To burn a man alive does not defend a doctrine — it burns a man"; the most powerful contemporary response

## The Modern Assessment

**Pope John Paul II** — *Day of Pardon* (March 12, 2000): acknowledged "the use of violence that some have committed in the service of the truth" and asked forgiveness. He did not specifically repudiate Augustine's or Aquinas's arguments, but the gesture was clear.

**[[Thomas Aquinas]] — the tragedy**: His argument for executing heretics has been universally rejected by contemporary Catholic theologians. His logic was internally coherent given his premises (heresy as the gravest harm); but the premises were wrong — religious coercion does not produce genuine faith; the church's role is proclamation, not compulsion; *Dignitatis Humanae* (Vatican II) declared the right to religious liberty for all persons.

**[[Karl Barth]]** — the Inquisition was the inevitable result of Constantinianism; when the church identifies with political power, it will use political means; the church's only weapon is the Word

## The Definitive Catholic Reversal: *Dignitatis Humanae* (1965)
Vatican II's Declaration on Religious Liberty declared:
> "The human person has a right to religious freedom... No one is to be forced to act in a manner contrary to his own beliefs, whether privately or publicly, whether alone or in association with others."

This is the direct contradiction of the medieval theology that justified the Inquisitions. The Catholic Church took 700 years to reach this reversal.

# The Spanish Inquisition (1478–1834)
*The most infamous Inquisition — and the most misunderstood*

## Origin
Pope Sixtus IV authorized the Spanish Inquisition at the request of Ferdinand and Isabella in *Exigit sinceras devotionis* (1478). Unlike the Medieval Inquisition, it was under royal — not papal — control. Rome had limited ability to restrain it.

**The specific problem it was created to address**: The *conversos* — Jews who had converted to Christianity, often under duress during the pogroms of 1391 and after. Suspicion was widespread that many *conversos* secretly practiced Judaism (*crypto-Judaism*). The Inquisition was tasked with investigating the sincerity of these conversions.

## Tomás de Torquemada (1483–1498)
The first and most infamous Inquisitor General. A Dominican friar, confessor to Isabella. His *Instrucciones* codified Spanish inquisitorial procedure. Under his direction, the Inquisition burned approximately 2,000 people — the majority of all Spanish executions. His name became synonymous with fanatical persecution.

Torquemada was himself of converso ancestry — his grandmother was Jewish.

## The Procedure
The Spanish Inquisition developed a more systematic procedure than the medieval model:

1. **Edict of Grace**: public proclamation; 30–40 days for voluntary confession; lighter penalties
2. **Edict of Faith**: list of heretical acts; neighbors encouraged to denounce
3. **Secret trial**: accused not told accusers' names; could not confront witnesses
4. **Torture**: *potro* (the rack), *garrucha* (strappado), *toca* (water torture / waterboarding)
5. **Sentence at auto-da-fé**:
   - Reconciliation: penances, confiscation, public humiliation
   - Relaxation in effigy (if escaped or dead): burned in effigy
   - Relaxation in person: handed to secular arm for burning alive (unrepentant) or strangling first then burning (repentant)

## The Auto-da-Fé
The "act of faith" — the public ceremony of sentencing. Not the burning itself (which happened after, outside). A major civic and religious event:
- Procession with penitents in distinctive robes (*sambenito*)
- Mass reading of sentences
- Public humiliation was part of the punishment
- Major auto-da-fés drew thousands of spectators
- The last auto-da-fé in Spain was in 1826

## Major Phases

**The Converso Phase (1478–c.1530)**
Primary target: Jewish *conversos* suspected of crypto-Judaism. After the expulsion of Jews from Spain in 1492, the targets shifted to *conversos* who may have been influenced by Jewish relatives before the expulsion.

**The Morisco Phase (1502–1614)**
Muslims were given the choice of conversion or expulsion in 1502. The resulting *Moriscos* (Muslim converts) were subsequently investigated for crypto-Islam. Eventually 300,000 Moriscos were expelled from Spain (1609–1614) — one of the largest ethnic cleansings in early modern history.

**The Protestant Phase (1558–c.1600)**
When Lutheranism reached Spain, the Inquisition acted swiftly. Protestant communities in Seville and Valladolid were destroyed in 1558–1559. Protestantism never gained a foothold in Spain.

**The Decline (1600–1834)**
Gradually the Inquisition focused on lesser matters: bigamy, blasphemy, solicitation in the confessional, Enlightenment ideas. The last execution for heresy was in 1826. The Inquisition was suppressed in 1834.

## The Black Legend vs. Historical Reality

**The Black Legend**: Millions killed; systematic torture; the embodiment of fanatical religious evil. Promoted by Protestant propagandists in the 16th century; reinforced by the *leyenda negra*.

**Historical reality**:
- Approximately 3,000–5,000 executions over 356 years — terrible but far fewer than the legend
- Inquisitorial courts were actually more procedurally careful than secular courts of the period
- The Spanish secular courts executed far more people
- But: torture was systematic; anonymous accusation created terror; the *sambenito* hanging in churches for generations destroyed families; the psychological and social damage was enormous

**What can't be minimized**:
- The terror created by anonymous denunciation
- The use of torture
- The destruction of converso communities and culture
- The expulsion of Jews (1492) and Moriscos (1609) — ethnic cleansing
- The intellectual suppression (the Index of Forbidden Books)

## What the Scholars Say
**[[Henry Charles Lea]]** — *A History of the Inquisition in Spain* (4 vols., 1906–1907): the foundational scholarly history; Protestant perspective but rigorously documented

**[[Henry Kamen]]** — *The Spanish Inquisition: A Historical Revision* (1997): the most important recent scholarly reassessment; significantly lowers the death toll estimates; contextualizes within the period

**[[Benzion Netanyahu]]** — *The Origins of the Inquisition in Fifteenth Century Spain* (1995): argues the Inquisition was primarily a racial/ethnic persecution, not a religious one; the *conversos* were largely sincere Christians

**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — his theological framework justified the procedure: heresy endangers souls; the state should execute relapsed heretics; the church "does not shed blood" by handing them over

# The Cathars and Waldensians
*The movements that triggered the Inquisition*

## The Cathars (Albigensians)

### What They Believed
Catharism was a dualist religion — not simply a reform movement but a genuinely different religion that used Christian language:

- **Two principles**: a good God (spiritual) and an evil God (material); the material world is a prison created by the evil God
- **The soul**: a spiritual being trapped in matter; reincarnation until final liberation
- **Jesus**: a purely spiritual being; the Incarnation was an illusion (*docetism*)
- **The sacraments**: meaningless; the Roman church is the church of Satan
- **The perfecti**: the Cathar "clergy" who had received the *consolamentum* (their sole sacrament); lived in radical poverty, celibacy, and vegetarianism
- **The credentes**: the mass of Cathar believers who supported the perfecti; could receive the consolamentum on their deathbed

### Why the Church Found It Threatening
Catharism was genuinely popular in Languedoc (southern France). It offered a coherent worldview, a pure clergy in contrast to Catholic corruption, and radical simplicity. By 1200 it may have been the majority religion in parts of Languedoc.

### The Albigensian Crusade (1209–1229)
Pope Innocent III declared a crusade. Northern French knights invaded southern France.

**The massacre of Béziers (1209)**: The crusaders asked Arnaud Amalric, the papal legate, how to distinguish Cathars from Catholics. His alleged reply: "Kill them all; God will know his own" (*Caedite eos. Novit enim Dominus qui sunt eius*). Whether he said this exactly is disputed. The massacre happened — perhaps 20,000 killed.

The crusade destroyed Occitan culture and civilization. The troubadour tradition, the most sophisticated courtly culture in Europe, was extinguished.

### The Inquisition's Work
After the crusade, the Inquisition systematically hunted the remaining Cathars. The last known Cathar perfectus, Guilhem Bélibaste, was burned in 1321.

## The Waldensians

### What They Were
Peter Waldo (Valdès) of Lyon — a wealthy merchant who gave away his fortune c. 1173, commissioned a vernacular translation of the Gospels, and began preaching poverty and the Gospel as a lay movement.

The Waldensians were not originally heretical — they were a reform movement. Pope Alexander III initially approved their vows of poverty. They were condemned only when they refused to stop preaching without episcopal authorization.

### What Made Them Targets
- **Lay preaching**: unauthorized; reserved to clergy
- **Vernacular Scripture**: threatening to clerical Latin monopoly
- **Anti-clerical critique**: they condemned masses for the dead, purgatory, and prayers to saints
- **The two-witness rule**: they claimed to need no sacraments other than those of the community
- **Gradually more radical**: over generations, the Waldensians developed their own theology and rejected more Roman practices

### Survival
Unlike the Cathars, the Waldensians survived. Alpine communities in Piedmont (Italy) and southeastern France maintained the movement. In 1532 they formally allied with the Protestant Reformation. Their descendants, the *Chiesa Evangelica Valdese* (Waldensian Evangelical Church), still exists in Italy with ~20,000 members.

# Key Individual Cases of the Inquisition

## Galileo Galilei (1633)
→ See [[Roman Inquisition]] for full account
**Verdict**: Vehemently suspected of heresy; forced to recant; house arrest
**What it was about**: Heliocentrism + insulting the pope + violating 1616 injunction
**The rehabilitation**: John Paul II, 1992

## Giordano Bruno (1600)
→ See [[Roman Inquisition]] for full account
**Verdict**: Burned alive in Rome
**What it was about**: Denial of Trinity, Incarnation, transubstantiation; possible pantheism
**Not primarily about**: Science (though he was a Copernican)

## Joan of Arc (1431)
The Inquisition-adjacent trial of Joan:
- Tried by a French ecclesiastical court aligned with the English
- Charges: witchcraft, heresy (wearing male clothing), relapse
- Burned at the stake in Rouen, May 30, 1431; she was 19
- **Rehabilitation**: 25 years later (1456) the trial was declared null
- **Canonized**: 1920 by Benedict XV
- **Historical note**: her trial was politically motivated; the English wanted her discredited; it was conducted by French collaborators

## Jan Hus (1415)
→ See [[Jan Hus Burned at Constance (1415)]] for full account
- Council of Constance; burned July 6, 1415
- Not the Inquisition per se but a conciliar trial using inquisitorial procedures

## Thomas Cranmer (1556)
- Archbishop of Canterbury under Henry VIII and Edward VI
- Tried under Mary I's restoration of Catholicism
- Recanted under pressure; then recanted his recantation at the stake
- Famously thrust his right hand into the flames first — "the unworthy hand" that had signed the recantation
- Not technically the Inquisition but ecclesiastical trial

## Michael Servetus (1553)
- Spanish physician; denied the Trinity
- Fled the Catholic Inquisition — captured in Geneva instead
- Burned by Calvin's Geneva
- **The irony**: Servetus escaped the Catholic Inquisition only to be burned by Protestants
- Demonstrates that the problem of coercion for heresy was not uniquely Catholic

## Meister Eckhart (1329)
- Dominican mystic; posthumously condemned by Pope John XXII
- 28 propositions declared heretical or suspect
- Eckhart had died before the condemnation was published
- Modern assessment: largely rehabilitated; his language was extreme but not heretical in intent

## The Templars (1307–1312)
- Knights Templar arrested by Philip IV of France on October 13, 1307 (Friday the 13th — origin of the superstition)
- Charges: heresy, sodomy, idol worship (*Baphomet*)
- Torture produced confessions later recanted
- Grand Master Jacques de Molay burned 1314; allegedly cursed Philip IV and Pope Clement V from the stake (both died within a year)
- Modern assessment: almost certainly politically motivated; the charges were fabricated

# The Pelagian Controversy (410–431 AD)
*Grace vs. free will — the debate that never ended*

## The Positions
**Pelagius** (British monk, active in Rome): 
- Humans have genuine free will
- Sin is imitation of Adam, not inherited corruption
- Grace helps but is not strictly necessary — humans can choose God without it
- God would not command the impossible (e.g., "be perfect")

**Augustine**:
- Adam's sin corrupted human nature (*original sin*)
- The will is in bondage — it cannot choose God without prevenient grace
- Grace is not assistance but transformation — God must change the will before it can choose God
- Predestination — God's election is not based on foreseen merit

## The Outcome
Pelagianism was condemned at the Council of Carthage (418) and the Council of Ephesus (431). Augustine's framework became dominant in the West.

## Why It Never Ended
The tension between grace and free will reappears in every generation:
- **Semi-Pelagianism** — humans initiate, God assists; condemned at Orange (529)
- **Lutheranism vs. Erasmus** (16th c.)
- **Calvinism vs. Arminianism** (17th c.)
- **Molinism** (Jesuit middle knowledge)
- Every evangelical debate about altar calls, decisional regeneration

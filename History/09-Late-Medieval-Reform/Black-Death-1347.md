# The Black Death (1347–1351 AD)
*One-third of Europe dies — Christianity confronts mass death*

## The Scale
The bubonic plague killed an estimated 30–60% of Europe's population in four years — perhaps 50 million people. Some regions lost 80%. The scale of death was without precedent in Western history.

## The Theological Crisis
- **Why did God allow this?** — The dominant response: divine punishment for sin; repent immediately
- **Flagellants** — penitential movements where participants publicly beat themselves with chains; popular; condemned by Clement VI
- **Anti-Jewish pogroms** — Jews blamed for poisoning wells; massacred across Germany and France; Pope Clement VI issued two bulls condemning the persecutions (and was largely ignored)
- **Clergy died too** — undermined the idea that the church's prayers could protect

## The Spiritual Fruit
The Black Death intensified mysticism — if death could come at any moment, the interior life became urgent:
- [[Meister Eckhart]] (died 1328, just before)
- [[Johannes Tauler]] — preached during the plague; deeply shaped by it
- [[Catherine of Siena]] — nursed plague victims
- [[Julian of Norwich]] — received her visions (1373) in the context of surviving plague
- *Ars Moriendi* (Art of Dying) — a hugely popular genre of how to die well

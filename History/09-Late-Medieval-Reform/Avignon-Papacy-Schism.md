# The Avignon Papacy and Great Western Schism (1309–1417)
*Two popes — then three popes — at once*

## The Avignon Papacy (1309–1377)
The papacy moved to Avignon, France under French political pressure. Seven French popes reigned from Avignon. Critics called it the "Babylonian Captivity of the Church."

**[[Catherine of Siena]]** famously wrote to Pope Gregory XI calling him "Babbo mio" ("my daddy") and demanding he return to Rome. He did, in 1377.

## The Great Western Schism (1378–1417)
Gregory XI returned but died; the election of Urban VI went badly; the cardinals fled and elected a rival pope (Clement VII) in Avignon. For 40 years, two (then three) men claimed to be the true pope.

- Catholic states split: France, Scotland, Aragon → Avignon; England, Germany, Italy → Rome
- No one knew which pope's sacraments were valid
- The scandal destroyed papal prestige

## Resolution: Council of Constance (1414–1418)
Conciliarism — the theory that a general council has authority over the pope — resolved the schism by deposing all three claimants and electing Martin V. It also burned [[Jan Hus]] (1415).

## Why It Matters
The schism demonstrated that institutional Christianity could fail catastrophically. It created the intellectual context for the Reformation — if the papacy could be so corrupt, maybe it wasn't the foundation Christ intended.

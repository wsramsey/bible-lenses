# The Second Great Awakening (1790s–1840s)
*The revival that reformed America*

## What It Was
A wave of Protestant revivals across America — camp meetings on the frontier (Cane Ridge, 1801), urban revivals in the cities ([[Charles Finney]]). More optimistic theology than Edwards: humans can choose God; revival can be engineered with the right "new measures."

## The Social Reforms It Produced
The Second Great Awakening was the engine of virtually every 19th-century American reform movement:
- **Abolition** — the abolitionist movement was almost entirely evangelical; Finney, the Tappan brothers, the underground railroad
- **Temperance** — the WCTU (Women's Christian Temperance Union); Prohibition (18th Amendment)
- **Women's rights** — women preached at revivals; became organizers; the Seneca Falls Convention (1848) grew from this
- **Prison reform** — Charles Finney
- **Education** — Oberlin College (first coed, racially integrated); many Christian colleges founded

## Finney's "New Measures"
Finney introduced techniques that are still standard: the "anxious bench" (precursor to the altar call), protracted meetings (multi-night revivals), praying for people by name in public prayer. Jonathan Edwards's heirs were horrified. These practices became normative in American evangelicalism.

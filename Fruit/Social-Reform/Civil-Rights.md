# The Civil Rights Movement
*A Christian movement from beginning to end*

> **The claim:** The American Civil Rights Movement was not merely supported by Christians — it was a Christian movement in its leadership, its rhetoric, its theology, its organizational structure, and its moral logic.

## The Evidence
- **Martin Luther King Jr.** — a Baptist pastor whose "Letter from Birmingham Jail" cites [[Augustine]], [[Thomas Aquinas]], Martin Buber, and Paul Tillich; his theology of nonviolent resistance was explicitly drawn from the Sermon on the Mount via Gandhi
- **The Black Church** — churches were the organizational base; mass meetings happened in churches; the SCLC (Southern Christian Leadership Conference) was a network of Black pastors
- **"We Shall Overcome"** — adapted from a gospel hymn ("I'll Overcome Someday" by Charles Albert Tindley, 1900)
- **The March on Washington** — organized from churches; opened with prayer; speakers were overwhelmingly clergy
- **The moral argument** — the movement's power came from its moral authority; the argument was that segregation violated the *imago Dei* and the Fatherhood of God

## The Complication
The Christians on the other side:
- White Southern churches overwhelmingly supported segregation
- The "Letter from Birmingham Jail" was addressed to *white moderate clergy* who told King to wait
- Bob Jones University didn't admit Black students until 1971; banned interracial dating until 2000
- The Religious Right (1970s) was partly organized around opposition to the IRS revoking tax-exempt status for segregated Christian schools

# Sacred Architecture
*The greatest buildings in Western civilization were churches*

> **The claim:** Christian architecture is the most ambitious building program in human history. For 1,500 years, the tallest, most technically sophisticated, and most beautiful buildings in Europe were churches.

## The Timeline
1. **Basilicas (4th c.)** — Constantine repurposed Roman civic architecture for worship; Old St. Peter's (326); the basilica form became the template
2. **Hagia Sophia (537)** — Justinian's masterpiece; the largest enclosed space in the world for 1,000 years; a theology in stone
3. **Romanesque (10th–12th c.)** — massive, fortress-like churches; thick walls, round arches; Cluny III was the largest church in the world
4. **Gothic (12th–16th c.)** — the pointed arch, flying buttress, ribbed vault; walls of light; Chartres, Notre-Dame, Cologne — theology expressed as structure; Abbot Suger of Saint-Denis said light was the vehicle of divine presence
5. **Renaissance (15th–17th c.)** — Brunelleschi's dome (Florence); Michelangelo's St. Peter's; Bernini's colonnade
6. **Baroque (17th–18th c.)** — Counter-Reformation spectacle; Bernini, Borromini; overwhelming sensory experience as apologetics
7. **Protestant simplicity** — Calvinist whitewashing; the meetinghouse; Wren's London churches after the Great Fire; Quaker simplicity

## By Tradition
- **Catholic** — cathedrals, basilicas, Baroque splendor
- **Orthodox** — domes, icons, the iconostasis; the building *is* heaven on earth
- **Protestant** — stripped altars, central pulpit, clear glass; the Word over the image
- **Megachurch** — the amphitheater model; worship as performance; theological implications of architectural choices

## Why It Matters for the Vault
Architecture is theology you can walk into. The Gothic cathedral teaches Platonic light metaphysics. The Quaker meetinghouse teaches equality before God. The Orthodox church teaches the icon theology of the seventh ecumenical council. The megachurch teaches... something about entertainment and accessibility.

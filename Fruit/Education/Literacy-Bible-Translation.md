# Literacy, Bible Translation, and the Printing Press
*Christianity as the engine of mass literacy*

> **The claim:** The drive to translate the Bible into every language — and to teach people to read it — is the single greatest engine of mass literacy in human history.

## The Arc
1. **Jerome's Vulgate (405)** — translated the Bible into the common language (Latin) of the Roman Empire
2. **Missionary alphabets** — Ulfila created the Gothic alphabet (4th c.) to translate the Bible; Cyril and Methodius created the Glagolitic alphabet (9th c.) for Slavic peoples; dozens of languages first received written form specifically for Bible translation
3. **Wycliffe's English Bible (1382)** — the first complete English translation; Wycliffe's radical claim: everyone should read Scripture in their own language
4. **Gutenberg's printing press (1455)** — the first major book printed was the Bible; Gutenberg was a Catholic goldsmith; the press was the atomic bomb of the Reformation
5. **Luther's German Bible (1534)** — created modern literary German; Luther said he listened to "the mother in the home, the children on the street, the common man in the marketplace"
6. **Tyndale's English Bible (1526)** — strangled and burned for it; 90% of his translation survived into the King James Version
7. **King James Version (1611)** — the most influential work of English prose ever produced
8. **Wycliffe Bible Translators (1942)** — the organization has translated Scripture into 3,000+ languages; many of these languages had no written form until Wycliffe linguists created one

## By Tradition
- **Catholic** — invented the university, preserved manuscripts through monasteries; but also *restricted* vernacular Bible reading (Council of Toulouse, 1229; Index of Forbidden Books)
- **Protestant** — *sola scriptura* demanded mass literacy; Reformation countries had higher literacy rates; the Sunday School movement (Robert Raikes, 1780) was explicitly for literacy
- **Orthodox** — Cyrillic alphabet; liturgy in vernacular from early on

## The Numbers
- The Bible remains the most translated book in human history (3,500+ languages, full or partial)
- Summer Institute of Linguistics (Wycliffe's partner) has documented more languages than any other organization
- Protestant missionaries created the first written forms of hundreds of languages across Africa, Asia, and the Pacific

## Complications
- Catholic restriction of vernacular Bibles delayed literacy in Catholic countries
- Bible translation sometimes accompanied colonialism
- Translation choices embedded theological biases (e.g., "church" vs. "congregation")

# Christianity and Mental Health
*Mixed fruit — harm and healing both*

> **The honest picture:** Christianity has both advanced and retarded mental health care. The monastic tradition preserved sanity in insane times; the same tradition also attributed depression to the demon of *acedia*. Pastoral care anticipated psychotherapy; exorcism sometimes substituted for psychiatry.

## The Good
- **Desert Fathers** — Evagrius Ponticus (345–399) catalogued the "eight deadly thoughts" (later the seven deadly sins) — the first systematic psychology of temptation and mental disturbance; remarkably sophisticated
- **Monastic stability** — Benedictine monasteries provided structured environments that functioned as early therapeutic communities
- **Pastoral care tradition** — [[Gregory the Great]]'s *Pastoral Rule* instructed clergy to diagnose different spiritual/psychological conditions and apply different remedies; essentially the first pastoral counseling manual
- **Clinical Pastoral Education (CPE)** — Anton Boisen (1876–1965), a Protestant minister who experienced psychotic episodes, founded CPE in the 1920s — the integration of psychiatric care and pastoral ministry
- **Christian counseling movement** — [[Henri Nouwen]]'s *The Wounded Healer*; integration of psychology and spirituality

## The Bad
- **Demonic attribution** — mental illness attributed to demonic possession; exorcism as "treatment"; this persists in some Pentecostal and charismatic contexts
- **Prayer as substitute** — "You just need to pray more / have more faith"; spiritual bypassing of legitimate mental health needs
- **Stigma** — depression, anxiety, and suicidality treated as spiritual failure rather than medical conditions
- **Conversion therapy** — attempts to "cure" homosexuality through prayer and counseling; now widely condemned as harmful
- **Institutional abuse** — Magdalene laundries (Ireland), residential schools (Canada) — Christian institutions that warehoused vulnerable people in conditions that *caused* mental illness

## By Tradition
- **Catholic** — strong pastoral care tradition; also Magdalene laundries
- **Mainline Protestant** — CPE, integration of psychology; sometimes over-secularized
- **Evangelical** — growing awareness; still significant stigma in many churches
- **Pentecostal/Charismatic** — most likely to attribute mental illness to spiritual warfare

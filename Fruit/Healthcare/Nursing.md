# The Nursing Profession
*Christian origin — deaconesses to Nightingale*

> **The claim:** Professional nursing as a vocation of compassion is a Christian invention, flowing from the deaconess tradition through medieval religious orders to Florence Nightingale.

## The Arc
1. **Deaconesses (1st–5th c.)** — Phoebe (Rom 16:1); Olympias of Constantinople; organized care for the sick as a Christian ministry
2. **Medieval religious orders** — Augustinian nursing sisters; Beguines; care of lepers (Francis of Assisi); Hôtel-Dieu staffed by Augustinian canonesses
3. **Vincent de Paul + Louise de Marillac (1633)** — Daughters of Charity; first order designed specifically for active ministry outside the cloister; nursing in hospitals and homes
4. **Kaiserswerth deaconesses (1836)** — Lutheran pastor Theodor Fliedner revived the deaconess model; trained women in nursing
5. **Florence Nightingale (1854)** — trained at Kaiserswerth; professionalized nursing in the Crimean War; her *Notes on Nursing* (1859) founded the modern profession; deeply Anglican, understood nursing as sacred calling

## By Tradition
- **Catholic** — Sisters of Charity, Sisters of Mercy, Alexian Brothers (since 1334, nursing male patients); the largest global network of healthcare workers outside governments
- **Lutheran** — deaconess movement; Kaiserswerth model spread across Europe
- **Anglican** — Nightingale; the Lambeth deaconess order
- **Methodist** — deaconess tradition in America

## Complications
- Religious nursing orders sometimes prioritized spiritual care over medical best practice
- Nuns/deaconesses were unpaid labor exploited by institutional churches
- The transition from religious to professional nursing created tensions that persist

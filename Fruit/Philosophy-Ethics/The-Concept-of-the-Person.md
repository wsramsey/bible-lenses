# The Concept of the Person
*Christianity invented the philosophical concept of personhood*

> **The claim (Larry Siedentop, *Inventing the Individual*, 2014):** The Western concept of the individual person — as a unique, irreplaceable center of consciousness with inherent dignity — was not a Greek idea, not a Roman idea, but a Christian theological invention, forged in the Trinitarian and Christological debates of the 4th and 5th centuries.

## The Evidence
- **The Cappadocians** — [[Gregory of Nyssa]], [[Basil of Caesarea]], Gregory of Nazianzus; in defining the Trinity (three *hypostases*, one *ousia*), they had to define what a *hypostasis* (person) is: a unique, irreducible center of relationship and communion
- **Boethius** — *persona est naturae rationalis individua substantia* ("a person is an individual substance of a rational nature"); this definition became the standard for 1,000 years
- **Richard of St. Victor** — refined the definition: a person is a *being whose nature is communicative*; personhood is relational
- **[[Karol Wojtyła]]** — *The Acting Person*; personhood as revealed in action, relationship, and self-gift

## Why This Matters
The concept of "human rights" assumes that individual persons have inherent, non-negotiable dignity. This assumption is not self-evident — it was constructed by Christian theology and is not shared by all philosophical traditions. When the UN Declaration speaks of "the dignity of the human person," it is speaking Christian, whether it knows it or not.

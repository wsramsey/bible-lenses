# Christianity, Capitalism, and the Work Ethic
*Weber was half-right*

> **Max Weber's thesis (*The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*, 1905):** Calvinist predestination created psychological anxiety; Calvinists looked for signs of election in worldly success; this produced disciplined work, saving, and reinvestment — the "spirit" of capitalism.

## What Weber Got Right
- Calvinist cultures did develop market economies earlier
- The idea that ordinary work (not just religious work) is a divine calling (*Beruf*) was genuinely transformative — [[Martin Luther]] coined this usage
- Puritan work ethic was real and documented
- Protestant countries industrialized before Catholic ones (generally)

## What Weber Got Wrong
- **Catholic Scholastics invented market economics** — the School of Salamanca (Francisco de Vitoria, Domingo de Soto, Luis de Molina) developed the theory of just price, property rights, and monetary theory in the 16th century; capitalism's intellectual foundations are Catholic
- **Medieval monasteries were economic powerhouses** — Cistercians ran sophisticated agricultural enterprises; double-entry bookkeeping was invented by a Franciscan (Luca Pacioli, 1494)
- **Northern Italy** (Catholic) was capitalist before the Reformation
- **Usury** — both Catholic and Protestant traditions prohibited lending at interest; both eventually accommodated it; Calvin relaxed the prohibition first

## By Tradition
- **Catholic** — suspicious of unregulated capitalism; Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* (1891); Catholic Social Teaching emphasizes the common good, subsidiarity, and the universal destination of goods
- **Calvinist** — the work ethic; stewardship of creation; capitalism as potentially honoring God
- **Anabaptist/Mennonite** — communal economics; simplicity; suspicion of wealth
- **Prosperity Gospel** (Pentecostal) — God rewards the faithful with material wealth; Joel Osteen, Kenneth Copeland; most theologians consider this a heresy

## The Rotten Fruit
- Prosperity Gospel exploits the poor
- The conflation of Christianity with American capitalism
- Colonialism as economic extraction justified by Christian mission
- Evangelical support for laissez-faire economics without concern for the poor

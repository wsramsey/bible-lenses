# The Abolition of Slavery
*The greatest moral revolution in human history*

> **The claim:** The abolition movement was overwhelmingly Christian in its leadership, motivation, and rhetoric. Every major abolitionist leader was either a devout Christian or working within Christian categories. The counter-argument — that Christians also defended slavery — is true and makes the story more complex, not less Christian.

## The Abolitionists
1. **Quakers** — the first organized opposition to slavery in Western history; Philadelphia Yearly Meeting denounced slaveholding in 1758; John Woolman ([[already in vault]]) traveled for decades persuading Quakers to free enslaved people
2. **William Wilberforce (1759–1833)** — evangelical Anglican MP; spent 46 years fighting the slave trade in Parliament; succeeded 1807 (trade) and 1833 (slavery itself); explicitly motivated by his evangelical conversion
3. **The Clapham Sect** — Wilberforce's network of wealthy evangelicals; used political, economic, and cultural power to end the trade
4. **Frederick Douglass** — escaped slave; used Christian categories to condemn slavery; his "Slaveholder's Sermon" is devastating parody; distinguished between "the Christianity of Christ" and "the Christianity of this land"
5. **Harriet Beecher Stowe** — *Uncle Tom's Cabin* (1852); the most influential novel in American history; explicitly Christian argument
6. **Sojourner Truth** — "Ain't I a Woman?"; radical Christian feminist abolitionist

## The Counter-Argument
Christians also *defended* slavery:
- The "Curse of Ham" (Gen 9:25) used to justify racial slavery
- Paul's "Slaves, obey your masters" (Eph 6:5) cited literally
- Southern Baptist Convention founded in 1845 specifically to defend slaveholders' right to be missionaries
- The Catholic Church owned slaves; Pope Nicholas V's *Dum Diversas* (1452) authorized enslavement of non-Christians

## The Resolution
Holland's argument: the abolitionists won *because their argument was more Christian*, not less. The arc of the tradition bent toward freedom because the logic of *imago Dei* and Galatians 3:28 was more fundamental than the proof-texts used to defend slavery.

## By Tradition
- **Quaker** — earliest and most consistent
- **Evangelical Anglican** — Wilberforce, Clapham Sect
- **Catholic** — Las Casas (1474–1566) was the first European to argue against enslaving indigenous peoples; but the institutional church was slow; official condemnation of slavery: Gregory XVI, *In Supremo Apostolatus* (1839)
- **Southern Baptist** — didn't formally apologize for its pro-slavery founding until 1995

# Human Rights and the Dignity of the Person
*Christian origin — Tom Holland's central thesis*

> **The claim (Holland's):** The concept of universal human rights — that every person has inherent dignity and inviolable rights regardless of status, race, or power — is incomprehensible without Christianity. The pagan world had no such concept; it was Christianity that made it obvious that slaves, women, and barbarians were fully human.

## The Evidence

### The Idea
- **Imago Dei** (Gen 1:27) — every human is made in God's image; dignity is not earned but given
- **Galatians 3:28** — "There is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female" — a radical statement in the ancient world
- **Natural law** — [[Thomas Aquinas]] systematized the idea that moral norms (including human dignity) are accessible to reason and binding on all; this became the philosophical foundation of rights

### The History
1. **Early church** — opposed infanticide (common in Rome), gladiatorial games, ritual prostitution
2. **Bartolomé de las Casas (1484–1566)** — Dominican; argued before the Spanish crown that indigenous peoples were fully human with natural rights; the first human rights argument in the New World
3. **Francisco de Vitoria (1483–1546)** — Dominican; founder of international law; argued that indigenous peoples had sovereignty and could not be legitimately conquered
4. **William Penn (1644–1718)** — Quaker; Pennsylvania's "Great Law" (1682) was the most progressive legal code in the colonial world
5. **The abolitionist movement** — almost entirely Christian-led (Quakers first, then Evangelicals); Wilberforce's campaign was explicitly theological
6. **The UN Declaration of Human Rights (1948)** — [[Jacques Maritain]] (Catholic philosopher) was the key intellectual figure; Charles Malik (Lebanese Orthodox) was the rapporteur; the Declaration's language of "inherent dignity" and "inalienable rights" is Thomistic natural law in secular dress

### By Tradition
- **Catholic** — natural law tradition; Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* (1891) on workers' rights; John XXIII's *Pacem in Terris* (1963)
- **Protestant** — Reformed resistance theory (Huguenots, Scottish Covenanters, Puritans); the Bill of Rights
- **Quaker** — earliest consistent advocates of abolition, women's rights, pacifism, prison reform
- **Orthodox** — less developed rights tradition; more communitarian

### The Massive Contradiction
Christians invented the concept of universal human dignity — and then violated it systematically:
- Slavery defended biblically for 1,800 years
- The Crusades
- The Inquisition
- Colonialism and forced conversion
- The doctrine of discovery (papal bulls authorizing conquest)
- Apartheid (theologically justified by Dutch Reformed Church)
- Residential schools for indigenous children

This is not an accident but the central paradox of the Fruit folder: the tradition that invented human rights also committed some of history's worst violations of them.

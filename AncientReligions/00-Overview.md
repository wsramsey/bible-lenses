# Ancient Near Eastern Religions and the Bible
*The world Israel was born into — and what it kept, rejected, and transformed*

## The Central Question
The Bible did not fall from heaven into a cultural vacuum. It was written by people who lived in the ancient Near East, spoke its languages, shared its literary forms, and knew its stories. Understanding the similarities and differences between Israel's faith and its neighbors is essential for understanding what the Bible is actually claiming.

## The Positions

### The Conservative Position
The Bible is divinely inspired; similarities to surrounding cultures reflect either:
- Common human experience (everyone has flood memories)
- Israel's influence on its neighbors (not the reverse)
- Deliberate polemical engagement — Genesis 1 is arguing *against* the Enuma Elish, not copying it

### The Critical Position
The Bible borrowed heavily from surrounding cultures; its stories are adaptations of earlier Near Eastern myths; the "uniqueness" of Israel's religion was gradual and contested

### The Synthesis Position (most current scholarship)
The Bible *engages* ancient Near Eastern culture; it uses existing literary forms (creation myth, flood narrative, treaty structure, hymns) to make theological claims *different from* those forms. It's not borrowing without awareness — it's deliberate polemical reuse.

**[[John Walton]]** (*The Lost World of Genesis One*): Genesis 1 is not a cosmological account but a **temple inauguration text** — using the form of ancient Near Eastern temple founding narratives to say something theologically radical: *this entire cosmos is God's temple*.

## Why This Matters for Understanding the Bible
- **The Flood story** is not primitive folk memory — it is a *theological statement* about which God controls the waters and why, made using a known literary form
- **YHWH's conflict with chaos** (the sea, Leviathan, Rahab) reflects the ANE storm-god vs. sea-monster mythology — but transformed: Israel's God doesn't fight a co-equal opponent; he commands and it obeys
- **The Ten Commandments' form** is identical to ANE **suzerainty treaties** (great king to vassal) — the structure is: historical prologue → stipulations → blessings/curses; the covenant is a theological treaty
- **"Son of God"** language in ANE context referred to the divine king — understanding this illuminates what early Christians meant by calling Jesus "Son of God"

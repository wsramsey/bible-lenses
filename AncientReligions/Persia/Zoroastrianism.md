# Zoroastrianism and Its Influence on Judaism and Christianity
*The religion that may have given Judaism angels, Satan, resurrection, and the Last Judgment*

## What Zoroastrianism Is
Founded by the prophet **Zarathustra** (Greek: Zoroaster), c. 1500–1000 BC (date disputed). The first major monotheistic religion — or the first dualistic religion, depending on interpretation.

**The core theology**:
- **Ahura Mazda** ("Wise Lord"): the supreme good God; creator of all good things
- **Angra Mainyu** / **Ahriman**: the supreme evil spirit; source of all evil, falsehood, death
- These two forces are in cosmic combat; humans must choose sides
- **The Asha vs. Druj** opposition: truth/order vs. falsehood/chaos — a cosmic ethical dualism

## The Persian Empire and Jewish History
The **Achaemenid Persian Empire** (550–330 BC) was the political context for the most transformative period in Jewish religious history:

- **Cyrus the Great** (559–530 BC) conquered Babylon (539 BC) and **freed the Jewish exiles** — Isaiah 44:28; 45:1 names him "my shepherd" and "his anointed" (*meshiach* = messiah)
- **Darius I** supported the rebuilding of the Temple (Ezra 6)
- The Jews lived under Persian rule for 200 years (539–332 BC)
- **The books of Daniel, Ezra, Nehemiah, Esther** all set in the Persian period

**The contact was intimate and sustained** — enough for religious ideas to be exchanged.

## Zoroastrian Ideas That Appear in Post-Exilic Judaism

### Angels and Named Angels
Pre-exilic Hebrew: "sons of God" (divine beings); no named angels
Post-exilic Hebrew (Daniel, Zechariah): named angels — **Gabriel** (Daniel 8:16), **Michael** (Daniel 10:13)

Zoroastrian context: the **Amesha Spentas** — seven holy immortals (archangels) of Ahura Mazda, each with a name and function. This is the first developed angelic hierarchy in ancient religion.

### Satan as a Distinct Evil Being
Pre-exilic: **ha-Satan** (the accuser) is a member of the divine council, not an independent evil force (Job 1–2)
Post-exilic: Satan as a cosmic adversary of God's purposes

Zoroastrian context: **Angra Mainyu** — the supreme evil being in cosmic opposition to the good God. This may have crystallized the Jewish concept of Satan as a distinct hostile entity.

### The Resurrection of the Dead
Pre-exilic Hebrew: **Sheol** — a shadowy underworld; no resurrection for most of the OT
Post-exilic (Daniel 12:2; Isaiah 26:19): "Many who sleep in the dust of the earth will awake: some to everlasting life, others to shame and everlasting contempt"

Zoroastrian context: the **Frashokereti** — the final renovation of the world; the resurrection of all the dead for final judgment; the good rewarded, the evil punished

### Apocalypticism and the Last Judgment
The whole framework of **apocalyptic literature** (Daniel, Revelation, 1 Enoch) resembles Zoroastrian eschatology:
- A cosmic conflict between good and evil
- A period of intensifying evil before the end
- A final decisive battle
- The resurrection and judgment
- The renovation of creation

### The Three Wise Men (Magi)
**Matthew 2:1** — "Magi from the East." The word *Magi* (Greek *magoi*) refers specifically to **Zoroastrian priests** — the religious specialists of the Persian/Parthian empire who studied astronomy and interpreted signs. The story presupposes a priestly class that would follow a star to find a new king.

## The Scholarly Debate
**How much did Zoroastrianism influence Judaism?**

**Strong influence position**: Most of the post-exilic developments (angels, Satan, resurrection, eschatology) were borrowed from Zoroastrianism during the Persian period. Mary Boyce, some others.

**Minimal influence position**: These ideas develop organically within Judaism; Zoroastrian parallels reflect common ancient thought; correlation ≠ causation. Many Jewish scholars.

**Moderate position** (current consensus): Real contact and real influence during the Persian period, but the Jewish reception was selective and theologically transformed. The ideas were taken in a monotheistic, historical direction different from their Zoroastrian context.

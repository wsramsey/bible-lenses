# The Epic of Gilgamesh and the Flood Narrative
*The most famous flood story before Genesis — and remarkably similar to it*

## The Epic
The fullest surviving ancient literature outside the Bible. **Gilgamesh** is the king of Uruk (historically attested; c. 2700 BC), two-thirds god and one-third human.

**The story arc**:
1. Gilgamesh and Enkidu (a wild man created to humble Gilgamesh) become best friends
2. They kill the Bull of Heaven and Humbaba the forest monster
3. The gods kill Enkidu in punishment
4. Gilgamesh is devastated by Enkidu's death — confronts his own mortality
5. Seeks **Utnapishtim** (the immortal man) to learn the secret of eternal life
6. Utnapishtim tells him the **Flood story** — how he survived and was given immortality
7. Gilgamesh finds the plant of immortality; a serpent steals it while he sleeps
8. Gilgamesh returns to Uruk with nothing but the walls of his city

## The Flood Tablet (Tablet XI) — Comparison with Genesis

| Detail | Gilgamesh (Utnapishtim) | Genesis (Noah) |
|--------|------------------------|----------------|
| Warned by a god | Ea warns in a dream | God directly commands |
| Reason for flood | Gods annoyed by human noise | Human wickedness |
| Builds a boat | 6-decked cube; 120m | 3 decks; rectangular |
| Takes animals | All living things | All living things |
| Duration | 7 days and 7 nights | 40 days / 150 days |
| Sends out birds | Dove, swallow, raven | Raven, dove (3x) |
| Lands on a mountain | Mount Nimush | Mount Ararat |
| Sacrifice after | Fragrant offerings; gods crowd around | Burnt offerings |
| Divine response | Gods smell the sacrifice | God smells the sacrifice |
| Promise | Immortality given to Utnapishtim | Rainbow covenant |

The similarities are **too specific to be coincidental** — either one depends on the other, or both depend on a common Mesopotamian tradition.

## The Theological Difference
The Flood story in Genesis is not about a capricious divine council annoyed by noise but about **moral judgment** and **covenant promise**. Noah is righteous; the Flood is a response to sin; the rainbow is a covenantal commitment.

The biblical account *uses* the Mesopotamian literary form to make a theologically distinct claim: the God who controls the flood is morally consistent, keeps promises, and relates to humanity through covenant.

## Gilgamesh's Significance Beyond the Flood
The epic is the world's first exploration of **mortality and grief** in literature. Gilgamesh's response to Enkidu's death — his refusal to accept it, his desperate search for immortality, his ultimate failure — is profoundly human.

The **serpent stealing the plant of immortality** (Tablet XI) is widely compared to Genesis 3 — humanity's near-immortality lost through a serpent.

**The Tavern-keeper Siduri's advice** to Gilgamesh (Tablet X) is sometimes called the world's oldest wisdom literature: enjoy your life, love your wife, hold your child's hand — because the gods kept eternal life for themselves. An Epicurean conclusion the Bible would reject.

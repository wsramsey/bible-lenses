# Paradise Lost (John Milton, 1667)
*"To justify the ways of God to men"*

## The Project
John Milton — blind, fallen from political power after the Restoration, a Puritan in a post-Puritan England — dictated this 10,000-line epic to his daughters. His stated purpose: "to justify the ways of God to men" — theodicy in epic form.

## The Story
Books I–II: Satan and the fallen angels in Hell; Satan's journey through Chaos to the new creation
Books III–IV: God's foreknowledge; Satan arrives in Eden; Adam and Eve in Paradise
Books V–VIII: Raphael tells Adam the story of the War in Heaven
Books IX–X: The Fall; the consequences; God's judgment
Books XI–XII: Michael shows Adam the future history of humanity; the expulsion

## Satan — The Most Complex Character
Milton's Satan is arguably the most psychologically compelling character in English literature:
- "Better to reign in Hell than serve in Heaven"
- He is magnificent, eloquent, tortured
- The Romantic poets (Blake, Shelley) thought Milton was "of the devil's party without knowing it"
- **The orthodox reading**: Satan's grandeur is seductive precisely because he is the Great Deceiver; his speeches are brilliant self-justifications for catastrophic choices; he is what pride looks like when it has everything
- Satan's tragedy: in Hell, contemplating his fall, he briefly sees the truth — and turns away from it. That is damnation.

## The Theology
- **Free will**: The Fall is possible because God gave Adam and Eve genuine freedom — without freedom, love is impossible; Milton's theodicy
- **The Fortunate Fall**: *felix culpa* — Augustine's idea that the Fall was necessary for the greater good of Redemption. Milton gestures toward this without fully committing.
- **Christ's obedience**: Contrasted with Satan's rebellion; the "better obedience" of the Son

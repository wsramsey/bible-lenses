# C.S. Lewis — The Complete Works as Theology
*The most widely read Christian apologist of the 20th century*

## The Major Works and Their Theological Contribution

### Mere Christianity (1952)
BBC radio talks during the Blitz (1941–1944); edited into book form. The "trilemma" argument: Jesus was either Liar, Lunatic, or Lord — the "just a good teacher" option is not available. The most influential popular apologetic of the 20th century.

**Theological anchor:** [[The Incarnation]] · [[Apologetics]] · [[The Moral Argument for God]]

### The Screwtape Letters (1942)
A senior demon (Screwtape) writes letters to his nephew (Wormwood) about how to tempt a new Christian. The inversion allows Lewis to describe spiritual warfare, temptation, and grace from the enemy's perspective.

**Best single insight**: "The safest road to Hell is the gradual one — the gentle slope, soft underfoot, without sudden turnings, without milestones, without signposts." Sin as gradual drift, not dramatic fall.

**Theological anchor:** [[Spiritual Warfare]] · [[Temptation]] · [[Sanctification]] · [[The Subtlety of Sin]]

### The Problem of Pain (1940)
Lewis's intellectual theodicy — before suffering touched him personally. "Pain is God's megaphone to rouse a deaf world." The problem of suffering addressed philosophically.

### A Grief Observed (1961)
After his wife Joy died of cancer, Lewis kept a journal. Published pseudonymously. The same man who wrote *The Problem of Pain* now experiencing it. "God has not been trying an experiment on my faith or love in order to find out their quality. He knew it already. It was I who didn't."

**Together these two books are the greatest Christian treatment of suffering** — the intellectual framework (*The Problem of Pain*) and the lived experience (*A Grief Observed*) by the same person.

**Theological anchor:** [[Theodicy and Suffering]] · [[Grief]] · [[Faith Under Trial]]

### The Chronicles of Narnia (1950–1956)
Seven children's novels encoding Christian theology through fantasy:
- **The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe**: Aslan's death and resurrection (substitutionary atonement; the Deeper Magic from before the dawn of time)
- **The Voyage of the Dawn Treader**: The voyage as sanctification; Eustace's "undragoning" (transformation); Aslan at the end of the world
- **The Last Battle**: Eschatology; the stable that is larger on the inside than on the outside (heaven); "Further up and further in"

Lewis: "I am aiming at a supposal — what might Christ become like, if there really were a world like Narnia and He chose to be incarnate and die and rise again in that world as He actually has done in ours?"

### Till We Have Faces (1956)
Lewis's mature masterpiece — a retelling of the Psyche/Eros myth. The question: Why does God not simply show himself? The answer: we are not yet fit to see him; we do not yet have faces clear enough to bear the sight.

**Theological anchor:** [[The Vision of God]] · [[Spiritual Transformation]] · [[Why God Seems Hidden]]

### The Space Trilogy (Out of the Silent Planet, Perelandra, That Hideous Strength)
Cosmic theology: Earth as "the silent planet" — the one planet that has fallen; Mars (Malacandra) and Venus (Perelandra) unfallen; the cosmic war between good and evil; Perelandra as a retelling of the Fall with a different ending.

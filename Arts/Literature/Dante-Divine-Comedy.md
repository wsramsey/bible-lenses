# The Divine Comedy (Dante Alighieri, 1308–1321)
*The greatest work of Christian literature — a complete theology in narrative*

## What It Is
Three canticles — *Inferno* (Hell), *Purgatorio* (Purgatory), *Paradiso* (Heaven) — 100 cantos total; the poet travels through all three realms guided first by Virgil (reason/classical wisdom), then by Beatrice (faith/theology), then by Bernard of Clairvaux (mystical love).

## The Theological Framework

### Inferno
The nine circles of Hell, organized by the principle of **contrapasso** — the punishment fits the crime:
- The lustful are blown about by eternal wind (as they were driven by passion)
- The gluttonous wallow in filth
- The violent are immersed in boiling blood
- The fraudulent are encased in ice (fraud freezes love)
- **The deepest circle**: Satan, frozen in ice, chewing on Judas, Brutus, and Cassius — the three betrayers of their lords (Christ, Caesar)

**Who's in Hell**: Popes (Boniface VIII — still alive when Dante wrote; his place already reserved), politicians, clergy, mythological figures. Dante's Hell is profoundly political.

### Purgatorio
The most theologically interesting canticle for many readers:
- A mountain; each terrace devoted to purging one of the seven deadly sins
- The souls are **penitent** — cooperating with their purification; movement is upward and hopeful
- The artistic emphasis: art is the vehicle of purification; beautiful images of virtue carved in marble; corrupt images of vice
- **The Ante-Purgatory**: those who delayed repentance; the excommunicated; the negligent
- **The Earthly Paradise** at the summit: Eden restored; Beatrice appears

### Paradiso
Nine concentric spheres of heaven; each sphere occupied by souls whose virtue was expressed in a particular way:
- The Moon: those who broke vows
- Mercury: the ambitious
- Venus: the amorous
- The Sun: the wise (Thomas Aquinas, Bonaventure appear here)
- Mars: the warriors for the faith
- Jupiter: the just rulers
- Saturn: the contemplatives (Bernard of Clairvaux)
- The Fixed Stars: the apostles (Peter examines Dante on faith)
- The Primum Mobile: angels
- The Empyrean: the celestial rose; the Trinity; the Beatific Vision

**The ending**: Dante sees God as three circles of light — the Trinity. In the second circle he sees a human form — Christ. The last line: "my desire and will were moved, like a wheel in perfect balance, by the Love which moves the sun and the other stars."

## Dante's Theological Sources
- **[[Thomas Aquinas]]** (dominant): the Thomistic synthesis of Aristotle and Christianity; natural reason + faith
- **[[Augustine of Hippo]]**: *Confessions*; the restless heart; pilgrimage
- **[[Bernard of Clairvaux]]**: guides Dante to the Beatific Vision; mystical theology
- **[[Aristotle]]**: structure of ethics; the virtues
- **Virgil**: classical wisdom; the limits of reason without faith

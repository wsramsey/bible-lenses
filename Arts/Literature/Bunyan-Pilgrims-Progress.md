# The Pilgrim's Progress (John Bunyan, 1678)
*The second bestselling book in English history after the Bible*

## The Context
Bunyan was a tinker (metalworker), Baptist preacher, and prisoner — jailed for 12 years for preaching without a license. He wrote *The Pilgrim's Progress* in prison.

## The Allegory
Christian flees the City of Destruction with a heavy burden on his back (sin) and seeks the Celestial City. The journey:

**Key locations and their theology**:
- **The Slough of Despond**: The depression that comes from recognizing sin before experiencing grace; many sink here
- **The Wicket Gate**: Entry into the Christian life; few find it
- **The Cross**: Christian's burden falls off here — at the cross, not through effort; Reformation theology in narrative
- **The Palace Beautiful** (the church): Rest, fellowship, armor for the journey
- **The Valley of Humiliation**: Where Apollyon attacks — the spiritual warfare of the normal Christian life
- **The Valley of the Shadow of Death**: Psalm 23; darkness, voices, fear
- **Vanity Fair**: The world; all things are bought and sold; Faithful is executed here
- **Doubting Castle** (Giant Despair): Christian and Hopeful imprisoned; the key Promise unlocks every door
- **The Delectable Mountains**: A vision of the Celestial City
- **The River of Death**: Every Christian must cross; some with shallow water, some with deep

## The Characters as Theology
- **Evangelist**: The preacher who sets Christian on the road
- **Obstinate, Pliable**: Those who refuse or quickly abandon the journey
- **Worldly Wiseman**: Moralism — the attempt to lose the burden through good behavior, not the Cross
- **Faithful**: Martyred at Vanity Fair; dies witnessing
- **Hopeful**: Joins Christian; hope as a companion in the Christian life
- **Ignorance**: The most troubling character — confident, appears virtuous, but lacks genuine conversion; turned away at the gate

## Why It Endures
Bunyan mapped the entire inner landscape of the Christian life in narrative form. Every reader recognizes themselves in at least one character. The allegory is psychologically exact — Bunyan's depression, spiritual warfare, and imprisonment gave him firsthand knowledge of what he described.

# Romanesque Christian Art and Architecture (10th–12th century)
*The age before Gothic — massive, solid, and psychologically intense*

## What Makes It Romanesque
Named for its use of **rounded Roman arches** — the defining structural element. Characteristics:
- **Thick walls** — weight-bearing; windows small
- **Rounded arches** everywhere — doorways, arcades, windows
- **Barrel vaults** — semicircular stone ceilings
- **Massive piers and columns**
- **Emotional intensity** in sculpture — twisted figures, bug-eyed saints, demons

## The Great Romanesque Buildings

### Cluny Abbey (910/955/1088)
Three successive buildings; Cluny III (1088–1130) was the largest church in Christendom until St. Peter's was rebuilt. Mostly destroyed in the French Revolution. The **Cluniac Reform** spread the Romanesque style across Europe through 1,450 daughter houses.

### Durham Cathedral (1093–1133)
The finest Romanesque building in England. The first building anywhere to use **ribbed vaulting** in a large nave — a technical breakthrough that led directly to Gothic. Tomb of Saint Cuthbert; Venerable Bede's tomb.

### Worms Cathedral (c. 1130–1181)
Romanesque at its most elemental — the twin towers like fortresses.

### Pisa Cathedral Complex (1063–1278)
The Duomo, Baptistery, and **Leaning Tower** (campanile) — a complete Romanesque monumental program; begun before the tower started leaning.

## The Sculpture

### Gislebertus at Autun (c. 1120–1135)
The greatest Romanesque sculptor. His **Last Judgment** tympanum (the semicircular relief above the west door of Saint-Lazare Cathedral, Autun) is:
- **Christ in Majesty** at the center; the saved on his right, the damned on his left
- **The Weighing of Souls**: the Archangel Michael and a demon compete for souls at the scale
- **The Damned**: twisted, terrified figures being grabbed by demons; one shows a pair of hands gripping someone's head from above — death's grip
- **The Elect**: serene, upright, processing toward heaven

**Gislebertus hoc fecit** ("Gislebertus made this") — he signed the work at Christ's feet; the first medieval sculptor to claim individual authorship.

The figures are psychologically disturbing in a way that Gothic sculpture rarely is — Gothic idealized; Romanesque terrorized.

### The Bayeux Tapestry (c. 1066–1083)
Not technically a tapestry (it's embroidery); 70 meters long; tells the story of the Norman Conquest of England. Made in England, probably for Odo of Bayeux (William's half-brother).

**Theological content**: Harold's oath-breaking (swearing on sacred relics, then breaking the oath) justifies his defeat; God judges the oath-breaker; the conquest is divine judgment.

**As evidence**: Shows medieval military equipment, ships, clothing, and daily life in extraordinary detail; the only contemporary visual account of the Norman Conquest.

### Bernward of Hildesheim's Bronze Doors (1015)
16 biblical scenes in two columns:
- Left column (top to bottom): Old Testament (Creation → Cain and Abel)
- Right column (bottom to top): New Testament (Annunciation → Noli Me Tangere)

The **typological program**: scenes pair diagonally — Adam and Eve receiving their punishment ↔ the Annunciation; Cain killing Abel ↔ the Crucifixion. The Old Testament events are fulfilled in the New Testament.

**Museum**: Cathedral of Saint Michael, Hildesheim (in situ) — UNESCO World Heritage Site

# Liturgical Objects as Theology — The Sacred Things
*The objects through which Christianity is enacted — theology made tangible*

## The Chalice and Paten

The **chalice** (cup) and **paten** (plate) are the central objects of Christian worship — used for the Eucharist since the Last Supper.

### The Ardagh Chalice (c. 8th century)
**National Museum of Ireland, Dublin — NMI 1874:99**
Discovered 1868 in a field at Ardagh, County Limerick. One of the masterworks of early medieval metalwork:
- Silver, gold, bronze, brass, lead, enamel, glass
- The names of the apostles around the bowl
- The underside is elaborately decorated — visible only to the priest as he lifts it

### The Chalice of Antioch (c. 4th–6th century)
**Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York — 50.4**
Controversial — once claimed to be the Holy Grail (it isn't) but is a genuine early Christian silver chalice from Antioch with figures of Christ and the apostles.

### The Theology of the Chalice
- The chalice holds the blood of Christ
- In Catholic and Orthodox tradition: transformation (transubstantiation / real presence)
- In Protestant tradition: memorial; the cup of blessing (1 Corinthians 10:16)
- The most contested physical object in Christian history — what it contains determined denominational identity for 500 years

## Reliquaries — The Sacred Remains

### What Relics Are
The physical remains (bones, hair, blood) or objects associated with saints. Medieval Catholic and Orthodox theology: the saint's body participated in grace; that grace persists; proximity to the remains can channel it.

**Scripture**: Acts 19:12 — "handkerchiefs and aprons that had touched Paul" healed the sick; 2 Kings 13:21 — a man revived when he touched Elisha's bones.

### Major Reliquaries

**The Shrine of the Three Magi (c. 1180–1225)**
**Cologne Cathedral — in situ**
The largest reliquary in the Western world; gold, silver, enamel, gems; allegedly contains the bones of the Magi. The reason Cologne became the most important pilgrimage site in northern Europe.

**The Reliquary of the True Cross**
Multiple pieces of wood claimed to be from the cross; distributed across Europe; Erasmus joked that enough pieces existed to build a ship. Calvin calculated the same.

**The Sainte-Chapelle, Paris (1239–1248)**
King Louis IX built this Gothic chapel essentially as a **reliquary** — to house the Crown of Thorns purchased from the Emperor of Constantinople. The building *is* the reliquary. The stained glass (15 windows, 1,000+ biblical scenes) makes the chapel into the New Jerusalem.

**The Theology of Relics**
- Catholic/Orthodox: the communion of saints; the body matters; material grace
- Protestant Reformation: relics are superstition; the Reformers destroyed them systematically
- The irony: Protestant visitors to Rome and Jerusalem often report being moved by *place* — the physical location of sacred events. Place functions as a relic for Protestants who would deny the category.

## The Monstrance — Making God Visible

A **monstrance** (from Latin *monstrare* = to show) is the vessel that displays the consecrated Eucharistic host for **adoration** — the practice of worshiping Christ present in the bread.

**The architecture of a monstrance**: A sunburst of gold rays surrounding a glass lunette (window) holding the host. The rays encode Pseudo-Dionysius's light theology — the divine reality radiates outward from its center.

**The Theology**: The consecrated host *is* Christ, present. The monstrance makes this presence visible. Eucharistic adoration ("perpetual adoration") is the contemplative practice of sitting in silence before the monstrance.

**The Toledo Monstrance (Juan de Arfe, 1517–1524)**
**Cathedral of Toledo, Spain — in situ**
Nearly 3 meters tall; solid silver; 16kg; Gothic architecture in metal. The largest Gothic monstrance in existence.

## The Bible as Liturgical Object

The **Gospel Book** — the physical book containing the four Gospels — has a specific liturgical function:
- Processed into the church
- Held aloft and announced: "The Gospel of the Lord"
- Kissed by the celebrant
- The book itself is venerated, not just its content

**The Lindisfarne Gospels** (c. 715 AD) — British Library, Cotton MS Nero D.IV
A complete Gospel book made by Bishop Eadfrith as an act of devotion to Saint Cuthbert. Every page is a visual theology:
- The **carpet pages**: elaborate interlace patterns representing divine order in creation
- The **incipit pages**: the first words of each Gospel in decorative lettering
- The **canon tables**: concordances of parallel passages set in architectural arches

The book was never meant to be read from — it was too precious. It *is* the sacred offering; it enacts what it contains.

## The Baptismal Font

The font holds the water of baptism — the locus of new birth, the crossing from death to life.

**Romanesque fonts** (12th century): Often octagonal (8 = the day beyond the week = eternity); the eight sides echo the octave of creation. Common carved scenes: the baptism of Christ; the crossing of the Red Sea; death and resurrection.

**The Baptismal Pool at Dura-Europos** (c. 240 AD): The oldest surviving baptismal font; carved niches; the space is a womb — you descend into it and rise. The paintings above show healing, resurrection, the women at the tomb.

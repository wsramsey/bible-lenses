# The Isenheim Altarpiece (Matthias Grünewald, c. 1512–1516)
*The most theologically serious crucifixion image ever painted*

## The Context
Commissioned for the chapel of the Hospital of St. Anthony in Isenheim — a hospital that specialized in treating patients with **ergotism** ("St. Anthony's Fire"), a horrific skin disease caused by fungal contamination of grain. The patients would see this altarpiece daily.

## The Crucifixion Panel
The most confrontational image of the crucifixion in Western art:
- Christ's body is **physically destroyed** — not the serene Platonic Christ of Italian Renaissance; his flesh is torn, discolored, covered with the wounds of flogging
- His fingers claw the air; his feet are twisted and nailed
- The body is **diseased** — deliberately; the hospital patients would see their own affliction in Christ's body
- John the Baptist stands to the right pointing at Christ with an impossibly long finger and a text scroll: *"He must increase; I must decrease"*
- The Lamb at John's feet bleeds into a chalice — the Eucharistic theology explicit

## The Resurrection Panel (inner wings open)
When the wings open: the Resurrection. Christ bursts from the tomb in an explosion of golden light — transfigured, radiant, beyond all suffering. The guards fall as if dead. The contrast with the suffering of the crucifixion is total.

**The theological statement**: The path from crucifixion to resurrection; suffering is not the last word; but suffering is real first.

## Why Karl Barth Kept It Above His Desk
[[Karl Barth]] kept a reproduction of the Isenheim Altarpiece above his desk throughout his theological career. For Barth, it represented the core of his theology: the crucified Christ who takes upon himself the full weight of human sin and suffering, and the Resurrection that declares God's "Yes" despite and through that suffering.

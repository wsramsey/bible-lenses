# William Blake — Prophetic Christian Art
*The artist as prophet — a unique Christian visionary*

## Blake's Theology
William Blake was a Christian — but not an orthodox one. His theology was:
- The God of institutional religion is "Urizen" — the false god of law, reason, and repression
- The true God is "Los" — creative energy, imagination, the divine humanity
- Christ is the supreme human — the divine energy become flesh
- **"The imagination is not a state; it is the human existence itself"**

Blake opposed both deism (the clockmaker God) and institutional Christianity (which he saw as oppressing the poor and suppressing human creativity). His Christianity was prophetic — in the tradition of Isaiah, Ezekiel, and John of Patmos.

## Key Works

### The Book of Job (1825)
Blake's 21 engraved illustrations of the Book of Job are among the greatest works of Western art. They reinterpret Job's story:
- Job begins comfortable, pious, unimaginative — his God is a law-giver
- The Satan is not an enemy but a part of Job's own psyche — the accusation that comes from within
- Job's suffering is not punishment but education — the stripping away of false religion
- God's answer from the whirlwind shows Job the cosmos — and Job's God becomes the living, creative, terrifying reality rather than the comfortable lawgiver

### "The Ancient of Days" (1794)
God (Urizen) as a bearded figure reaching down with a compass to measure the universe — the false god of rational order. Blake's critique of the Enlightenment's clockmaker God.

### Jerusalem: The Emanation of the Giant Albion (1804–1820)
Blake's epic prophecy — Britain as a fallen spiritual entity; Jerusalem as the spiritual vision of what Britain could be; Albion as the universal human fallen and redeemable.

**"And did those feet in ancient time / Walk upon England's mountains green?"** — the hymn "Jerusalem" from the preface to *Milton*; now virtually England's second national anthem; Blake's prophetic vision of England as a holy land.

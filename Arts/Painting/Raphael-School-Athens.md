# The School of Athens (Raphael, 1509–1511)
*The visual manifesto of Renaissance humanism — Athens and Jerusalem reconciled*

## The Painting
Painted for Pope Julius II's private library, paired with *The Disputation over the Holy Sacrament* (theology) on the opposite wall. The juxtaposition is deliberate: faith and reason are complementary, not opposed.

## The Central Figures
**Plato** (left, pointing upward) and **Aristotle** (right, gesturing down):
- Plato's gesture indicates the realm of Forms — ideas, transcendence, the eternal
- Aristotle's gesture indicates the earth — matter, observation, the empirical
- Together: the two intellectual poles of all subsequent philosophy

**The figures identified**:
- Socrates (left foreground, enumerating arguments on fingers)
- Diogenes (reclining on steps)
- Pythagoras (lower left, writing)
- Euclid/Archimedes (demonstrating geometry with compass)
- Michelangelo as Heraclitus (added late, brooding alone on steps — famously)
- Raphael himself (far right, looking at viewer)

**Plato's face = Leonardo da Vinci**: Raphael's tribute to the Renaissance genius

## The Theological Significance
The painting occupies the *Stanza della Segnatura* (signature room) alongside *The Disputation* (Christian theology), *Parnassus* (poetry), and *The Cardinal Virtues* (ethics/law). The program: human knowledge has four branches — philosophy, theology, poetry, law — and they are harmonious.

This is the visual argument of [[Thomas Aquinas]]: faith and reason are complementary. The painting hangs in the Vatican because the Catholic Church in the Renaissance held that Greek philosophy prepared the way for Christian truth.

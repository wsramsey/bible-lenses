# The Sistine Chapel Ceiling (Michelangelo, 1508–1512)
*The most famous ceiling in the world — a complete theology of creation*

## The Commission
Pope Julius II commissioned Michelangelo (initially reluctant — he considered himself a sculptor) to paint the ceiling of the Sistine Chapel. Michelangelo worked lying on his back on scaffolding for four years.

## The Program
The ceiling tells the story of Genesis (central panels) surrounded by prophets, sibyls, and the ancestors of Christ:

**Central Panels (reading from the altar)**:
1. The Separation of Light from Darkness
2. The Creation of the Sun, Moon, and Plants
3. The Separation of Land from Water
4. **The Creation of Adam** — the most famous image in Western art
5. The Creation of Eve
6. The Temptation and Fall
7. The Sacrifice of Noah
8. The Flood
9. The Drunkenness of Noah

**The prophets and sibyls** (on the thrones): Jonah, Isaiah, Ezekiel, Joel, Zechariah, Jeremiah, Daniel; and five pagan sibyls — the Cumaean, Libyan, Persian, Eritrean, Delphic. The inclusion of sibyls reflects Renaissance typology: the pagan world also witnessed God's truth.

## The Creation of Adam (Panel 4)
The most theologically loaded image in Renaissance art:
- God reaches toward Adam; Adam reaches back — but Adam's finger is lower, less energetic; God's is higher, more dynamic
- Adam is already beautiful — the image suggests the *imago Dei* is already present; God's touch *activates* it
- The group of figures under God's left arm: the female figure (Eve? the Soul? the Church?) sheltered in the divine cloak
- **The theological claim**: The *imago Dei* is not a quality already possessed but a relationship — humanity becomes fully human only in the turning toward God

## The Last Judgment (1536–1541)
Michelangelo returned to paint the altar wall 25 years later under Pope Paul III. The vision is darker, the bodies more tortured, the Christ more wrathful. The artist himself painted as the flayed skin held by St. Bartholomew — a self-portrait of suffering and judgment.

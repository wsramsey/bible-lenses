# The Burial of the Count of Orgaz (El Greco, 1586–1588)
*Heaven and earth meeting — the Counter-Reformation's visual manifesto*

## The Painting
Commissioned for the Church of Santo Tomé in Toledo; still in its original location.

**The composition**: The painting is split into two registers:
- **Earth (lower half)**: The funeral of the Count; St. Augustine and St. Stephen descend from heaven to lower his body into the grave; Toledo's nobility watch
- **Heaven (upper half)**: Christ at the apex; Mary and John the Baptist as intercessors; an angel carries the Count's soul upward as a baby-like form; the cloud of witnesses

## The Theology
El Greco's Spanish Counter-Reformation world:
- **The intercession of saints**: Stephen and Augustine physically appear at the burial — the Catholic doctrine of saintly intercession made visible
- **Heaven and earth permeable**: The division is real but not absolute; saints move between them
- **The communion of saints**: The creed's claim given visual form
- **El Greco's elongated figures**: The vertical stretching has been interpreted as mystical — the souls reaching upward; influenced by [[Byzantine iconography]] (El Greco trained in Crete)

## El Greco and the Spanish Mystics
El Greco painted in Toledo, the city of [[Teresa of Ávila]] and [[John of the Cross]]. His visual mysticism and their contemplative theology were contemporary responses to the same religious world.

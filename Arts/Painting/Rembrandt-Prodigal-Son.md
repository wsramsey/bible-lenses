# The Return of the Prodigal Son (Rembrandt, c. 1668)
*Henri Nouwen's contemplation of this painting became the most influential spiritual book of the late 20th century*

## The Painting
Rembrandt painted this in the last year of his life — having himself experienced bankruptcy, the death of his wife and son, public humiliation. He understood the prodigal's return from the inside.

**The composition**:
- The father bends over the ragged son kneeling before him
- His hands rest on the son's shoulders — one hand appears more masculine (the father), one more feminine (the mother) — Rembrandt collapses gender in the image of divine tenderness
- The son's head is shaved (slave's mark); his shoes are worn through; he is dressed in rags
- Observers stand at the edges — including a figure often identified as the older brother
- The background is dark; only the reunion is lit

## Henri Nouwen's Meditation
[[Henri Nouwen]] spent hours before this painting in the Hermitage in 1983. The experience produced *The Return of the Prodigal Son* (1992) — his most important work.

Nouwen identified with all three major figures:
- **The younger son**: the one who has left home and wasted his inheritance
- **The older son**: the resentful, dutiful one who has never felt free
- **The father**: the calling to become a compassionate presence for others

His reflection: most of us live our entire lives as the younger or older son, never becoming the father — the one whose arms are always open.

## The Parable (Luke 15:11–32)
The most theologically loaded parable in the Gospels:
- The father runs to meet the son — undignified for a Middle Eastern patriarch; [[Kenneth Bailey]] (*Poet and Peasant*) argues this running prevented the village from shaming the son
- The robe, ring, and shoes restore full sonship
- The feast is eucharistic — "this son of mine was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found"
- The older son's complaint is never answered — the parable is left open

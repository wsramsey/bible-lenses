# Hieronymus Bosch — Hell, Heaven, and Human Folly
*The most disturbing Christian painter — and the most theologically serious*

## Who Was Bosch?
Dutch painter from 's-Hertogenbosch (hence the name); deeply embedded in late medieval Catholic piety; member of the Brotherhood of Our Lady (a Marian devotional confraternity). His images drew on medieval sermon literature, popular allegory, and alchemical imagery.

## The Garden of Earthly Delights (c. 1490–1510)

### The Three Panels
A triptych (three-paneled altarpiece):
- **Left panel**: Eden; the creation of Eve; Paradise before sin — lush, peaceful, but with strange animals hinting at what's coming
- **Central panel**: Earth; humanity indulging in sensory pleasure — eating, sexuality, games — a teeming world of people and hybrid creatures; apparently pleasurable but hollow
- **Right panel**: Hell; musical instruments as torture devices; a demon seated on a throne of ice; the burning city; hybrid creature-torturers; the man-tree looking at the viewer

### The Theological Reading
The triptych traces the Fall: creation (left) → sin (center) → consequence (right). The central panel's pleasures are revealed as the path to the right panel's torments. The strangeness of Bosch's Hell is not gratuitous — it reflects the medieval conviction that sin is *distorting*: it makes the human less human, produces monstrous hybrids of what humans should be.

### The Music in Hell
In the right panel, a man is crucified on a harp; another is impaled on a lute. Music — created to praise God — is twisted into torture. The instrument of worship becomes the instrument of suffering. This encodes a theology of distorted creation: sin does not destroy what is good but perverts it.

## Seven Deadly Sins Tondo (c. 1500–1525)
A circular painting of the seven deadly sins as scenes from ordinary life, surrounding an eye of God at the center. The inscription: *Cave Cave Dominus Videt* — "Beware, beware, the Lord sees." The theological claim: sin is visible to God even when invisible to the sinner.

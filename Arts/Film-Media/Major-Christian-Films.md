# Major Christian Films and Their Theology

## The Passion of the Christ (Mel Gibson, 2004)
The final 12 hours of Jesus's life. Aramaic and Latin dialogue; uncompromising depiction of the physical suffering of crucifixion.

**Theology**: Substitutionary atonement visualized with maximum intensity. The suffering *is* the point. Critics accused it of being medieval Catholic flagellant piety.

**Cultural impact**: 612 million domestic gross; record-breaking for a foreign-language film; sparked enormous conversation about faith, antisemitism (critics of the film's portrayal of Jewish leaders), and the nature of the Passion.

**Connected to**: [[The Crucifixion]] · [[Atonement]] · [[Isenheim Altarpiece]] (similar aesthetic)

---

## Of Gods and Men (Xavier Beauvois, 2010)
Based on true events: French Cistercian monks at Tibhirine, Algeria decide whether to stay or flee during the Algerian civil war (1990s). They stay. They are kidnapped and killed.

**Theology**: The decision to remain as an act of love; martyrdom not as heroism but as ordinary faithfulness; a final Eucharistic meal together before they are taken (deliberate Last Supper imagery).

The film is almost entirely silent — liturgy, work, simple meals. The theology is in what is not said.

**Connected to**: [[Martyrdom]] · [[Cistercian Monasticism]] · [[The Last Supper]] · [[Islam and Christianity in North Africa]]

---

## Silence (Martin Scorsese, 2016)
Based on Shūsaku Endō's novel: 17th-century Jesuit missionaries in Japan; apostasy under torture; God's silence.

**The theological crisis**: The missionary's mentor has apostatized (trampled on an icon of Christ to save Japanese Christians who will be killed if he doesn't). The missionary refuses — until he hears the voice of Christ from the icon saying "Trample. I was born into this world to share men's pain."

**The unanswerable question**: Is the silence of God absence or a different form of presence? Is apostasy to save others a form of love?

**Connected to**: [[Silence of God]] · [[Theodicy]] · [[Martyrdom]] · [[Japan Missions]] · [[The Hidden Church]] · [[Endō Shūsaku]]

---

## A Man for All Seasons (Fred Zinnemann, 1966)
Thomas More's refusal to endorse Henry VIII's divorce and his subsequent execution.

**Theology**: The primacy of conscience; "I die the King's good servant — but God's first"; the cost of integrity; the soul as the one thing that cannot be surrendered.

**Connected to**: [[Thomas More]] · [[Henry VIII]] · [[The English Reformation]] · [[Conscience]] · [[Martyrdom]]

---

## The Mission (Roland Joffé, 1986)
18th-century Jesuit missions to the Guaraní of Paraguay; the political suppression of the missions; destruction of the Reductions.

**Theology**: Two responses to injustice — Father Gabriel (nonviolent witness, dying in procession) and Rodrigo (armed resistance). The film does not resolve which was right.

**Connected to**: [[Jesuit Reductions of Paraguay]] · [[Colonialism and Missions]] · [[Nonviolence vs. Just War]] · [[The Suppression of the Jesuits (1773)]]

---

## Babette's Feast (Gabriel Axel, 1987, based on Karen Blixen)
A French refugee cook, living with a austere Protestant community in 19th-century Norway, wins the lottery and spends it all on a magnificent feast for the community.

**Theology**: Grace as abundance; the feast as eschatological anticipation; art (cooking) as a vehicle of the sacred; the Eucharistic table as the great equalizer.

**Connected to**: [[Grace]] · [[Eucharist]] · [[The Kingdom Feast]] · [[Pietism]] (the community's piety is recognizable)

---

## The Tree of Life (Terrence Malick, 2011)
A Texas family in the 1950s; the death of a son; cosmic origins; grace and nature.

**Theology**: The Book of Job as frame (the film begins with Job 38). The "way of grace" (the mother) vs. "the way of nature" (the father). Creation as revelation. Eschatological ending.

**Connected to**: [[Job (Bible)]] · [[Theodicy]] · [[Creation Theology]] · [[Grace vs. Law]]

---

## Schindler's List (Steven Spielberg, 1993)
Not explicitly Christian but deeply theological:
- Oskar Schindler: an opportunist transformed by proximity to horror into someone who spends everything to save lives
- The last scene: descendants of the Schindler Jews placing stones on his grave
- **The Talmudic text** spoken at the end: "Whoever saves one life, saves the world entire" (Sanhedrin 37a)

**Connected to**: [[Holocaust]] · [[Christian Antisemitism]] · [[Imago Dei]] · [[The Righteous Among the Nations]]

---

## Ben-Hur (William Wyler, 1959)
Judah Ben-Hur's life intersects with Jesus's three times — at the beginning, middle, and end of Jesus's ministry. The healing of his mother and sister at the crucifixion. The film never shows Jesus's face.

**Theology**: The intersection of Roman power and divine grace; revenge vs. forgiveness; the one who saves cannot be shown directly.

**Connected to**: [[Roman Occupation of Judea]] · [[The Crucifixion]] · [[Healing]] · [[Forgiveness]]

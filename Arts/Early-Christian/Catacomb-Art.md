# Catacomb Art — The First Christian Images
*The visual language of Christianity had to be invented — here is where it was*

## What the Catacombs Are
Underground burial networks beneath Rome (and other cities), used by Christians and Jews from the 2nd to 5th centuries. Not primarily hiding places (the Roman authorities knew about them) but cemeteries — Christianity's emphasis on bodily resurrection meant careful burial, not cremation.

**Extent**: 600km of tunnels beneath Rome alone; estimated 500,000–750,000 burials.

## The Paintings — Inventing Christian Visual Language

### The Good Shepherd (c. 200–400 AD)
The most common early Christian image: a young, beardless man carrying a sheep on his shoulders. Not immediately recognizable as Christ — deliberately ambiguous. Roman pagan figures carried rams in the same pose.

**Sources**: Psalm 23; John 10:11–16; Luke 15:4–7
**Theological content**: Christ as the one who seeks and carries the lost; protection; pastoral care
**Why ambiguous**: During persecution, Christian identity had to be deniable
**Notable examples**: Catacomb of Priscilla (Rome); Catacomb of Domitilla

### Jonah (c. 200–400 AD)
The second most common subject — Jonah swallowed by the sea monster and vomited out.

**Why Jonah**: The first explicit Gospel statement about the resurrection sign: "For as Jonah was three days and three nights in the belly of a huge fish, so the Son of Man will be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth" (Matthew 12:40). Jonah = the resurrection, cryptically.

The catacombs show Jonah's story in sequence: thrown overboard → swallowed → vomited → resting under a vine (*the resurrection rest*).

### The Orant Figure
A standing figure with arms raised in prayer — the classic pose of Roman prayer. In Christian context: the soul in heaven at prayer; sometimes the deceased; sometimes the Church.

### Daniel in the Lions' Den
Salvation from death; echoes the resurrection; the faithful preserved.

### Abraham and Isaac
The Akedah as a type of Christ's sacrifice; the ram caught in the thicket = Christ.

### The Multiplication of the Loaves / Wedding at Cana
Eucharistic imagery; the abundance of grace; Christ as provider.

## The Symbols

### The Fish (Ichthys)
Greek: *Iēsous Christos Theou Huios Sōtēr* — "Jesus Christ, Son of God, Savior." The initial letters spell ICHTHYS (fish). The secret identifier of persecuted Christians.

### The Chi-Rho (☧)
The first two letters of *Christos* (XP). Used as a secret identifier before Constantine; after Constantine, became the official imperial Christian symbol.

### The Anchor
Hebrews 6:19: "We have this hope as an anchor for the soul." An anchor with a cross hidden in its shape — the cross disguised as a utilitarian object.

### The Vine
John 15:1: "I am the true vine." Grapes and vines were also standard Roman decorative motifs — another example of ambiguous Christian visual language.

## The Dura-Europos House Church (c. 240 AD)
The oldest known Christian house church (and oldest known synagogue, in the same city) was discovered at **Dura-Europos** (modern Qalaat es-Salihiyye, Syria) in the 1920s.

**The paintings** (now in Yale University Art Gallery):
- The Good Shepherd (oldest known?)
- The Healing of the Paralytic (John 5) — the oldest known Gospel narrative scene
- Peter Walking on Water
- The Women at the Tomb
- David and Goliath
- A large baptistery with a font and healing imagery

**Significance**: Shows Christian visual art 75 years before Constantine; not a luxury production; a house converted into a church; the images are functional — the baptistery paintings instruct baptismal candidates.

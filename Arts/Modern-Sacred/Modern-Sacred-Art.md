# Modern and Contemporary Sacred Art
*The 20th century's unlikely sacred artists*

## Marc Chagall — Jewish Artist, Christian Commissions

**Marc Chagall** (1887–1985) — born in a Hasidic Jewish community in Belarus; became one of the 20th century's most important artists; was commissioned by Christian churches for their most significant art projects.

**Hadassah Medical Center Windows, Jerusalem (1962)**
12 stained glass windows representing the 12 tribes of Israel. Chagall: "All the time I was working, I felt my father and my mother were looking over my shoulder; and behind them were Jews, millions of other vanished Jews."

**Reims Cathedral Windows (1974)**
The east window of the cathedral rebuilt after WWI. Chagall's intensely colorful, floating figures — the Crucifixion, the Sacrifice of Isaac — in a cathedral that was nearly destroyed by German artillery.

**Metz Cathedral Windows (1958–1968)**
The largest Chagall stained glass program in a French cathedral.

**The theological significance**: A Jewish artist given the commission to express Christian mysteries — the wound of the Shoah addressed through the window of a restored cathedral. Beauty as response to catastrophe.

## Henri Matisse — The Chapel of the Rosary, Vence (1947–1951)

Matisse was 77 years old, post-cancer surgery, largely confined to his studio. Dominican nun Sister Jacques-Marie (who had nursed him) asked him to design a small chapel for her community.

**The Chapel of the Rosary, Vence**:
- Matisse called it "my masterpiece" — the only work of architecture he ever designed
- White-tiled walls; three stained glass windows (yellow, green, blue — representing sun, vegetation, sky/sea)
- The light from the windows projects moving color onto the white tiles — the chapel breathes
- Three ceramic tile murals: Saint Dominic, the Virgin and Child, the Stations of the Cross
- The Stations of the Cross are pure black line drawing on white tile — reduced to essence

**Matisse** (who described himself as an agnostic): "Although I am not a Christian, I have created this chapel for the good of the human soul." He spent 4 years on it.

**The theological reading**: A chapel designed by an agnostic that achieves serenity; the light itself becomes the primary experience; the building as a vessel of light — exactly what Abbot Suger intended with Gothic architecture.

## Georges Rouault — Christ Mocked

**Georges Rouault** (1871–1958) — the only major 20th-century painter who was explicitly and primarily a religious artist; deeply Catholic.

His **Miserere et Guerre** series (58 prints, 1922–1927): The war, the poor, the face of Christ.

His style: heavy black outlines (like stained glass or cloisonné enamel); dark, jewel-like colors. His Christ figures are battered, buffeted, often barely recognizable — the face of Christ overlaid with suffering humanity.

**Clown as Christ**: Rouault repeatedly painted clowns. The clown — who entertains while suffering, who wears a mask over pain — is his visual type for Christ and for all marginalized humanity. A Catholic working-class theology in paint.

**Head of Christ (1905)**: Museum of Fine Arts, Houston. A face of absolute suffering, dignity, and compassion — perhaps the most powerful 20th-century image of Christ.

## Barnett Newman — The Stations of the Cross (1958–1966)

**Barnett Newman** (1905–1970) — Jewish abstract expressionist. His 14 Stations of the Cross paintings (National Gallery of Art, Washington DC) are among the most extraordinary works of 20th-century religious art.

**The paintings**: Black and white vertical bands (*zips*) on raw canvas. No imagery. No representation. Just the paintings themselves.

**The title**: The subtitle is "Lema Sabachthani" — "Why hast thou forsaken me?" (Matthew 27:46). Newman: "The Passion is not a story about death. It is a story about abandonment."

**The theological statement**: The abstract removes all the comforting details — no beautiful face, no golden light, no narrative closure. What remains is the raw question: Where is God? The paintings refuse to answer.

**In the National Gallery**: The 14 paintings installed in a room. Visitors walk them as they would the actual Stations. The absence of imagery forces the theological question into the present.

## Andy Warhol — The Last Supper Series (1986)

Warhol produced **over 100 works based on Leonardo's Last Supper** in the last year of his life (1986). Most were shown at a Milan gallery across the street from Santa Maria delle Grazie, where Leonardo's original is.

**What most people don't know**: Warhol was a practicing Byzantine Catholic his entire life; attended Mass regularly; volunteered at homeless shelters; lived an ascetic personal life despite his public persona.

His Last Supper works: some reverent (close copies of Leonardo); some overlaid with advertising logos and price tags; some in camouflage. The range embodies the ambiguity of sacred art in consumer culture — is this reverence or critique?

**The PRICE 59¢ Last Supper**: The body of Christ on offer at the price of a fast-food meal. Is Warhol critiquing the commodification of Christianity? Or celebrating the infinite accessibility of grace? Probably both.

## The Rothko Chapel, Houston (1971)

**Mark Rothko** (1903–1970) — Jewish Russian-born American abstract expressionist. Commissioned by John and Dominique de Ménil to create an interfaith chapel in Houston.

14 large black/maroon canvases (octagonal room). The darkest works Rothko ever produced. He died before the chapel opened.

**The theological effect**: Visitors describe the chapel as overwhelming, contemplative, sometimes terrifying. The colors are nearly black — but in changing light, they glow. The paintings are often called "presences" rather than objects.

**Interfaith by design**: No specific religious imagery; hosts Buddhist, Jewish, Christian, Muslim ceremonies. The sacred space defined by profound art rather than religious symbol.

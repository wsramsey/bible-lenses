# Gregorian Chant — The Church's Oldest Music
*The soundscape of Western Christianity for a thousand years*

## What It Is
Monophonic (single melody line) liturgical music of the Roman Catholic Church. "Gregorian" after Pope Gregory I (590–604), who standardized and codified the chant tradition — though the repertoire developed over centuries before and after him.

## The Eight Modes
Gregorian chant uses **eight modes** (scales) — different from the major/minor system of modern Western music:
- Each mode has a characteristic emotional quality
- Medieval theorists associated modes with psychological/spiritual states
- The **Dorian mode** (characteristic of many chants): often described as deep, sober, solemn
- The music's strangeness to modern ears is part of its effect — it places the listener outside normal time

## The Office and the Hours
Gregorian chant was the primary vehicle of the **Divine Office** (Liturgy of the Hours) — the Benedictine structure of prayer throughout the day:
- **Matins** (night): the longest; multiple psalms
- **Lauds** (dawn): praise psalms
- **Prime, Terce, Sext, None** (day hours): brief
- **Vespers** (evening): major hour; Magnificat
- **Compline** (night prayer): *Salve Regina*; going to sleep

The entire monastery's day was structured around chant. Time itself was liturgical.

## Musical Notation
Guido of Arezzo (991–1033), a Benedictine monk, **invented staff notation** specifically to teach Gregorian chant more efficiently. The names of the notes (do, re, mi, fa, sol, la, si) come from the opening syllables of a hymn to John the Baptist:
*Ut* queant laxis / *Re*sonare fibris / *Mi*ra gestorum / *Fa*muli tuorum / *Sol*ve polluti / *La*bii reatum / *S*ancte *I*oannes

## The 20th Century Revival
The **Solesmes monks** (France) meticulously restored authentic Gregorian chant in the 19th–20th centuries. Their recordings of chant became unexpected bestsellers in the 1990s — secular audiences discovering contemplative space.

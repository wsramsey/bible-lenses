# Johann Sebastian Bach — Theology in Music
*"The aim and final end of all music should be none other than the glory of God and the refreshment of the soul"*

## Bach's Theological Identity
Bach was a devout **Lutheran**. He:
- Signed all his manuscripts **SDG** (*Soli Deo Gloria* — To God Alone the Glory)
- Signed his personal works **J.J.** (*Jesu Juva* — Jesus, help me)
- Owned Luther's complete works (annotated heavily) and a full set of the church fathers
- Wrote in the margins of his Bible: "Where there is devotional music, God with his grace is always present" (on 2 Chronicles 5:13)

## The Cantatas — Weekly Theology
Bach composed approximately 295 **church cantatas** for the Lutheran liturgical year (about 200 survive):
- Each written for a specific Sunday and its appointed Scripture readings
- Typically 20–35 minutes; performed between the Gospel reading and the sermon
- Every week the congregation received a musical meditation on that week's biblical text
- The cantata form: opening chorus → recitatives → arias → closing chorale (congregational hymn)

**Example**: Cantata BWV 82 (*Ich habe genug* — "I have enough") for the Feast of the Purification:
- Simeon's words at the presentation of Jesus ("Lord, now let your servant depart in peace")
- A meditation on death as release — "I have enough; I have beheld the Lord"
- Some of the most profound music Bach wrote

## The B Minor Mass (BWV 232)
Bach's greatest choral work — compiled across his career, finished in 1749.
- Begins with **Kyrie** (Lord have mercy) in the most complex counterpoint
- The **Crucifixus** (He was crucified): descending chromatic bass line; the world descending into chaos; then — silence — then the **Et Resurrexit** (And he rose): explosion of joy
- Ends with **Dona nobis pacem** (Grant us peace): the same music as the **Gratias agimus tibi** (We give thanks to you) — peace and thanksgiving are the same thing

## St. Matthew Passion (BWV 244)
3 hours; double chorus; narrates the Passion from Matthew's Gospel set to music. The crowd's shout "Crucify him!" is in both choirs simultaneously. Christ's words are set apart in strings (a halo of sound). The chorus *Erbarme dich* (Have mercy upon me) after Peter's denial — Bach's most emotionally devastating aria.

## The Well-Tempered Clavier
Preludes and fugues in all 24 major and minor keys — demonstrating the mathematical completeness of the new tuning system. The **mathematical order** in Bach's counterpoint was, for him, a reflection of divine order in creation.

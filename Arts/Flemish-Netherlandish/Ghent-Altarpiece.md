# The Ghent Altarpiece (1432)
*The most theologically complex panel painting ever made — and the most stolen artwork in history*

## What It Is
A polyptych (multi-panel altarpiece): **12 large panels** on the exterior, **12 panels** on the interior when open. Approximately 24 individual scenes and figures.

## The Theft Record
**Most stolen artwork in history** — stolen or moved at least 13 times:
- Philip II of Spain wanted it (16th c.) — it stayed
- Emperor Joseph II ordered the Adam and Eve panels removed (18th c.) — too scandalous
- French Revolutionary troops seized it (1794) — returned 1816
- German art dealer bought it (1821) — partially
- World War I Germany occupied Belgium — considered stealing it
- **World War II**: the Nazis *did* steal it (1942); hidden in a salt mine in Altaussee, Austria; recovered by the Monuments Men (1945); returned to Ghent

## The Interior Program (Opened for High Feasts)

### Upper Register
**God the Father** (center): an extraordinary figure — a pope? a king? a bishop? All three — Christ as Priest, Prophet, and King; or simply the sovereign God; wearing a tiara with a real jeweled crown painted in impossible detail
**Mary Annunciate** (left of God): reading a book; crowned; Queen of Heaven
**John the Baptist** (right of God): robed in green; the precursor
**Angel Musicians** (sides): playing organ, singing; the heavenly liturgy
**Adam and Eve** (outer panels): the only nudes in Flemish painting of the period; startlingly realistic; Adam shown from below (the viewer's eye level); Eve slightly more idealized

### Lower Register — The Adoration of the Mystic Lamb
The central scene: **the Lamb of God** on an altar, blood flowing from its chest into a chalice, surrounded by:
- Angels carrying the instruments of the Passion
- **The Just Judges** and **Knights of Christ** (left panels)
- **The Holy Hermits** and **Pilgrims** (right panels)
- **The Prophets and Patriarchs** (far left)
- **The Apostles and Martyrs** (far right)
- All converging on the Lamb from the four directions of the compass

**The Theology**: Revelation 5 made visible — every nation, tribe, and tongue worshiping the Lamb; the communion of all saints across all time; the Eucharist as participation in the eternal heavenly liturgy

### The Fountain of Life
In the foreground: a hexagonal fountain with water flowing from it (Revelation 22:1 — "the river of the water of life").

## The Just Judges — The Stolen Panel
The lower-left panel depicting the Just Judges on horseback was **stolen in 1934** and never recovered. A 1945 copy by Jef Van der Veken is displayed instead. A ransom note was received; Bishop Coppejans paid the ransom but the panel was never returned. The thief took the secret to his grave in 1934.

## The Technical Innovation
The Ghent Altarpiece is significant in art history because it represents the *fullest early use of oil paint* — a technique the Van Eyck brothers did not invent but perfected. The optical effects of oil (glazes, reflections, light within color) allowed them to paint:
- Individual hairs in beards
- Reflected text in the Virgin's crown
- The specific species of plants in the lower panels (botanically identifiable)
- The texture of different fabrics

This level of naturalistic detail was revolutionary and reflects a theological claim: the material world, created by God, is worth depicting with maximum fidelity.

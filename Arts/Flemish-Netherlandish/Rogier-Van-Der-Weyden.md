# Rogier van der Weyden — The Painter of Grief
*Many art historians consider him the greatest painter who ever lived*

## The Descent from the Cross (c. 1435)
**Museo del Prado, Madrid — P002825**

Ten figures in a shallow gold box — like a carved altarpiece made of paint. The body of Christ being lowered from the cross by Joseph of Arimathea, Nicodemus, and others.

**The theological key**: **Mary mirror-images Christ**. Her swooning body curves in exact parallel to her dead son's — she dies in sympathy. John catches both of them. The *cor*responding postures say: the mother dies with the son; the Mater Dolorosa participates in the passion.

**The compression**: The figures have no depth to retreat into — they press forward, almost into the viewer's space. Grief is unavoidable. You cannot stand at a comfortable distance.

**Scale**: Nearly life-size figures (2.2 × 2.6 meters). The figures would be human if they stepped off the panel.

**Detail**: The crystal tears on Mary Magdalene's face; the broken fingers of Christ's hand; the wound in his side still open. Rogier refuses sentimentality while refusing brutality — the suffering is absolute and dignified simultaneously.

## The Last Judgment Altarpiece (1446–1452)
**Hôtel-Dieu de Beaune, Beaune, France — in situ**

Commissioned by Nicolas Rolin (Chancellor of Burgundy) for the hospital he founded. The altarpiece was visible to the dying in their beds — the last thing they saw.

**The program**: 
- **Christ the Judge** (center, top): seated on a rainbow; wounds visible; both judging and showing mercy
- **The Archangel Michael** (center, middle): weighing souls; a demon tries to tip the scales
- **The Blessed** (left): rising peacefully, processing toward the golden gates of heaven
- **The Damned** (right): falling, screaming, clawing each other, tumbling toward hell's flames

**The theological context**: Commissioned for a *hospital* — the dying saw the Last Judgment. The implicit message: how you have treated the sick (Matthew 25:36: "I was sick and you visited me") will determine your fate.

## Other Major Works

### Saint Luke Drawing the Virgin (c. 1435–1440)
**Boston Museum of Fine Arts — 93.153**; multiple versions in various museums. St. Luke drawing the Virgin from life — Luke as the patron saint of painters; the act of painting sacred subjects as a holy vocation.

### The Crucifixion Triptych (c. 1443–1445)
**Kunsthistorisches Museum, Vienna — GG 901**. The mourning figures under the cross; white and red backgrounds (the extremity of grief vs. the blood of atonement).

# The Reformation, Iconoclasm, and Protestant Art
*The most powerful visual statement in the Reformation is an absence*

## The Iconoclasm

### What Happened
Beginning in 1521–1525, Reformed and radical Protestant movements systematically destroyed religious images:
- **Zürich (1523–1524)**: Zwingli supervised the removal of all images from Zürich's churches; altarpieces stripped; frescoes whitewashed; statues smashed
- **Wittenberg (1521)**: Karlstadt (without Luther's approval) led iconoclastic riots; Luther preached against it on his return — "the heart, not the hand"
- **Basel, Bern, Strasbourg (1529)**: systematic removal
- **Netherlands (1566 — the Beeldenstorm)**: the "image storm" — crowds attacked churches; statues, altarpieces, relics destroyed; churches stripped in hours
- **England (1536–1547, Henry VIII)**: dissolution of the monasteries; systematic destruction of shrines and images
- **England (1641–1660, Civil War/Puritan)**: Parliamentary soldiers and Puritans destroyed thousands of medieval church furnishings

**Scale of loss**: The English Reformation alone destroyed more medieval art than any event since the Roman Empire's fall. Most of England's medieval wall paintings were whitewashed (later discovered in the 19th–20th centuries). Most altarpieces, screens, and statues were smashed.

## The Theological Argument

### Against Images (Calvin, Zwingli)
- The Second Commandment: "You shall not make for yourself an image" (Exodus 20:4)
- Images encourage false notions of the divine
- God cannot be depicted; any depiction distorts
- The **Regulative Principle**: only what Scripture commands is permitted in worship
- "True religion... is not about seeing, but hearing" (Calvin)

### For Images (Luther, Anglican)
- The Second Commandment refers to idols worshiped, not images for instruction
- Images serve as "the Bible of the illiterate" (Gregory the Great)
- The Incarnation sanctified material representation
- Iconoclasm is itself violent — "it offends the weak"

## The Whitewashed Church as Theology
The stripped, whitewashed Reformed or Puritan church is as theologically loaded as the Baroque Catholic church:
- **Sola Scriptura** made visible: only the Word (spoken/read); no competing visual claims
- **The priesthood of all believers**: no priestly zone separated from the laity
- **Equality**: no expensive tombs, expensive altarpieces, expensive windows — all equal before God
- **Anti-idolatry**: the material world is not a vehicle of grace

The Dutch Reformed interior (Pieter Saenredam's paintings of empty whitewashed churches, c. 1630–1660) is one of the most powerful images in Western art precisely because of what it removes.

## Protestant Art After the Reformation

### What Survived
Protestant tradition retained legitimate art forms:
- **Portrait painting**: the Reformation's explosion of individual dignity; faces matter; Luther, Melanchthon, Calvin all sat for portraits
- **Biblical narrative**: Rembrandt's 600+ biblical works; Lucas Cranach's Reformation paintings
- **Landscape**: Calvinist theology of creation; the natural world as God's "other book"
- **Music**: Luther's hymnody; Bach's cantatas — art redirected from visual to sonic

### Rembrandt's Answer
Rembrandt was Dutch Reformed — worshiped in churches that had been stripped of art. His answer: biblical subjects in paint, but for private homes and collectors, not churches. The "private devotional painting" becomes the Protestant solution — sacred subjects in secular spaces.

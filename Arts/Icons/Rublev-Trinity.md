# Trinity Icon (Andrei Rublev, c. 1410)
*The greatest theological image in the Christian tradition*

## The Subject: Genesis 18
Three angels visit Abraham at Mamre; he offers them hospitality; they announce the birth of Isaac. The entire Christian tradition reads this as a pre-Incarnation appearance of the Trinity.

Rublev paints not the narrative but the **theological meaning** behind it: the three divine Persons in eternal communion, bending toward each other, sharing the chalice.

## The Composition: A Complete Trinitarian Theology

**The three figures**:
- **Left** (Father? — position of authority): blue robe under golden mantle; face averted; the source who gives
- **Center** (Son): darker garments; face turned toward the Father; the one sent
- **Right** (Spirit): green robe; bowed head; completing the gesture

The figures' **heads** bow toward each other and toward the chalice on the table — creating a circular movement of mutual self-giving.

**The empty space**: At the table's near edge — facing the viewer — there is a *rectangular notch*. In the original, this was a mirror insert. The viewer looks into the Trinity and sees themselves. The icon *invites* participation.

**The chalice**: On the table, a cup. The head of the sacrificial animal on the plate. The Eucharistic interpretation: the eternal self-offering of the Trinity enacted in the Eucharist; the viewer invited to the table.

## The Colors
Each figure's colors encode theological meaning:
- **Blue**: divinity/eternity
- **Gold**: divine glory
- **Purple/dark blue**: royal dignity
- **Green**: life, the Holy Spirit, nature

## Henri Nouwen on the Trinity Icon
Nouwen spent time before this icon at a monastery in Rome. His reflection: "I began to see that the icon was an invitation to enter into the divine being itself." He wrote an entire book (*Behold the Beauty of the Lord*) meditating on this and three other icons.

# Christ Pantocrator — The Icon of Icons
*The face of Christ — a Chalcedonian theology in paint*

## What "Pantocrator" Means
Greek: *pantokrator* — "Ruler of All" or "Almighty." In the Septuagint, it translates *Sabaoth* ("Lord of Hosts"). In Christian iconography, it became the standard image of Christ as cosmic ruler.

## The Theological Content
The Pantocrator icon encodes Chalcedonian Christology (two natures, one person):
- The **face is asymmetrical**: one side more divine (serene, transcendent), one more human (sorrowful, engaged)
- The **right hand**: raised in blessing — three fingers together (Trinity), two extended (two natures of Christ)
- The **left hand**: holds a Gospel book — Christ as the living Word
- The **gold background**: eternity; outside time
- The **direct gaze**: Christ looks at the viewer; the icon is not a window into the past but an encounter with the present risen Christ

## The Oldest Pantocrator (Sinai, c. 550 AD)
The earliest surviving Pantocrator panel is at Saint Catherine's Monastery, Sinai — painted before the iconoclast controversy, preserving a pre-controversy tradition. It shows the asymmetrical face technique already fully developed.

## In the Dome
In Byzantine churches, the Pantocrator typically occupies the **dome** — the highest point, looking down over the congregation. The theological program: Christ as cosmic ruler encompasses the entire worship space; the congregation stands beneath his gaze.

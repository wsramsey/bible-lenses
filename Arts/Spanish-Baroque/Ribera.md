# José de Ribera — Theology of Suffering and Old Age
*Working-class saints; peasant faces on apostles; the body in extremis*

## The Theological Vision
Ribera (Spanish-born, worked in Naples) painted suffering as spiritual truth. His saints are:
- Old — wrinkled, spotted, sagging
- Poor — rough hands, worn clothing
- In pain — genuinely, not decoratively

This is a theological statement: the divine encounters human beings in their full bodily reality, not in idealized forms.

## Major Works

### The Martyrdom of Saint Bartholomew (1634)
**Prado P001101**
Bartholomew was flayed alive — skinned. Ribera does not flinch: the ropes are tied; the knife is present; the face shows genuine agony. But Bartholomew's eyes look upward — not resigned, but genuinely looking toward something. The martyrdom is not defeat but witness.

### Saint Jerome (multiple versions)
Jerome in the desert, aged, emaciated, books and skull. Where Caravaggio's Jerome is sensual and vigorous, Ribera's is truly old — veins, spots, the body failing. The contemplative life *in* the body, not escaping it.

### Jacob and Esau
**Prado** — Jacob's hands, smooth from deception; Esau's hairy arm. The tactile detail renders the biblical deception physical and immediate.

### The Clubfoot (1642)
**Louvre — RF 2081**
A begging boy with a deformed foot holds out a piece of paper (his begging license). He grins. The landscape behind him is sunlit and beautiful. 

This is not a religious painting in the obvious sense — but it is a theological statement: this child is made in the image of God; his grin is not ironic but genuine; the landscape celebrates the same light that falls on him. The Counter-Reformation option for the poor.

# Pietà (Michelangelo, 1498–1499)
*The theology of the Incarnation in marble — God held in human arms*

## The Commission
Cardinal Jean de Bilhères commissioned the work for his funeral monument in St. Peter's. Michelangelo was 23 years old. It remains the only work he ever signed (on the sash across Mary's chest — according to legend, after hearing someone attribute it to another sculptor).

## The Composition
Mary holds the dead body of Christ across her lap. The theological challenges Michelangelo solved:
- **Scale**: A grown man cannot realistically fit across a seated woman's lap — Michelangelo enlarged Mary, used flowing drapery to create a pyramidal base, and arranged Christ's body diagonally
- **Mary's youth**: Mary appears younger than Christ — Michelangelo reportedly said pure souls do not age; she bears the perpetual youth of sinlessness
- **Christ's body**: Not broken or contorted (unlike Grünewald's) — peaceful, noble, beautiful; the Incarnation's dignity preserved even in death

## The Theology
The *Pietà* (Italian for "pity/compassion") encodes multiple theological claims:
- **The Incarnation is real**: God's body was held by human hands; matter matters
- **The Mater Dolorosa**: Mary as the grieving mother; "a sword will pierce your own soul" (Simeon, Luke 2:35) — fulfilled
- **Kenosis**: God emptied (Philippians 2:7) — here absolutely; the Creator held by the creature
- **The Eucharistic offering**: Mary presenting the Body of Christ — an eternal priestly gesture

## The 1972 Attack
On May 21, 1972, Laszlo Toth attacked the Pietà with a hammer shouting "I am Jesus Christ!" Fifteen blows before being subdued; Mary's arm, nose, and eye damaged. Now behind bulletproof glass.

The attack — a madman claiming to be Christ attacking an image of Christ held by his mother — has its own dark theological resonance.

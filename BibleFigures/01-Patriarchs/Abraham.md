# Abraham
*Genesis 12–25 · Father of Faith / Father of Many Nations*

## In the Biblical Story
Abraham (originally Abram = "exalted father"; renamed Abraham = "father of many nations") is the pivot of the entire biblical narrative:

- **Genesis 12** — the call: "Go from your country... to the land I will show you"; the covenant promises: land, descendants, blessing to all nations
- **Genesis 15** — the covenant ceremony: God walks through the divided animals alone; Abraham believes and it is "credited to him as righteousness" (*tsedeqah*) — the verse Paul will build an entire theology on
- **Genesis 17** — circumcision as covenant sign
- **Genesis 18** — Abraham intercedes for Sodom; the remarkable argument with God
- **Genesis 22** — the Akedah (binding of Isaac); "Take your son, your only son, whom you love..."
- **Genesis 23–25** — death and burial; the covenant continues through Isaac

## The Bigger Story
Abraham is the hinge between the primeval history (universal, shadowy) and the particular covenant history of Israel. God's response to the universal problem of sin (Genesis 3–11) is not a program but a person — Abraham, through whom "all families of the earth shall be blessed."

**The New Testament's use of Abraham:**
- **Paul (Romans 4, Galatians 3)** — Abraham was justified by faith *before* circumcision; therefore faith justifies, not works of the Law; Abraham is the father of all who believe, not just Jews
- **James 2** — "Abraham was justified by works when he offered his son Isaac" — the apparent contradiction with Paul is the most debated passage in the NT
- **Hebrews 11** — Abraham as the model of faith that acts without seeing
- **John 8:56** — "Your father Abraham rejoiced at the thought of seeing my day; he saw it and was glad"

## Historical/Archaeological Context
- **The Bronze Age context** — Abraham's customs (wife-sister deception, servant inheritance, adoption) fit 2nd millennium BC Mesopotamian legal patterns
- **No direct archaeological evidence** for Abraham as an individual
- **Mari texts** (18th c. BC) mention tribal groups with similar names and customs
- **The route** — Ur of the Chaldees → Haran → Canaan matches known Bronze Age migration patterns
- **Hebron/Mamre** — the Cave of Machpelah at Hebron, where Abraham buried Sarah, is still a holy site (now under a mosque built over a Byzantine church)

## What the Theologians Have Said
**[[Paul (Romans 4)]]** — Abraham is the father of faith for both Jews and Gentiles; circumcision came *after* faith, proving faith is the basis of righteousness; Abraham believed God and it was counted as righteousness.

**[[Augustine of Hippo]]** — Abraham's faith as the model of the elect; predestination; the City of God is traced through Abraham's line.

**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — The covenant with Abraham is the foundation of the "old law"; the moral content of the old law is natural law; Abraham's circumcision anticipated baptism as the sign of covenant membership.

**[[Martin Luther]]** — Abraham's justification by faith (Genesis 15:6 / Romans 4) is the paradigm case for *sola fide*; Abraham did not earn righteousness — it was credited to him.

**[[Søren Kierkegaard]]** — *Fear and Trembling*: the Akedah (binding of Isaac) is the supreme illustration of the "teleological suspension of the ethical"; Abraham's faith cannot be justified by ethics or reason — it is a leap into the absurd; the "knight of faith."

**[[Karl Barth]]** — Abraham is elected in and through Christ; his faith is a response to the prior covenant grace of God; election is not individual fate but participation in God's purpose.

**[[N.T. Wright]]** — Abraham's story is Israel's story in miniature; the promise "in you all nations will be blessed" is not a prediction but a *commission* — Israel's vocation was always to be the means of universal blessing; Jesus is the faithful Israelite who finally fulfills that vocation.

**[[Walter Brueggemann]]** — Abraham as the model of the wandering sojourner; the land promises and their ambiguity; the theology of promise that sustains the vulnerable.

## Key Theological Debates
- **Paul vs. James on justification**: Was Abraham justified by faith alone (Paul) or by works (James)? Luther: Paul speaks of initial justification, James of its evidence. Catholic: both are necessary.
- **The Akedah**: Does God command child sacrifice? (No — he stops it.) What does this reveal about God? About faith? Kierkegaard's "teleological suspension" vs. the simpler "faith and obedience."
- **Historical vs. literary Abraham**: Does the theology require a historical individual?

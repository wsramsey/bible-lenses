# David
*1 Samuel 16 – 1 Kings 2 · The Shepherd-King / Type of the Messiah*

## In the Biblical Story
David is the most fully developed character in the OT — his story spans from teenage shepherd to dying king, encompassing his entire inner and outer life:

- **1 Samuel 16** — anointed by Samuel; "The LORD looks at the heart"
- **1 Samuel 17** — Goliath; the stone and the sling
- **1 Samuel 18–26** — the friendship with Jonathan; Saul's jealousy; years as a fugitive
- **2 Samuel 2–5** — David becomes king of all Israel; captures Jerusalem
- **2 Samuel 7** — the Davidic Covenant: "Your house and your kingdom will endure forever before me; your throne will be established forever"; the covenant that all messianic hope flows through
- **2 Samuel 11–12** — Bathsheba; Uriah's murder; Nathan's rebuke: "You are the man"; Psalm 51
- **2 Samuel 13–18** — Absalom's revolt; David's flight; the death of Absalom; "O Absalom, my son!"
- **1 Kings 1–2** — David's death and Solomon's succession

## The Psalms
David is credited with composing roughly half the 150 Psalms — the most personal window into an OT figure's inner life. The Psalms of lament (22, 51, 69, 88) map the full range of human experience before God.

## The Bigger Story
The **Davidic Covenant** (2 Samuel 7) is the theological hinge of the OT's second half. God promises an eternal dynasty — an heir of David who will reign forever. Every subsequent king is measured against this promise. The prophets (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Zechariah) keep the promise alive through the exile.

The New Testament begins: "Jesus Christ, the son of David, the son of Abraham" (Matthew 1:1). Jesus is the fulfillment of the Davidic covenant.

## Archaeological Confirmation
The **Tel Dan Stele** (c. 841 BC), discovered in 1993, contains the phrase "House of David" — the first extrabiblical confirmation of David's dynasty. **The Mesha Stele** also likely references the Davidic dynasty.

## What the Theologians Have Said
**[[Augustine of Hippo]]** — David as the type of Christ the king; his repentance (Psalm 51) as the model of Christian contrition; the City of God traced through the Davidic line.

**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — David's adultery and murder do not invalidate God's covenant promises; the covenant is grace, not reward; David's repentance as the model of the virtue of penance.

**[[Martin Luther]]** — David's Psalms, especially the penitential psalms, are Luther's constant companions; Psalm 46 ("A Mighty Fortress") is his own composition in the Davidic tradition.

**[[John Calvin]]** — David as the model of pastoral leadership; his Psalms commentary is Calvin's most personal work — he saw his own exiles and persecutions in David's story.

**[[Walter Brueggemann]]** — *David and His Theologian*; *The Message of the Psalms*; David's narrative as a study in power, ambiguity, and covenant; the psalms of orientation, disorientation, and new orientation.

**[[N.T. Wright]]** — Jesus as the climactic fulfillment of the Davidic covenant; the "son of David" title is not merely a genealogical claim but a covenantal-royal claim; the cross as the unexpected way David's greater son establishes his kingdom.

## Key Theological Debates
- **David's sin**: How do we read the Bathsheba-Uriah episode? Nathan's "you are the man" as prophetic truth-telling; the consequences unfold throughout the rest of his reign
- **The Davidic Covenant**: Conditional or unconditional? The prophets seem to apply both
- **Messianism**: How directly do texts like 2 Samuel 7 and Psalm 110 point to Jesus?
- **The Psalms' authorship**: Some critical scholars doubt Davidic authorship of most psalms; but the canonical tradition matters theologically

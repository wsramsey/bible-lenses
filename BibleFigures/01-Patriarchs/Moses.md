# Moses
*Exodus–Deuteronomy · The Lawgiver / Type of Christ as Prophet*

## In the Biblical Story
Moses dominates four books of the Pentateuch — the most words devoted to any individual in the OT after possibly David:

- **Exodus 1–2** — birth, hidden in basket, rescued by Pharaoh's daughter, kills Egyptian, flees to Midian
- **Exodus 3** — the burning bush; "I AM WHO I AM"; the call to lead Israel out of Egypt
- **Exodus 7–12** — the ten plagues; Passover
- **Exodus 14** — the crossing of the Red Sea
- **Exodus 19–20** — Mount Sinai; the Ten Commandments; the covenant
- **Exodus 32–34** — the golden calf; Moses intercedes; sees God's back; "the LORD, the LORD, compassionate and gracious..."
- **Numbers** — wilderness wanderings; rebellion; judgment
- **Deuteronomy** — Moses's farewell speeches; dies on Mount Nebo within sight of the Promised Land

## The Bigger Story
Moses is the mediator of the Mosaic Covenant — the constitution of Israel as a nation. The Law he receives at Sinai is the central revelation of the OT.

**Jesus and Moses:**
- Matthew's Gospel structures Jesus's teaching in five discourses (like the five books of Moses); Jesus is the new Moses
- The Transfiguration: Moses and Elijah appear with Jesus — Law and Prophets fulfilled
- John 1:17 — "The Law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ"
- Hebrews 3 — Jesus is greater than Moses as a son is greater than a servant

## Historical/Archaeological Context
- **The Exodus dating** — two main views: c. 1446 BC (early date, 1 Kings 6:1) or c. 1270s BC (late date, Ramesses II as Pharaoh)
- **No Egyptian records** of the Exodus or Moses — absence of evidence is not evidence of absence in ancient historiography; Egypt systematically erased defeats
- **The Merneptah Stele (1208 BC)** — the earliest extrabiblical reference to "Israel" as a people in Canaan; establishes Israel was in Canaan by 1208 BC
- **Tell el-Borg** — possible site of Israelite forced labor in the eastern Nile Delta

## What the Theologians Have Said
**[[Philo of Alexandria]]** — Moses as the ideal philosopher-king-prophet; his account is deeply influenced by Platonism; Moses as the "the most perfect of men" who ascended to the divine; hugely influential on early Christian exegesis.

**[[Origen of Alexandria]]** — Moses's encounter with the burning bush prefigures the Incarnation; the Exodus is the type of spiritual liberation; allegorical reading throughout.

**[[Gregory of Nyssa]]** — *The Life of Moses* is his masterpiece of mystical theology: Moses's progressive ascent into the cloud (darkness/unknowing) at Sinai models the mystical life; *epektasis* — endless progress into the infinite God.

**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — Moses as the greatest OT prophet; his face-to-face encounter with God was a unique prophetic vision; the Mosaic Law is the highest expression of natural law until Christ.

**[[Martin Luther]]** — The Law of Moses condemns; it reveals sin but cannot save; the Ten Commandments as the summary of natural law; Moses as the great pedagogue who drives us to Christ.

**[[John Calvin]]** — The Mosaic Law has three uses: civic, pedagogic (drives to Christ), normative (guide for Christian life); the third use is Calvin's distinctive contribution.

**[[Karl Barth]]** — Moses is the prophet of the covenant; his intercession for Israel after the golden calf is the paradigm of prophetic intercession; the Law is the form of the Gospel, not its opposite.

**[[N.T. Wright]]** — Moses and the Exodus are the master story that Jesus recapitulates; the new Exodus is the deepest structure of the Gospel narratives; Jesus is the new Moses leading a new Exodus from the slavery of sin and death.

## Key Theological Debates
- **The Law's status for Christians**: Fulfilled/abolished (Luther) vs. three uses (Calvin) vs. the whole Torah reinterpreted through Christ (Wright)
- **Historical Exodus**: Did it happen? At what scale? The debate among archaeologists and biblical scholars
- **Moses's encounter with God**: What did "face to face" mean? How can finite humans encounter infinite God? Gregory of Nyssa's answer: in the cloud, not in the light

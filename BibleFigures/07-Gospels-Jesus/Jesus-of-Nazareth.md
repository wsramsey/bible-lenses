# Jesus of Nazareth
*The Gospels · The Center of Everything*

> This note provides the historical and cross-reference framework. The theological claims about Jesus are documented throughout the entire vault.

## Historical Attestation
Jesus of Nazareth is one of the best-attested figures of the ancient world:

**Jewish sources:**
- **Josephus** (*Antiquities* 18.3.3) — the *Testimonium Flavianum*; probably partially authentic: "a wise man... the Christ... appeared to them on the third day alive again"
- **Josephus** (*Antiquities* 20.9.1) — "James the brother of Jesus who was called Christ" — widely considered authentic

**Roman sources:**
- **Tacitus** (*Annals* 15.44) — "Christus, from whom the name had its origin, suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilatus"
- **Pliny the Younger** (c. 112 AD) — Christians sing hymns "to Christ as to a god"
- **Suetonius** (*Claudius* 25.4) — Jews expelled from Rome for disturbances at the instigation of "Chrestus"

## The Primary Sources: The Gospels
- **Mark** (c. 65–70 AD) — oldest Gospel; fast-paced; "immediately"; the passion narrative as its center
- **Matthew** (c. 80–90 AD) — structured for Jewish audience; five discourses; "fulfill the Law"
- **Luke** (c. 80–90 AD) — most literary; concern for the poor, women, Gentiles; Luke-Acts as a unified work
- **John** (c. 90–100 AD) — most theological; "In the beginning was the Word"; "I AM" sayings; the Beloved Disciple

## The "Third Quest" for the Historical Jesus
**E.P. Sanders**, **N.T. Wright**, **John Meier**, **Marcus Borg**, **Bart Ehrman** — the major scholars who have reconstructed the historical Jesus:
- Sanders: apocalyptic prophet announcing the kingdom of God
- Wright: *Jesus and the Victory of God* — Jesus understood himself as Israel's representative, enacting the return from exile
- Ehrman: Jewish apocalypticist who expected the imminent end of the age; disappointed his followers by dying
- Meier: the "marginal Jew" — Jewish, miracle-worker, teacher, prophet, executed by Rome

## What Makes Jesus Unique in the Biblical Story
Every figure in the OT points forward to Jesus — this is the NT's own claim:
- **Adam** — Christ as second Adam (Romans 5, 1 Cor 15)
- **Abraham** — Christ as the seed of Abraham in whom all nations are blessed (Gal 3)
- **Moses** — Christ as the new Moses, giving a new law from a mountain (Matthew 5)
- **David** — Christ as the Son of David, fulfilling the eternal covenant (2 Sam 7)
- **Isaiah's Servant** — Christ as the one pierced for our transgressions (Isa 53)
- **The Temple** — Christ's body as the true temple (John 2:19–21)
- **The Passover Lamb** — Christ as the Lamb of God (John 1:29)

## What the Theologians Have Said
*(Cross-references to other notes — every theologian in the vault is fundamentally a theologian of Jesus)*

**[[Council of Nicaea (325)]]** — Jesus is *homoousios* (of the same substance) with the Father; fully divine
**[[Council of Chalcedon (451)]]** — Jesus is one person in two natures; fully divine and fully human; without confusion, without change, without division, without separation

**[[Anselm of Canterbury]]** — *Cur Deus Homo*: Why did God become human? Because only one who is fully human can represent humanity; only one who is fully divine can make infinite satisfaction for infinite offense.

**[[Abelard]]** — The moral influence theory: the cross demonstrates God's love and motivates our response.

**[[Karl Barth]]** — Jesus Christ is the one Word of God; the election of Jesus Christ is the election of all humanity in him; revelation is not a doctrine but a person.

**[[N.T. Wright]]** — *Jesus and the Victory of God*: Jesus understood himself as the climax of Israel's story, enacting the return from exile and defeating the real enemy (sin and death, not Rome) through his death.

**[[René Girard]]** — Jesus is the innocent scapegoat who exposes the scapegoat mechanism; the cross is not a sacrifice that satisfies God but the revelation that God is always on the side of the victim.

**[[Bart Ehrman]]** — Jesus was a Jewish apocalyptic prophet who expected the imminent end; he was wrong about the timing; his followers transformed his failure into a resurrection theology.

## Key Theological Debates
The entirety of Christian theology is an extended debate about Jesus:
- **His identity**: Fully divine? Fully human? Both? How?
- **His death**: What did it accomplish? Substitution? Satisfaction? Moral example? Victory over evil? Revelation of God's love?
- **His resurrection**: Bodily? Spiritual? Historical event? Transformed body?
- **His return**: When? How? What will it look like?

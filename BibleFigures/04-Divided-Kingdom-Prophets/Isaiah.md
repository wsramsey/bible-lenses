# Isaiah
*Isaiah · The Evangelical Prophet / The Fifth Gospel*

## In the Biblical Story
Isaiah ("The LORD saves") prophesied in Jerusalem during the reigns of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah — roughly 740–700 BC — one of the most politically turbulent periods in Israel's history:
- The Northern Kingdom (Israel) fell to Assyria in 722 BC
- Sennacherib besieged Jerusalem in 701 BC; Isaiah prophesied the city would be spared — it was

**Isaiah's call** (Isaiah 6): The throne room vision; "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD Almighty"; Isaiah's lips touched by a burning coal; "Here am I — send me."

**The book of Isaiah** is the longest prophetic book (66 chapters) and the most cited by NT authors.

## The Great Isaiah Scroll
The **Great Isaiah Scroll** from Qumran (Dead Sea Scrolls, c. 100 BC) is the oldest complete manuscript of any biblical book. It confirms that the text we have today is essentially identical to what was read in the 1st century.

## The Servant Songs (Isaiah 42, 49, 50, 52–53)
The most theologically significant section of the prophetic literature:
- "Here is my servant, whom I uphold, my chosen one in whom I delight" (42:1)
- "It was our infirmities he carried... he was pierced for our transgressions" (53:4–5)
- "By his wounds we are healed" (53:5)
- "He was led like a lamb to the slaughter" (53:7)

The NT quotes Isaiah 53 more than almost any other OT passage to interpret the cross.

## The Authorship Debate
**Deutero-Isaiah theory**: Most critical scholars argue chapters 40–66 were written by one or two anonymous authors during and after the Babylonian exile (550–520 BC), not by the 8th century Isaiah. The evidence: the specific naming of Cyrus (44:28), the context of Babylonian exile rather than Assyrian threat, the different vocabulary and themes.

**Conservative response**: Predictive prophecy is possible; the NT quotes "Isaiah" from both halves as the same author; the Dead Sea Scrolls show no division.

## What the Theologians Have Said
**[[Origen of Alexandria]]** — Isaiah 6 as the supreme OT vision of the Trinity; the seraphim crying "holy" three times; the prophet's cleansing as a type of baptism.

**[[Augustine of Hippo]]** — called Isaiah "the fifth evangelist" for the clarity of his messianic prophecies; the book of Isaiah as the OT's own Gospel.

**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — Isaiah 53 is the most explicit OT prediction of the passion; the "wounds" by which we are healed are Christ's; the atonement as substitution is implicit here.

**[[Martin Luther]]** — Isaiah is Luther's favorite OT prophet; "comfort, comfort my people" (40:1) is the key to understanding the Gospel's relation to the Law; the Servant Songs are the heart of the book.

**[[Karl Barth]]** — Isaiah 53 as the paradigmatic text for understanding substitutionary atonement; "the Judge judged in our place"; Israel's suffering and the Servant's suffering are theologically linked.

**[[N.T. Wright]]** — Isaiah 40–55 is the controlling narrative for understanding Jesus's mission; the "new Exodus" from Babylon is the framework; Jesus is the Servant who fulfills Israel's vocation by bearing its sin.

**[[Brevard Childs]]** — canonical reading: the two-part structure of Isaiah (judgment 1–39, comfort 40–66) must be read as a unified whole; critical questions about authorship are secondary to the canonical shape.

## Key Theological Debates
- **Deutero-Isaiah**: One book or two/three? This is the most important OT critical question
- **Servant's identity**: The Servant as collective Israel, individual prophet, ideal future figure, or Christ? NT: primarily Christ; Jewish tradition: Israel
- **Predictive prophecy**: Does Isaiah 44:28 name Cyrus 150 years before his birth?

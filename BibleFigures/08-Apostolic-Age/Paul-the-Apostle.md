# Paul of Tarsus
*Acts 9–28, The Pauline Letters · The Apostle to the Gentiles*

## In the Biblical Story
Paul (Saul of Tarsus) is the most influential figure in early Christianity after Jesus:

- **Acts 7–8** — present at Stephen's stoning; "consenting to his death"
- **Acts 9** — Damascus road: "Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?"; blinded; converted; "a chosen instrument"
- **Acts 13–21** — three missionary journeys; cities across the Mediterranean; founding churches
- **Acts 21–28** — arrest in Jerusalem; imprisonment; appeal to Caesar; voyage to Rome
- **Tradition** — martyred in Rome under Nero, c. 64–68 AD; beheaded (as a Roman citizen)

## The Letters
Paul wrote roughly half the New Testament. The undisputed letters (Romans, 1-2 Corinthians, Galatians, Philippians, 1 Thessalonians, Philemon) are among the most theologically dense texts in any literature.

**Romans** — his systematic account of the Gospel: the righteousness of God revealed in the Gospel; justification by faith; Jew and Gentile in God's purposes; life in the Spirit; the destiny of Israel

**Galatians** — "the Magna Carta of Christian liberty"; against the Judaizers; "There is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female"

**Philippians** — the Christ hymn (2:6–11): Jesus "did not consider equality with God something to be grasped"; the kenosis

## What the Theologians Have Said
**[[Augustine of Hippo]]** — Paul's letters (especially Romans) shaped Augustine's theology of grace, predestination, and original sin more than any other text. Romans 9 is the foundation of his predestinarianism.

**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — *Commentary on Romans* is one of Aquinas's most important works; he reconciles Paul's justification language with the sacramental system through his distinction between justification (initial) and meriting (ongoing).

**[[Martin Luther]]** — Romans 1:17 and Galatians are the texts that broke Luther open; "the righteousness of God" is not God's punishing standard but the righteousness he *gives* through faith; *sola fide* is Paul's doctrine.

**[[John Calvin]]** — Paul as the supreme interpreter of the Gospel; Calvin wrote commentaries on almost all the Pauline letters; his *Institutes* is organized around Pauline categories.

**[[Krister Stendahl]]** — "The Apostle Paul and the Introspective Conscience of the West": Paul didn't have a guilty conscience problem; the Lutheran reading of Paul through guilt is anachronistic; Paul's question was about Jew-Gentile relations, not individual salvation.

**[[E.P. Sanders]]** — *Paul and Palestinian Judaism* (1977): Judaism was not a religion of works-righteousness; Paul was not converting from law-keeping to grace; he was responding to the question of how Gentiles enter the covenant.

**[[N.T. Wright]]** — *Paul and the Faithfulness of God*: Paul was a Second Temple Jew who had a world-view transformed by the resurrection; his "justification" language is covenantal, not primarily about individual salvation; the "righteousness of God" is God's covenant faithfulness.

**[[James D.G. Dunn]]** — coined "New Perspective on Paul"; "works of the Law" in Paul refers primarily to Jewish identity markers (circumcision, dietary laws, Sabbath), not moral achievement.

**[[Bart Ehrman]]** — Paul transformed Jesus's message; Jesus proclaimed the kingdom, Paul proclaimed Jesus; the historical Jesus and Paul's Christ are quite different figures.

## Key Theological Debates
The most debated figure in NT scholarship:
- **Justification**: What does Paul mean? Individual acquittal? Covenant membership? Both?
- **The New Perspective**: Did Sanders, Dunn, and Wright get Paul right? The debate continues
- **Paul and women**: 1 Corinthians 14:34–35 and 1 Timothy 2:12 — silencing of women; authentic Paul or not? How to interpret?
- **Paul and Judaism**: Supersessionism? Replacement? Covenant fulfillment? Completion?
- **The deutero-Pauline letters**: Which are authentic? (Ephesians, Colossians, Pastorals — debated)

# Mary Magdalene
*The Gospels · Apostle to the Apostles / First Resurrection Witness*

## In the Biblical Story
Mary from Magdala (a town on the Sea of Galilee) is:

- **Luke 8:2** — healed of "seven demons"; one of the women who supported Jesus's ministry
- **Mark 15:40–41, Matthew 27:55–56** — present at the crucifixion; watching from a distance
- **Mark 15:47, Matthew 27:61** — present at the burial; saw where Jesus was laid
- **Mark 16:1–8** — first at the empty tomb; the angel's message; "He is risen"
- **John 20:1–18** — the most extended resurrection appearance account: "Mary!" / "Rabboni!"; Jesus commissions her: "Go to my brothers and tell them..." — *apostola apostolorum* (apostle to the apostles)

## The Tradition's Errors and Corrections
**The "sinful woman" confusion** — Pope Gregory I (591 AD) conflated Mary Magdalene with the unnamed sinful woman of Luke 7 and with Mary of Bethany. This created the "prostitute" tradition that has no basis in Scripture.

In 1969, the Roman Catholic Church officially acknowledged Gregory's error and separated the three figures in the liturgical calendar.

## What the Theologians Have Said
**[[Gregory the Great]]** — the conflation sermon (591 AD) that created 1,400 years of misidentification; the prostitute interpretation was influential beyond any other patristic reading of Mary Magdalene.

**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — followed the Gregorian tradition; the penitent sinner narrative

**[[Phyllis Trible]]** and feminist scholarship — recovered Mary Magdalene as the primary resurrection witness; her commissioning by the risen Christ is an apostolic commissioning; the tradition erased a significant female apostle

**[[N.T. Wright]]** — the criterion of embarrassment: the NT's choice of women as the first resurrection witnesses is strong evidence for historicity; no one inventing the story in a 1st century Jewish context would choose women as primary witnesses (their testimony was legally discounted)

**[[Elizabeth Schüssler Fiorenza]]** — *In Memory of Her*: Mary Magdalene represents the women leaders of the early Jesus movement who were systematically marginalized as the church institutionalized

## Key Theological Debates
- **Who was Mary Magdalene?**: Not a prostitute; a prominent disciple and first resurrection witness
- **Was she an apostle?**: Eastern tradition calls her "equal to the apostles"; John 20 gives her a commission comparable to the other apostolic commissions
- **The Gospel of Mary**: 2nd-3rd century Gnostic text giving her a prominent teaching role; not canonical but historically significant

# The Early Roman Basilicas (4th century AD)
*Constantine's gifts to the church — the architectural language of Christianity*

## The Four Major Basilicas

### Saint John Lateran (San Giovanni in Laterano) — 314 AD
- **The Pope's cathedral** — "Mother of all churches" (*omnium urbis et orbis ecclesiarum mater et caput*)
- First Christian basilica; built by Constantine on land seized from the Lateran family
- Not St. Peter's — the Lateran is the Pope's actual cathedral; St. Peter's is the larger pilgrimage church
- Rebuilt after fires (1308, 1360); current interior by Borromini (1646)

### Saint Peter's Basilica (original) — c. 326 AD
- Constantine built the first St. Peter's over the traditional site of Peter's tomb
- A massive basilica — 120m long; held 3,000–4,000 worshipers
- Survived until Pope Julius II demolished it in 1506 to build the current basilica
- **The decision to demolish**: one of the most controversial in Christian history — a 1,200-year-old building destroyed; it triggered the Reformation (indulgences funded the replacement)

### Saint Paul Outside the Walls (San Paolo fuori le Mura) — c. 386 AD
- Built over the traditional site of Paul's martyrdom and burial
- Destroyed by fire 1823; rebuilt identically; current church is 19th century
- The **cloisters** (c. 1205–1214): among the finest medieval cloisters surviving in Rome

### Santa Maria Maggiore — c. 430–440 AD
- The oldest Marian basilica in the West
- **The Liberian Chronicle** records Pope Liberius (352–366) built it after a miraculous August snowfall on the Esquiline Hill — the **Feast of the Snow** (August 5) is still celebrated
- Original 5th-century mosaics survive in the nave and apse — among the oldest Christian mosaics in Rome
- The **Salus Populi Romani** icon: one of the most venerated Marian icons; attributed to Luke; probably 6th–13th century; Francis prays here before and after every papal journey

## The Basilica Form as Theology
Constantine adapted the **Roman basilica** (a civil court/marketplace building) for Christian worship. The form:
- **Nave**: long central hall (from Latin *navis* = ship — the church as the vessel of salvation)
- **Aisles**: flanking corridors
- **Apse**: semicircular end; the judge's throne became the bishop's throne (*cathedra*); the altar replaced the judge's table
- **Atrium**: forecourt; the unbaptized could wait here

The architectural choice was profound: the church chose the form of *justice* (the basilica) not the form of *religion* (the temple). The Gospel was framed as a legal/cosmic justice claim.

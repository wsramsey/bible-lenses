# Major Christian Sculptures and Monuments

## Michelangelo's Christian Sculpture

### Pietà (1498–1499) — St. Peter's, Vatican
→ See [[Pietà (Michelangelo)]]
The most famous Christian sculpture; Mary holding the dead Christ.

### David (1501–1504) — Accademia, Florence
→ See [[David (Michelangelo)]]
The shepherd boy before Goliath — courage in the face of overwhelming odds.

### Moses (c. 1513–1515) — San Pietro in Vincoli, Rome
For the tomb of Pope Julius II. Moses descends from Sinai with the tablets; his expression captures the moment he sees the Israelites worshiping the golden calf. **Horns** on his head: mistranslation of the Hebrew *karan* (rays of light) as *keren* (horns) in the Vulgate. **Scale**: over 2.4m tall; one of the most powerful sculptures in Western art.

### The Slaves / Prisoners (c. 1513–1530) — Louvre and Accademia
Four unfinished sculptures of figures struggling to emerge from marble — the "non finito" technique. Theological interpretation: the soul struggling free from the body; the unredeemed struggling toward grace.

## Gian Lorenzo Bernini's Sculpture

### The Ecstasy of Saint Teresa (1647–1652) — Santa Maria della Vittoria, Rome
Teresa of Ávila in mystical ecstasy, an angel's arrow piercing her heart. The most overtly sensual religious sculpture in the tradition — deliberately; Bernini visualizing Teresa's own description: "The pain was so great that I screamed aloud; but simultaneously I felt such infinite sweetness that I wished the pain to last eternally."
**Theological content**: mystical union as simultaneously painful and pleasurable; the dissolution of the self; divine love as overwhelming force.
**Museum**: In situ, Cornaro Chapel, Santa Maria della Vittoria, Rome.

### Apollo and Daphne (1622–1625) — Borghese Gallery, Rome
Technically mythological, but the Cardinal's inscription transformed it into a moral: "Whoever loves to pursue fleeting forms of pleasure will find only leaves and bitter berries in his hand." Secular subject, Christian moral.

## Donatello's Sculptures

### Mary Magdalene (c. 1455) — Museo dell'Opera del Duomo, Florence
A gaunt, emaciated figure in ragged clothing — the Magdalene after years of penitential desert asceticism. One of the most psychologically powerful religious sculptures ever made; the face combines horror and peace.
**Museum**: Museo dell'Opera del Duomo, Florence.

### David (c. 1440s) — Bargello Museum, Florence
First free-standing nude sculpture since antiquity; David after Goliath's decapitation. The triumph of youth and beauty over brute force.

## The Christ the Redeemer, Rio de Janeiro (1931)
- **Artist**: Paul Landowski (sculptor) and Heitor da Silva Costa (engineer)
- **Height**: 30m statue; 38m total on pedestal; 635m above sea level on Corcovado
- **UNESCO**: World Heritage Site (part of the Carioca Landscapes)
- **Theology**: Arms spread in welcome/embrace — the open-armed Christ welcoming all; the outstretched arms reference both the crucifixion and the invitation
- **Significance**: The most recognizable Christian monument in the 20th century; became the symbol of Brazil itself

## The Angel of the North (1998) — Gateshead, England
- **Artist**: Antony Gormley
- **Dimensions**: 20m tall; 54m wingspan (wider than a Boeing 757)
- **Material**: Steel; rusting
- Not explicitly Christian but placed at a mine entrance (the underworld) with wings spread upward — widely interpreted as a guardian angel over a former mining community
- Gormley: the figure "has no face because we don't know what angels look like"

## The Christus (1839–1844) — Thorvaldsen's Museum, Copenhagen; multiple replicas worldwide
- **Artist**: Bertel Thorvaldsen
- **Original**: Vor Frue Kirke (Church of Our Lady), Copenhagen
- **Famous replicas**: Temple Square, Salt Lake City (LDS); many Catholic churches worldwide
- Christ with arms open and hands raised, looking downward at the viewer; inscription: *Kommer til mig* ("Come unto me") — Matthew 11:28
- One of the most reproduced Christian sculptures of the 19th–20th centuries

## The Statue of Constantine, York (1998)
- At York Minster, on the spot where Constantine was proclaimed Emperor in 306 AD
- A seated, contemplative figure; crown at his feet; reading
- York's claim: "Here Christianity became the religion of the Roman Empire" — slightly exaggerated but not wrong
- **Museum**: In situ — York Minster exterior, south transept

## Monuments to Reformers

### Luther Monument, Worms (1868)
- **Artist**: Ernst Rietschel
- A bronze Luther stands at the center surrounded by his predecessors (Hus, Wycliffe, Savonarola, Reuchlin) and supporters
- Inscription: *Hier stehe ich. Ich kann nicht anders* ("Here I stand. I can do no other")
- **Museum**: Luther Ring, Worms, Germany — in situ

### Monument to the Reformation, Geneva (1917)
- **Artists**: Paul Landowski (same sculptor as Christ the Redeemer, Rio)
- 93m long bas-relief; central figures are Calvin, Farel, Beza, Knox
- Behind them, the motto of Geneva: *Post Tenebras Lux* ("After Darkness, Light")
- **Museum**: In situ — Parc des Bastions, Geneva

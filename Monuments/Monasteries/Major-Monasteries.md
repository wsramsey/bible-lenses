# Major Christian Monasteries — World Survey

## The Most Historically Significant

### Monte Cassino, Italy (529 AD)
- Founded by **[[Benedict of Nursia]]** — the monastery that launched Western monasticism
- Destroyed four times: Lombards (589), Saracens (883), earthquake (1349), WWII bombing (February 1944)
- Rebuilt each time; current building is post-WWII reconstruction
- **The WWII destruction**: the Allies believed it was being used as a German observation post; bombed it; it wasn't (the Germans moved into the ruins after); a tragic military and cultural loss
- Benedict's tomb is in the crypt

### Saint Catherine's Monastery, Sinai, Egypt (548 AD)
- Commissioned by **Justinian I** at the base of Mount Sinai (traditional site of Moses's burning bush and the giving of the Law)
- Continuously occupied since 548 AD — the oldest continuously inhabited Christian monastery
- **Never been sacked**: protected by the Muslim community (has a mosque inside the walls); claimed to have a document from Muhammad granting it protection
- Library: 3,300+ manuscripts — second only to the Vatican; the *Codex Sinaiticus* was found here
- The burning bush: a living bush in the courtyard, allegedly the original

### Iona, Scotland (563 AD)
- Founded by **[[Columba]]** (Colm Cille) — the Irish monk who re-evangelized Scotland and northern England
- The island from which Celtic Christianity spread; Aidan of Lindisfarne was trained here
- Repeatedly sacked by Vikings (795, 802, 806 — the Martyrs' Bay massacre killed 68 monks)
- Abandoned; restored by the Benedictines (c. 1200); ruined at the Reformation; restored again in 1938 by the **Iona Community** (ecumenical; George MacLeod)
- A pilgrimage destination for tens of thousands annually

### Mount Athos, Greece (9th century–present)
- The **Holy Mountain** — a peninsula in northern Greece; 20 monasteries; approximately 2,000 monks
- A self-governing monastic community within Greece; no women permitted (since at least the 10th century)
- Founded: the Great Lavra (963 AD) by Athanasius of Athos
- **Significance**: The living center of Orthodox hesychast spirituality; Palamas's theology was vindicated here; the monks' prayer sustains Orthodox civilization (in Orthodox theology)
- **Library**: collectively the most significant repository of Byzantine manuscripts in existence
- Access requires a special permit (*diamonitirion*)

### Cluny, France (910 AD)
- Founded by William I of Aquitaine; given absolute independence from local bishops and lay lords
- The most powerful monastic institution of medieval Europe; at its height the largest church building in Christendom
- The Cluniac Reform spread to 1,450 daughter houses across Europe
- **Mostly destroyed**: the French Revolution; 90% of the church demolished for building materials; only one transept arm survives

### Taizé, France (1940–present)
- Founded by Brother Roger Schutz during WWII; initially sheltering Jewish refugees
- An ecumenical monastic community (Catholic, Protestant, and Orthodox brothers)
- **The most visited Christian pilgrimage site in Europe**: 100,000+ young people annually come for the simple chant, silence, and community
- Taizé chant has spread to churches worldwide

### Gethsemani Abbey, Kentucky (1848)
- **Thomas Merton** entered here in 1941; *The Seven Storey Mountain* (1948) made it internationally famous
- Still the largest Trappist monastery in the Americas
- The abbey produces fruitcakes and other products (self-supporting through labor)

## Eastern Orthodox

### Meteora, Greece (14th–16th century)
- Six monasteries built on top of enormous rock pillars; accessible only by rope ladders (until 20th century)
- Built by monks fleeing Ottoman invasions
- UNESCO World Heritage Site; still inhabited by monks and nuns

### Kyiv-Pechersk Lavra (Cave Monastery), Ukraine (1051 AD)
- Founded by the monks Anthony and Theodosius
- **The Caves**: extensive tunnel system; mummified monks whose bodies did not decay — venerated as saints
- Crucial to Ukrainian Christian identity; bombed/seized by the Soviet Union; current status contested between Ukrainian Orthodox and Russian Orthodox

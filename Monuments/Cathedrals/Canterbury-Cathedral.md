# Canterbury Cathedral
*The mother church of the Anglican Communion — built on a martyr's blood*

## Foundation and Early History
**Augustine of Canterbury** arrived in 597 AD (sent by Gregory the Great); converted King Æthelberht; established the first cathedral. The present structure dates from 1070 (after the Norman Conquest).

## Thomas Becket (1170)
Archbishop Thomas Becket was murdered by four knights of Henry II on December 29, 1170 in the northwest transept — now the "Martyrdom" or "Altar of Sword's Point."

Henry had allegedly muttered "Who will rid me of this troublesome priest?" The knights took it literally. Becket's death created the most famous medieval English martyr and pilgrimage site.

**The Canterbury Pilgrimage**: Pilgrims flocked immediately; miracles were reported; Becket was canonized in 1173. Geoffrey Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* (c. 1387–1400) is structured around pilgrims traveling to Becket's shrine.

Henry II performed public penance — walking barefoot to the shrine, being scourged by monks.

## The Reformation
**Henry VIII** ordered Becket's shrine demolished (1538), his bones scattered, his name erased from service books. The cathedral survived but was stripped of its treasures.

**Thomas Cranmer**, Archbishop of Canterbury, was burned at the stake (1556) under Mary I — in Oxford, not Canterbury, but as the holder of the same office as Becket.

## The Architecture
- **The Corona** (circular east chapel): where Becket's skull was enshrined
- **The Crypt** (c. 1100): the oldest part; Norman Romanesque; still used for daily worship
- **The Choir** (1174–1184): rebuilt after a fire; the first major Gothic choir in England
- **The Trinity Chapel**: where the Becket shrine stood; the floor worn by pilgrims' knees; the stained glass windows depicting Becket's miracles (c. 1213–1220)

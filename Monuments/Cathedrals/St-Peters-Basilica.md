# St. Peter's Basilica (1506–1626)
*The center of the Catholic Church — built partly on indulgences that sparked the Reformation*

## The Irony of Its Construction
Pope Julius II demolished the original 4th-century basilica (built by Constantine) in 1506 to build the new St. Peter's. The project required enormous funding.

**Pope Leo X authorized the sale of indulgences** to fund the construction — specifically in Germany, sold by Johann Tetzel. In 1517, Luther wrote his 95 Theses partly in protest against these indulgences.

**The greatest building in the Catholic Church was partly funded by the very practice that split the church.**

## The Architecture
- **Dome**: Michelangelo's design (completed by Giacomo della Porta, 1590); 42.34m internal diameter; 136.57m to the top of the cross; still the tallest dome in the world
- **Interior**: 15,160 sq meters — can hold 60,000 standing
- **Bernini's Baldachin** (1623–1634): 28.7m bronze canopy over the papal altar and St. Peter's tomb; made from bronze stripped from the Pantheon
- **Bernini's Colonnade** (1656–1667): 284 columns in four rows embracing the piazza; Bernini described them as "the motherly arms of the Church" reaching out to Catholics, welcoming heretics, and enlightening unbelievers

## What's Inside
- **Pietà** (Michelangelo, 1498–1499): first chapel on the right; behind bulletproof glass since 1972
- **The Confessio**: the sunken area before the main altar, directly over St. Peter's tomb
- **St. Peter's tomb**: excavations (1939–1950) beneath the basilica found a 1st-century burial the Vatican identifies as Peter's
- **Chair of Saint Peter** (Bernini, 1647–1653): bronze reliquary enclosing what may be Peter's episcopal throne

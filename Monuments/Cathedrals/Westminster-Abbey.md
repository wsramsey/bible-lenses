# Westminster Abbey
*Every English monarch crowned here since 1066 — and Darwin, Newton, and Chaucer buried here*

## The Royal Function
**Every English/British monarch since William the Conqueror (1066) has been crowned in Westminster Abbey** — except Edward V and Edward VIII (who were never crowned). The Coronation Chair (made 1301) has been used for every coronation.

The Abbey contains the tombs of 17 monarchs including: Edward the Confessor (its founder), Henry III (who rebuilt it), Elizabeth I, Mary I (in the same vault — Protestant and Catholic queens together), and Mary Queen of Scots.

## Poets' Corner
The south transept contains the graves and memorials of England's greatest writers:
- **Geoffrey Chaucer** (died 1400) — the first burial that created the tradition
- **Edmund Spenser, John Dryden, Samuel Johnson, David Garrick, Handel, Charles Dickens, Rudyard Kipling, Thomas Hardy, Alfred Lord Tennyson**

## Scientists' Corner (Nave)
- **Isaac Newton** (died 1727): buried in the nave with the inscription "Here is buried Isaac Newton, Knight, who by a strength of mind almost divine... demonstrated the figure of the comets, the tides of the sea, the motions of the planets"
- **Charles Darwin** (died 1882): buried near Newton — a Church of England decision that acknowledged Darwin's scientific greatness despite the evolution controversy
- **Stephen Hawking** (ashes interred 2018)

## The Theological Irony
A church that houses Darwin and Newton alongside medieval kings, Anglican martyrs, and Poets' Corner is a statement about the English relationship between faith and culture. The Abbey is simultaneously deeply Christian and deeply secular — the national church as national pantheon.

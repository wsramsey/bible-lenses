# Second Temple Judaism — The World Jesus Inhabited
*You cannot understand Jesus without understanding this world*

## What "Second Temple" Means
The **Second Temple period** spans from the rebuilding of the Jerusalem Temple (516 BC, after the Babylonian exile) to its destruction by Rome in 70 AD. This is the world of:
- The Psalms being collected and edited
- The Torah being interpreted by expanding oral tradition
- The Prophets being read as pointing to an imminent divine intervention
- Four distinct Jewish parties each with their own interpretation
- Jesus and Paul

## The Four Jewish Parties (According to Josephus)

### Pharisees
The most significant party for understanding Jesus.
- Believed in the resurrection of the dead (unlike Sadducees)
- Developed **oral Torah** — the interpretation of written Torah for everyday life; this became the Talmud
- Believed holiness was possible for all Jews, not just priests — "a kingdom of priests"
- **Jesus's constant interlocutors** in the Gospels — the debates are *intra-Jewish*
- The **rabbinic Judaism** that survived after 70 AD was Pharisaic; modern Judaism descends from the Pharisees

### Sadducees
- Priestly aristocracy; controlled the Temple
- Accepted only the written Torah; rejected oral tradition and resurrection
- Accommodated Roman rule
- Disappeared after 70 AD (no Temple, no priesthood)
- **Caiaphas** was a Sadducee

### Essenes
- Separatist; fled to the desert (Qumran)
- Believed the Jerusalem Temple was corrupt
- Followed a strict purity code and calendar
- Practiced communal living and shared property
- **The Dead Sea Scrolls community** — almost certainly Essene
- Apocalyptic expectation: the end was imminent; they were preparing

### Zealots
- Political revolutionaries; violent resistance to Rome
- Believed only God was king; paying Roman taxes was idolatry
- **Simon the Zealot** was one of the Twelve
- Led the revolt that produced the Jewish War (66–73 AD) and the destruction of Jerusalem

## Apocalypticism: The Air Jesus Breathed
**Apocalypticism** — the belief that God would imminently intervene in history to judge the nations, vindicate Israel, and establish his kingdom — was the dominant theological mood of 1st-century Judaism.

Key texts:
- **Daniel** (especially chapters 7–12): "Son of Man" coming on clouds; resurrection of the dead; the end of Gentile domination
- **1 Enoch**: The Watchers; judgment of the fallen angels; the Son of Man enthroned in heaven
- **4 Ezra**: Written after 70 AD; theodicy of national destruction
- **Dead Sea Scrolls**: The Community Rule; the War Scroll (eschatological battle between sons of light and darkness)

**Why it matters for Jesus**: When Jesus proclaimed "the kingdom of God is at hand; repent and believe," this was heard in an apocalyptic context. Not a metaphor for inner spiritual experience — a claim that God was acting now in history.

## Torah Interpretation
The Pharisees' oral tradition (*halakha*) was the most intellectually active part of 2nd Temple Judaism. The question "Which is the greatest commandment?" (Matthew 22:36) was a standard rabbinic debate — Jesus's answer ("Love God and love neighbor") was a recognized rabbinic summary.

The debates between Jesus and the Pharisees in the Gospels are **rabbinic debates** — they follow the form and content of debates preserved in the Mishnah and Talmud. Jesus was participating in a living Jewish intellectual tradition, not opposing it from outside.

## Paul as a Pharisee
**[[Paul the Apostle]]** was a Pharisee (Philippians 3:5) — trained probably under Gamaliel in Jerusalem (Acts 22:3).

**E.P. Sanders** (*Paul and Palestinian Judaism*, 1977): The dominant Protestant reading of Paul against "Jewish works-righteousness" is anachronistic. Pharisaic Judaism was not a religion of merit-earning but of **covenantal nomism** — you're in the covenant by God's grace; you stay in by Torah obedience.

**[[N.T. Wright]]**: Paul is a Second Temple Jew rethinking his entire worldview in light of the resurrection. His categories (justification, the law, Israel, the nations) are Jewish categories being reinterpreted, not Greek philosophy being imported.

## The Talmud and Early Christianity
After 70 AD, rabbinic Judaism and early Christianity developed simultaneously and in dialogue/competition:
- Both produced their defining texts in roughly the same period (NT: 50–100 AD; Mishnah: c. 200 AD; Talmud: c. 400–600 AD)
- Rabbinic debates sometimes seem to respond to Christian claims (and vice versa)
- The **Birkat ha-Minim** (blessing against heretics) in the synagogue liturgy may have targeted Jewish Christians
- Understanding what the rabbis were debating illuminates what the NT was responding to

# The Iconoclast Controversy (726–843)
*The most important theological argument about art in Christian history*

## The Crisis
Emperor Leo III ordered the removal and destruction of icons from churches throughout the Byzantine Empire (726 AD). He believed:
- Icon veneration was idolatry
- Icons were the reason Muslims and Jews rejected Christianity
- The second commandment prohibited religious images

## The Pro-Icon Response

**[[John of Damascus]]** wrote three treatises *On Holy Images* from the safety of Muslim-controlled Palestine (where Byzantine emperors had no jurisdiction):

> "In former times God, who is without form or body, could never be depicted. But now when God is seen in the flesh conversing with men, I make an image of the God whom I see."

**The argument**: The Incarnation changed everything. Before Christ, God was invisible — the second commandment prohibition made sense. After Christ, God *became visible* in human form. To refuse to depict Christ is to deny the Incarnation. Iconoclasm is a Christological heresy.

## The Theology of the Icon

The icon is not a portrait but a **theological statement**:
- The frontal pose, the flattened perspective, the gold background — these are not artistic incompetence but theological choices
- The icon depicts the **glorified, resurrected body**, not the biological appearance
- To venerate the icon is to venerate the person depicted (*proskynesis* — "relative veneration") not the wood and paint (*latria* — "absolute worship" reserved for God alone)
- The icon participates in the prototype — there is a real connection between the image and the person

**The Seventh Ecumenical Council** (Second Nicaea, 787) formalized this theology:
- Icons are to be honored (*timetiki proskynesis*), not worshipped (*latreia*)
- This distinction settles the idolatry charge
- The theology of the icon is Chalcedonian Christology made visible

## The Resolution: The Triumph of Orthodoxy (843)
Empress Theodora restored the icons definitively in 843. The **first Sunday of Lent** is still celebrated as the "Triumph of Orthodoxy" in Eastern Orthodox churches — the icons processed back into the church.

**The lasting legacy**: 
- Orthodox worship is saturated with icons — they are the congregation's cloud of witnesses
- Every Orthodox church has an **iconostasis** — a screen of icons separating the nave from the sanctuary
- Icon-writing is a spiritual discipline, not artistic self-expression; iconographers fast and pray
- The theology of the icon influenced all subsequent Christian understanding of sacred art

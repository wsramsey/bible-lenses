# Philip Melanchthon
*1497–1560 · Lutheran · The Reformer's Reformer*

> **Why he matters:** Melanchthon wrote the Augsburg Confession — the defining document of Lutheranism. He gave the Reformation intellectual respectability and tried (unsuccessfully) to hold it together after Luther's death. He is the most important Reformation theologian after Luther and Calvin who is almost unknown outside specialists.

## "Praeceptor Germaniae" — Teacher of Germany
Melanchthon was a humanist prodigy who became professor of Greek at Wittenberg at age 21. Luther called him the greatest theologian since Paul. His *Loci Communes* (1521) was the first systematic theology of the Reformation — printed 18 times in Luther's lifetime.

## The Augsburg Confession (1530)
Emperor Charles V summoned the Protestant princes to Augsburg to defend their faith or return to Rome. Melanchthon wrote the **Augsburg Confession** — a careful, irenic statement of Lutheran theology designed to show maximum agreement with Catholic tradition while defending the essential Reformation insights.

It remains **the primary confessional document of Lutheranism** — subscribed by the ELCA, LCMS, and Lutheran World Federation.

The first 21 articles state what Lutherans believe; the last 7 articles state what abuses they reject. The structure was deliberate: show we're not revolutionaries.

## The Mediator Who Pleased No One
After Luther's death (1546), Melanchthon attempted compromises:
- The **Leipzig Interim (1548)**: allowed some Catholic ceremonies to return in exchange for Protestant doctrines being retained — hated by hard-line Lutherans ("Gnesio-Lutherans")
- The **Crypto-Calvinist controversy**: his later writings on the Eucharist moved toward Calvin's position; after his death, Lutherans accused him of having been secretly Calvinist
- His **Variata** version of the Augsburg Confession (1540): modified to allow Calvinist interpretation — still causes ecumenical controversy

## His Legacy
- The German educational system — he reorganized the curriculum of virtually every German school; "Teacher of Germany" is not an exaggeration
- The Formula of Concord (1577) — written after his death to settle the disputes he left — both settles and buries his irenic project
- His attempt to hold Lutheranism together prefigures every ecumenical effort since

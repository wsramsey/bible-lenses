# Thomas Cranmer
*1489–1556 · Anglican · Father of the Book of Common Prayer*

> **Why he matters:** Cranmer's *Book of Common Prayer* is one of the greatest achievements in the history of Christian worship — Protestant theology in the most beautiful liturgical English ever written. It has formed more Christians than almost any other single document.

## The Architecture of the English Reformation
As Archbishop of Canterbury, Cranmer was the theological architect of the English Reformation:
- Pushed Henry VIII toward Protestant theology (the King's personal beliefs remained Catholic)
- Supervised the English Bible translations (*The Great Bible*, 1539)
- Under Edward VI, produced two Prayer Books (1549, 1552) that moved the English church in a Protestant direction
- Wrote the **42 Articles** (basis of the later 39 Articles) — the doctrinal standard of Anglicanism

## The Book of Common Prayer (1549, 1552)
Cranmer's genius was liturgical. The Prayer Book:
- Translated and simplified the complex medieval Latin rites into English
- Created a *single* liturgical standard for the entire English church
- Encoded Reformed theology (justification by faith, Scripture supremacy) in traditional liturgical forms
- Was **deliberately ambiguous** on the Eucharist to hold Catholic and Protestant interpretations

**The 1552 revision** was more decisively Protestant; the eucharistic language was changed to remove any implication of real presence.

The **1662 Prayer Book** (after the Restoration) remains the standard for traditional Anglicanism worldwide — 85 million Anglicans trace their worship to Cranmer.

## His Death: The Burned Hand
Under Mary I (Catholic restoration), Cranmer was tried for heresy. Under torture and pressure, he **recanted** his Protestant beliefs — multiple times, producing more and more groveling recantations.

At his burning (March 21, 1556), he did something extraordinary: he thrust his right hand — the hand that had signed the recantations — **into the flames first**, holding it there as it burned, saying "this hand hath offended." Only then did he allow the fire to consume him.

The gesture transformed his memory from a wavering betrayer into a martyr. The drama of a man punishing his own compromised hand in the act of dying is one of the most powerful moments in Reformation history.

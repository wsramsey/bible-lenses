# Ulrich Zwingli
*1484–1531 · Reformed · Swiss Reformation*

> **Why he matters:** Zwingli is the forgotten founder of Reformed Christianity — his influence on Calvin and the Reformed tradition is as great as Luther's, but he is lost between Luther's fame and Calvin's systematizing. His Eucharistic theology defines the majority Protestant position.

## The Zürich Reformation
Zwingli began reforming Zürich in 1519 — independently of Luther, from humanist and biblical principles. His method: preach straight through books of the Bible (*lectio continua*). Within a year he had more followers in Zürich than Luther had anywhere outside Saxony.

**The 67 Articles (1523)**: Zwingli's manifesto. Scripture is the sole authority; the Mass is not a sacrifice; images should be removed; clerical celibacy is unbiblical.

## The Eucharist: The Great Divide

At the **Marburg Colloquy (1529)**, Luther and Zwingli met to discuss unity. The deal-breaker was the Eucharist.

Luther's position: "This is my body" means *this is my body* — Christ is truly, bodily present in the Supper.

Zwingli's position: "This is" means "this signifies" (*est* = *significat*). Christ's body is at the right hand of the Father; finite matter cannot contain infinite spirit. The Supper is a *memorial* and a pledge — not a vehicle of Christ's presence.

Luther reportedly wrote "HOC EST CORPUS MEUM" on the table in chalk and refused to move from it. They could not agree. Protestant unity was never achieved.

**Zwingli's position became the dominant Protestant view**: most evangelical Christians today practice **memorialist** Eucharistic theology — the Lord's Supper as memorial — whether they know they're Zwinglian or not.

## The Theology of the Spirit
Zwingli's deepest contribution: **pneumatic hermeneutics**. The Spirit operates *independently* of external means (water, bread, words). External ceremonies are helpful but not necessary. This produced:
- His memorialism (the Spirit, not the bread, conveys grace)
- His rejection of infant baptism as conveying grace (though he kept it as a covenant sign)
- His willingness to remove images from churches
- Eventually, among his followers: the Anabaptist movement (Conrad Grebel was his student)

## His Death
Zwingli died at the **Battle of Kappel (1531)** — he accompanied the Zürich army as chaplain and was killed in battle against the Catholic cantons. Luther commented harshly; Calvin thought his death was divine judgment.

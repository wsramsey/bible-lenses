# Canon Formation — How We Got the Bible
*The real story of why these 66 (or 73, or 78) books and not others*

## The Trigger: Marcion (c. 140 AD)

**Marcion of Sinope** arrived in Rome around 140 AD with a radical proposal:
- The God of the OT is a different, inferior God from the Father of Jesus
- The OT should be rejected entirely
- Paul (properly edited) and Luke are the only trustworthy Gospels

**Why this matters**: Before Marcion, there was no pressing need for a defined Christian canon. The churches read whatever they had. **Marcion forced the question**: *Which writings are authoritative?*

The orthodox response to Marcion was not to condemn his *idea* of a canon but to affirm a *better* canon — one that included the OT and more Gospels.

## The Muratorian Fragment (c. 170–200 AD)
The oldest surviving canonical list. It names:
- Four Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John)
- Acts
- 13 Pauline letters
- Jude, 1-2 John, Revelation, Wisdom of Solomon
- Explicitly *excludes* the Shepherd of Hermas (too recent), the Apocalypse of Peter (disputed)

**Significance**: By 170 AD, a recognizable NT core already existed.

## The Process: Gradual Consensus (170–367 AD)

Canon formation was not a single decision but a gradual consensus:

**Criteria used (not official, but effectively applied)**:
1. **Apostolic origin** — written by an apostle or apostolic companion
2. **Orthodox content** — consistent with the rule of faith
3. **Liturgical use** — read in churches across the empire
4. **Catholicity** — recognized by many churches, not just one

**The contested books**:
- *Hebrews*: no named author; accepted slowly; questioned by some Eastern churches
- *James*: Luther called it "an epistle of straw" (works vs. faith)
- *2 Peter*: style differs from 1 Peter; latest accepted into the NT
- *2 and 3 John*: tiny letters; limited circulation
- *Jude*: quotes 1 Enoch (an extracanonical Jewish book)
- *Revelation*: bizarre imagery; accepted in West, contested in East for centuries

**Athanasius's Easter Letter (367 AD)**: The first known document listing exactly our 27-book NT. Athanasius's list was not a decision — it was a snapshot of emerging consensus in Alexandria.

## The OT Question: The Deuterocanonical Books

### Why There Are Different OT Canons
**The Hebrew canon**: 24 books (= the Protestant 39, counted differently) — settled by the rabbis at Yavneh (c. 90 AD)

**The Septuagint (LXX)**: The Greek translation used by the early church included additional books not in the Hebrew:
- Tobit, Judith, 1-2 Maccabees, Sirach (Ecclesiasticus), Wisdom, Baruch, additions to Esther and Daniel

**The early church used the LXX** — these books were scripture to them.

**Jerome** in the Vulgate (c. 400) noted the distinction between the Hebrew canon and the LXX additions, calling the extra books *deuterocanonical* ("second canon").

**The Reformation decision**: Luther and the Reformers returned to the Hebrew canon, removing the deuterocanonicals. Their reasons:
- The Jews don't accept them
- They're not in the Hebrew Bible
- Some contain doctrines Protestants reject (prayer for the dead — 2 Maccabees 12:43–46, which supports purgatory)

**The Council of Trent (1546)**: In response to the Reformation, definitively declared the deuterocanonical books canonical for Catholics.

**Result**: 
- Protestant OT: 39 books
- Catholic OT: 46 books (adding 7 deuterocanonicals)
- Orthodox OT: 49 books (adds 3 Esdras, Psalm 151, Prayer of Manasseh, etc.)
- Ethiopian OT: ~46 books (including 1 Enoch, Jubilees, Meqabyan)

## The Councils That Closed the Canon

- **Synod of Rome (382)**: Under Pope Damasus; first official list including the deuterocanonicals
- **Council of Hippo (393)**: Local African council; affirmed the 27-book NT and 46-book OT
- **Council of Carthage (397)**: Repeated and publicized the Hippo list; the effective "closing" date

**Critical point**: There was no single ecumenical council that closed the NT canon. It was regional councils formalizing an existing consensus. The canon was recognized, not invented.

## What This Means Theologically

**For Catholics**: The church's authority preceded the Bible — the church determined the canon, not the canon the church.

**For Protestants**: The church *recognized* what was already self-attesting (*autopiston*) — the Holy Spirit testified to the canonical books through the church's response.

**For critical scholars**: The canon reflects historical accidents and political decisions, not divine guidance — many excluded gospels and letters deserve equal consideration.

# Women in Church History — The Full Account
*The majority of the church, written out of most of its history*

## The Apostolic Era: Women as Leaders

### The Evidence in Paul's Letters
- **Phoebe** (Romans 16:1): *diakonos* (deacon/minister) of the church at Cenchreae; Paul calls her a *prostatis* (patron/leader) — the same word used for civic leaders
- **Prisca (Priscilla)** (Romans 16:3): Paul's "co-worker"; instructed Apollos (Acts 18:26); her name listed before her husband's 4 of 6 times — unusual, suggesting higher status
- **Junia** (Romans 16:7): "outstanding among the apostles" — female apostle; later manuscripts changed to the male "Junias" (a name that doesn't otherwise exist in antiquity)
- **Mary, Tryphaena, Tryphosa, Persis** (Romans 16:6, 12): all commended for "working hard" — the same word Paul uses for his own apostolic labor

### The Gospel Evidence
- Women were Jesus's most faithful disciples at the cross when the men fled
- Women were the first resurrection witnesses — commissioned by the risen Christ ([[Mary Magdalene]])
- The criterion of embarrassment: no 1st century Jewish fiction would invent women as primary witnesses

## The Desert Mothers (*Ammas*)
The Desert Fathers (*Abba*, father) are famous. The Desert Mothers (*Amma*, mother) are almost unknown despite being equally revered in antiquity.

**Amma Syncletica**: "Just as the most bitter medicine drives out poisonous creatures, so prayer joined to fasting drives evil thoughts away."

**Amma Sarah**: When attacked by a male demon, she prayed not for the temptation to go but for endurance; when a monk tested her humility, she responded: "According to my nature I am a woman, but not according to my thoughts."

**Amma Theodora**: Her sayings on humility, discernment, and the spiritual life rival the most celebrated desert fathers.

The *Sayings of the Desert Fathers* (*Apophthegmata Patrum*) include the ammas but they are regularly omitted from popular editions.

## Medieval Abbesses: Real Authority

**Hilda of Whitby (614–680)**: Abbess of Whitby (a double monastery for men and women); presided over the Synod of Whitby (664) — the most important ecclesiastical gathering in early English Christianity; trained five bishops; Bede called her "mother" of all who knew her.

**[[Hildegard of Bingen]] (1098–1179)**: Already in the vault. But her *actual authority* needs emphasis: she corresponded with popes and emperors as an equal; preached publicly in four preaching tours (highly unusual for a woman); her theological judgments were sought by male clergy.

**Clare of Assisi** (in vault): Her *Rule* was the first Rule written by a woman for women — a landmark in church history.

## Beguines: Women's Theology
The **Beguine movement** (12th–14th century) was an extraordinary women's movement:
- Women in northern Europe (Belgium, Germany, France) formed communities without formal religious vows
- They worked, prayed, and developed sophisticated mystical theology
- **Mechthild of Magdeburg** (*The Flowing Light of the Godhead*, c. 1250): visionary mystical theology; influenced Dante
- **Hadewijch of Brabant** (13th c.): poetry and letters on the love of God; comparable to John of the Cross
- **Marguerite Porete** (*The Mirror of Simple Souls*, c. 1295): burned at the stake by the Inquisition for her mystical theology (1310)

The Beguines were suppressed by the institutional church (Council of Vienne, 1311–12) — their independence was threatening.

## The 19th Century Women's Missionary Movement

By 1900, **women constituted 60%+ of the Protestant missionary force**:
- Women's missionary societies were among the first organizations where women held institutional leadership
- **Lottie Moon** (1840–1912): Southern Baptist missionary to China; the annual Lottie Moon Christmas Offering ($185 million/year) is the largest single offering in Protestantism
- **Amy Carmichael** (in vault as missionary): rescued children; wrote devotional classics
- **Ida Scudder** (1870–1960): founded the Christian Medical College, Vellore, India — now one of the leading hospitals in Asia; started by a woman who saw three women die in one night because no female doctor was available

The missionary movement was simultaneously the **most significant women's professional and leadership opportunity** in the 19th century and a vehicle of cultural imperialism.

## Suppressed Women Theologians

**Marie Dentière** (1495–1561): Former abbess; Reformed theologian; wrote a letter defending women's right to interpret Scripture and preach (1539); her name was removed from the letter's title page — she is largely unknown. Calvin opposed her.

**Anna Maria van Schurman** (1607–1678): Called "the star of Utrecht"; one of the most learned women in Europe; argued women should study theology (*The Learned Maid*, 1638); later abandoned Reformed Christianity for the Labadist sect.

**Katharina Zell** (1497–1562): Pastor's wife in Strasbourg; wrote biblical commentaries; led a congregation; the Reformation suppressed women it had briefly empowered.

## The Ongoing Question
The church has the consistent pattern of:
1. Women playing crucial roles in founding and sustaining movements
2. As movements institutionalize, women being pushed out of leadership
3. The pattern repeating in every era

Feminist historians argue this is the church's most persistent structural sin. Conservative historians argue the pattern reflects created order. The data is not in dispute; the interpretation is.

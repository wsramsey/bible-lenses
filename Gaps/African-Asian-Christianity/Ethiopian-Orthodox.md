# The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church
*The only ancient Christian nation that was never colonized*

## Founded (c. 330 AD)
**Frumentius** — a Syrian Christian shipwrecked as a boy in Ethiopia, became tutor to the royal princes, and eventually converted King Ezana of Aksum (c. 330). Athanasius of Alexandria consecrated Frumentius as the first Bishop of Ethiopia (c. 328–356).

**The Philip and the Ethiopian Eunuch** (Acts 8:26–40): The Ethiopian Orthodox tradition reads this as the actual founding moment — before Frumentius; the eunuch returned to Ethiopia with the Gospel. Whether historical or not, it roots Ethiopian Christianity in the apostolic era.

## What Makes Ethiopian Christianity Unique

### The Canon: 81 Books
The Ethiopian biblical canon is the largest in Christianity:
- **46 OT books** (including 1 Enoch, Jubilees, and books found nowhere else)
- **35 NT books** (including the Shepherd of Hermas and 1 Clement)

**1 Enoch** is particularly significant: the New Testament *quotes* Enoch (Jude 14–15) but only the Ethiopian church preserved the full text. The Dead Sea Scrolls confirmed its antiquity.

**Jubilees**: A retelling of Genesis and early Exodus; preserved only in Ethiopia; the Dead Sea Scrolls confirmed it was also known at Qumran.

The Ethiopian canon raises the question: who decided which books were "in"? The Ethiopian church maintained ancient texts that the rest of Christianity lost.

### The Solomonic Tradition
The **Kebra Nagast** ("Glory of Kings," 13th–14th c.) — the Ethiopian national epic — claims:
- The Queen of Sheba was Ethiopian (Queen Makeda)
- Her son Menelik I was Solomon's son
- Menelik brought the Ark of the Covenant to Ethiopia
- The Ark remains at **Aksum** (the city of St. Mary of Zion church) guarded by one monk

The Ark claim is unverifiable but defines Ethiopian Christian identity. The **tabot** (replica of the Ark) in every Ethiopian church is the most sacred object; the liturgy revolves around it.

### The Saturday Sabbath
Ethiopian Orthodox Christians observe **both** Saturday (Sabbath) and Sunday (Resurrection) as holy days — a practice maintained since antiquity connecting to Jewish roots.

### The Ge'ez Liturgy
The liturgy is in **Ge'ez** (classical Ethiopic) — a Semitic language related to ancient South Arabian. Ge'ez is to Ethiopian Christianity what Latin was to Western Catholicism and what Church Slavonic is to Russian Orthodoxy: a sacred language that connects worshipers to antiquity.

## Survived Without Colonization
Ethiopia is the **only African country that successfully resisted European colonization** (the Battle of Adwa, 1896, defeated the Italian invasion). Ethiopian Christianity therefore developed on African terms, not as a colonial import.

The Ethiopian church was never reformed by missionaries, never had its theology reshaped by Western categories, never lost its indigenous leadership. It represents what African Christianity looks like when it develops on its own terms for 1,700 years.

## Size and Significance
- ~40 million members (the third-largest Oriental Orthodox church)
- Deeply embedded in Ethiopian culture: the calendar, the architecture, the music, the art
- **Ethiopian Christian art**: large eyes, dark skin, distinctive style — African faces in Christian iconography centuries before Western missions

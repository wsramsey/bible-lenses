# The Church of the East — Christianity Along the Silk Road
*Christianity in China 1,100 years before Protestant missions*

## The Most Forgotten Major Christian Tradition

The Church of the East is the branch of Christianity that:
- Rejected the Council of Ephesus (431) and its condemnation of Nestorius
- Spread east while Western Christianity spread west
- At its peak (8th–9th century), was arguably the largest Christian church in the world
- Had dioceses in Iraq, Iran, Central Asia, India, China, and Mongolia
- Is almost entirely unknown in Western church history

## The Founding
The Church of the East traces its founding to the Apostle Thomas (India tradition) and to the disciples Addai and Mari (Mesopotamia). By the 2nd century, there were churches throughout the Persian (Sasanian) Empire.

**Key difference from Western churches**: developed outside the Roman Empire, under Persian rule. The church was often persecuted by Zoroastrian Persia; later found more tolerance under Islam.

## The Nestorian Question
After Ephesus (431), the church split:
- The **Church of the East** (Nestorian) maintained that Christ had two persons as well as two natures — the human Jesus and the divine Son — united in one being
- The **Chalcedonian churches** affirmed two natures in one person

Modern scholarship questions whether the Church of the East was actually "Nestorian" in the heretical sense — they may have been expressing orthodox theology in different terminology. Dialogue between Rome and the Assyrian Church of the East in the late 20th century largely resolved the dispute.

## The Silk Road Mission

### China: The Xi'an Stele (635 AD)
In 635 AD, the **Alopen** (an Assyrian bishop) arrived at the Tang Dynasty capital Chang'an (modern Xi'an). Emperor Taizong gave him an imperial audience and permission to build a church.

**The Xi'an Stele (781 AD)**: A 9-foot stone monument, discovered in 1625, documenting 150 years of Christianity in China. Written in Chinese and Syriac. Calls Christianity "the Luminous Religion" (*Jingjiao*).

**At its peak**: The Tang Dynasty had Assyrian Christian monasteries and churches across China. The faith reached China 1,100 years before Robert Morrison (the first Protestant missionary to China, 1807).

**Why it disappeared**: The Tang Dynasty collapse (907 AD) and subsequent anti-foreign religious policies eliminated Christianity in China. It did not survive without institutional support.

### Mongolia: Nestorian Christianity and the Mongols
Several Mongol tribes were Nestorian Christian before Genghis Khan. His general Kitbuqa was Christian. His daughter-in-law Sorghaghtani Beki (mother of Kublai Khan) was Nestorian. For a period, the Mongol Empire showed remarkable tolerance to Christianity — which is why Marco Polo and the Franciscan missions of the 13th century found Christians in Mongolia and China.

## The Modern Church of the East
After Mongol devastation (14th c.) and Tamerlane's massacres (1387–1401), the Church of the East was reduced to a small community in northern Iraq/Iran and the Malabar Coast of India.

**Today**: 
- **Assyrian Church of the East**: headquartered in Chicago; ~400,000 members
- **Chaldean Catholic Church**: in communion with Rome since 1552; ~400,000 members
- **Mar Thoma and other Thomas Christian churches**: India

**The ISIS genocide** nearly destroyed the ancient Mesopotamian Christian community — churches going back 2,000 years were emptied or destroyed 2014–2017.

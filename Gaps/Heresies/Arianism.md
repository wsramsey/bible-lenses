# Arianism — The Heresy That Almost Won
*For 50 years, Arianism was the majority position in the church*

## The Arian Position
**Arius** (c. 256–336), a priest in Alexandria, taught:
- The Son is the first and greatest *creation* of the Father — not eternal, not uncreated
- "There was a time when he was not" (*ēn pote hote ouk ēn*)
- The Son is *homoiousios* (similar in substance to the Father) but not *homoousios* (the same substance)
- The Son is divine but in a subordinate, secondary sense

**Why it was attractive**:
- Logically coherent: only the Father is truly God; the Son is the greatest of creatures
- Protected divine transcendence: the Father doesn't directly enter creation
- Had strong OT support: "The LORD created me at the beginning of his work" (Proverbs 8:22 — applied to the pre-existent Word)
- Many bishops found it more philosophically sophisticated than what seemed like two Gods

## The Nicene Response
**[[Athanasius of Alexandria]]**'s central argument: If the Son is a creature, then:
1. We worship a creature (idolatry)
2. The Incarnation doesn't accomplish what it claims — only God can deify; a creature cannot make us partakers of divine nature

The Council of Nicaea (325) condemned Arianism and affirmed *homoousios* — "of the same substance."

## The 50-Year Battle: Athanasius Contra Mundum

After Nicaea, Arianism didn't die. It controlled the church for much of the next 50 years:
- **Constantine** eventually favored Arian bishops
- **Constantius II** (reigned 337–361) was militantly Arian; exiled all Nicene bishops
- **Athanasius** was exiled **five times** by Arian emperors; spent 17 of his 45 years as bishop in exile
- At various points, virtually every bishop in the East had accepted some form of Arianism
- Jerome wrote in 380: "The whole world groaned, and was astonished to find itself Arian"

## Arian Christianity Among the Germanic Peoples
After Nicaea, the Arian position survived for centuries among the Germanic tribes:
- **Ulfilas** (311–383) — the Apostle to the Goths — was Arian; created the Gothic Bible in Arian theology
- **Visigoths, Ostrogoths, Vandals, Lombards** were all Arian Christians when they invaded the Roman Empire
- The conflict between Arian Germanic rulers and Nicene Roman populations shaped the 5th–7th centuries
- The Visigoths converted to Nicene Christianity in Spain (589); the last Arian kingdom fell in the 7th century

## Modern Arianism
- **Jehovah's Witnesses**: explicitly teach that Jesus was the first creation of God — the closest contemporary analog to Arianism
- **Unitarianism**: denies the Trinity; Jesus as a great teacher but not God — more Socinian than Arian but in the same trajectory
- **Islam**: Jesus as prophet and Messiah but not divine — structurally similar concern about divine transcendence

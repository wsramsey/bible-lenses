# The Oxford Movement (Tractarianism)
*The Catholic retrieval within Anglicanism — and its most dramatic conversion*

## The Context
The Oxford Movement began in 1833 in response to a crisis: the British Parliament was abolishing 10 Irish Anglican bishoprics. To the Oxford men, this was the state treating the church as its department.

John Keble's Assize Sermon ("National Apostasy," July 14, 1833): The founding sermon. The church must defend its independence and its apostolic heritage.

## The Tracts for the Times (1833–1841)
Newman, Keble, Pusey, and others published 90 "Tracts for the Times" — pamphlets arguing for:
- The apostolic succession of Anglican bishops
- The sacraments as means of grace
- The authority of tradition alongside Scripture
- The church as distinct from the state

**Tract 90 (1841)**: Newman's argument that the 39 Articles of the Church of England could be interpreted in a Catholic sense. The furor was enormous; Newman was condemned; the tract series ended.

## Newman's Conversion (1845)
John Henry Newman entered the Roman Catholic Church on October 9, 1845. The most dramatic intellectual conversion of the Victorian era.

His *Essay on the Development of Christian Doctrine* (1845) was his answer to the question that had stopped him: how is medieval and modern Catholicism related to the New Testament church? His answer: like a seed growing into a tree — organic development, not corruption.

## Pusey's Continuation
**E.B. Pusey** stayed in the Church of England and continued the movement. "Puseyism" became the popular name for Anglo-Catholicism.

The Oxford Movement transformed Anglicanism:
- Renewed emphasis on sacraments
- Reintroduction of vestments and ceremonial
- The Catholic-looking "high church" in Anglicanism
- Revival of religious orders for Anglicans (nuns, monks)
- Recovery of patristic theology

## The Legacy
The Oxford Movement contributed to:
- The **liturgical renewal** in Anglican, and eventually Protestant, worship
- The **ecumenical movement** — taking the church's catholicity seriously
- The **Anglo-Catholic tradition** — still a significant strand in Anglicanism
- Newman became a Catholic cardinal; his ideas permeated Vatican II; he was beatified (2010) and canonized — rare for a former Anglican

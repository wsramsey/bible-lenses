# Liberation Theology
*The preferential option for the poor — the most controversial theological movement of the 20th century*

## The Context
Latin America in the 1960s:
- Extreme economic inequality; a few wealthy families; vast poor majority
- Military dictatorships supported by the US (Cold War anti-communism)
- The Catholic Church historically aligned with the elite
- Vatican II (1962–1965) opened the door to social engagement
- The Medellín Conference (1968) was the turning point

## The Medellín Conference (1968)
The Second General Conference of Latin American Bishops in Medellín, Colombia. The bishops declared:
- The church must make a "preferential option for the poor"
- Latin American poverty is not natural but the result of unjust structures
- The church must denounce injustice and support the transformation of society

This was the most radical official Catholic statement since the Sermon on the Mount.

## Gustavo Gutiérrez (1928–2024)
Peruvian Dominican priest; the father of liberation theology.

*A Theology of Liberation* (1971): The founding text.

Key ideas:
- **Theology as second act**: The first act is commitment to the poor; theology reflects on that commitment
- **Structural sin**: Sin is not only personal but embedded in unjust social structures
- **The Exodus as the paradigm**: God liberates the oppressed; this is the central biblical narrative
- **Base communities** (*comunidades de base*): Small groups of poor Christians studying Scripture together and connecting it to their situation
- **Orthopraxis over orthodoxy**: Right action is as important as right belief

## The Vatican's Response
Two "Instructions" from Cardinal Ratzinger (CDF):
1. **1984**: Warned against Marxist analysis; liberation theology at risk of reducing Christianity to politics
2. **1986**: Acknowledged the legitimate concerns; affirmed the preferential option for the poor; but warned against specific methodological errors

Leonardo Boff was silenced for one year (1985). Several liberation theologians were investigated.

**The irony**: The preferential option for the poor was incorporated into official Catholic Social Teaching. Pope Francis (himself from Latin America) is deeply shaped by liberation theology. Gutiérrez was rehabilitated; Francis hosted him at the Vatican.

## Oscar Romero (1917–1980)
Archbishop of San Salvador, El Salvador. Initially a conservative; transformed by the assassination of his friend Jesuit priest Rutilio Grande.

Began denouncing the death squads and military violence. On March 24, 1980, he was assassinated while saying Mass — shot by a right-wing death squad.

**Canonized** by Pope Francis (2018). His last sermon: "In the name of God, I implore you, I beg you, I order you: Stop the repression!"

## Black Liberation Theology
**James Cone** (1938–2018): *Black Theology and Black Power* (1969).
- God is on the side of the oppressed; in America, God is Black
- Jesus identifies with the oppressed; in America, Jesus is Black
- The cross is about liberation from oppression, not simply individual sin
- White theology has been complicit in white supremacy

Enormously controversial. The most important American theological work of the 20th century in terms of cultural impact.

## The Base Communities
The most durable fruit of liberation theology: *comunidades de base* — small groups of poor Christians meeting to read the Bible through the lens of their situation.

"See, judge, act": See the situation; judge it in the light of the Gospel; act to change it.

At their peak: 180,000 base communities in Brazil alone. Many of their members became politicians, labor leaders, and social activists. The Workers' Party (PT) in Brazil — which elected Lula — grew partly from this movement.

# Anabaptism
*The radical Reformation — and Christianity's most consistent pacifist tradition*

## The Origin
January 21, 1525: In Conrad Grebel's house in Zürich, a group of young reformers re-baptized each other — adults, by choice, confessing faith. This was the founding act.

The name *Anabaptist* (re-baptizer) was a slur given by their enemies. They were immediately outlawed — drowning was the prescribed punishment (the cruel irony: execution by water for those who insisted on a second baptism).

## Why They Split from Zwingli
Zwingli was reforming the Zürich church with the support of the city council. The Anabaptists said: the church cannot be reformed by government; the church is a gathered community of believers, not a territorial institution. **Separation of church and state** — a century before Roger Williams.

## The Schleitheim Confession (1527)
The foundational Anabaptist document, written by Michael Sattler:
1. Believers' baptism only
2. The ban (church discipline)
3. The Lord's Supper as memorial (not sacrament)
4. Separation from the world
5. **Pastors supported by the congregation**
6. **The sword** — Christians may not use violence; cannot serve as magistrates or soldiers
7. **No oaths** (Matthew 5:33–37)

Sattler was arrested weeks after writing it. He was tortured (tongue cut out, flesh torn with red-hot pincers) and burned. His wife was drowned eight days later.

## The Radical Anabaptists
Not all Anabaptists were peaceful:
- **The Münster Rebellion (1534–1535)**: Jan van Leiden established a theocratic kingdom in Münster; polygamy; violent apocalypticism. Crushed by a combined Catholic-Lutheran army. Used to discredit all Anabaptists for generations.

## Menno Simons (1496–1561)
The stabilizing figure. Former Catholic priest; became the pastor and theologian of the peaceful Anabaptist mainstream. The **Mennonites** are named for him.

His key contribution: careful, patient pastoral work; theology of the peaceful church; the church as a community of disciples committed to nonviolence.

## The Persecution
Anabaptists were persecuted by Catholics, Lutherans, and Zwinglians alike:
- Felix Manz: drowned in the Limmat River, Zürich (1527) — the first Anabaptist martyr
- **The Martyrs Mirror (1660)**: A 1,500-page collection of Anabaptist martyrdom accounts; still read in Mennonite/Amish communities; the most comprehensive martyrology in Christian history

## The Legacy

**Mennonites**: 2 million members globally; known for disaster relief (Mennonite Central Committee); pacifism; simple living; service

**Amish**: Separated from Mennonites (1693) over church discipline; the most distinctive Christian subculture in America; deliberately pre-modern lifestyle

**Brethren**: Multiple bodies; Annual Conference (progressive) and conservative

**Hutterites**: Communal living; descended from Jakob Hutter (burned 1536)

**The Anabaptist Vision** (Harold S. Bender, 1943): The most influential 20th century retrieval — argued that the Anabaptists represented the true Reformation; their emphasis on discipleship, community, and nonviolence is normative; influenced John Howard Yoder and Stanley Hauerwas

# Fundamentalism
*The defense of the "fundamentals" against liberal Christianity*

## The Origin: "The Fundamentals" (1910–1915)
Ninety essays published in 12 volumes, funded by California businessman Lyman Stewart (co-founder of Union Oil). Distributed free to every pastor, missionary, theology student, and Christian worker in the English-speaking world — approximately 3 million copies.

The "fundamentals" they defended:
- The verbal inerrancy of Scripture
- The virgin birth of Jesus
- The physical resurrection
- The substitutionary atonement
- The literal Second Coming

## The Fundamentalist-Modernist Controversy (1920s)
→ See [[Fundamentalist-Modernist Controversy]] for full account

The decade when fundamentalism became a defined movement and cultural phenomenon.

## Dispensationalism
Most early fundamentalists were **dispensationalists** — a system of Bible interpretation developed by John Nelson Darby (1800–1882) and popularized by C.I. Scofield's *Reference Bible* (1909):

- History divided into "dispensations" — ages in which God deals with humanity differently
- The church age is a "parenthesis" in God's plan for Israel
- **The Rapture**: Christ secretly returns to take believers to heaven before a period of Tribulation
- **Israel restored**: God's Old Testament promises to Israel will be literally fulfilled
- **The Millennium**: Christ reigns on earth for 1,000 years

Dispensationalism is the theological framework of Tim LaHaye's *Left Behind* series — 80 million copies sold. It has no support among mainstream biblical scholars but enormous popular influence.

## The Separation
Fundamentalists developed a theology of **separation**:
- **Ecclesiastical separation**: Don't associate with apostates or denominations that tolerate them
- **Secondary separation**: Don't even associate with those who associate with apostates
- This produced increasing isolation and fragmentation

## Fundamentalism vs. Neo-Evangelicalism
In the 1940s–50s, Billy Graham and Carl Henry led a "neo-evangelical" movement that:
- Maintained the fundamentals
- BUT rejected the separatist and anti-intellectual posture
- Bob Jones Sr. condemned Graham for cooperating with Catholics and mainline Protestants
- The two streams split: fundamentalists (separatist, suspicious of scholarship) vs. evangelicals (engaged, intellectually serious)

## Contemporary Fundamentalism
- **Independent Baptist churches**: the most consistently separatist
- **Bob Jones University**: the flagship institution
- **Jack Hyles**: influential and eventually disgraced pastor; Hyles-Anderson College
- The term "fundamentalist" is now pejorative in most contexts; most who share the theology prefer "conservative evangelical"

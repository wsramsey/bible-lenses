# Progressive Christianity and the Deconstruction Movement
*Rethinking evangelical Christianity from within*

## The Emerging/Emergent Church (2000–2010)
**Brian McLaren**, **Tony Jones**, **Doug Pagitt**: A loose network of pastors and thinkers who questioned evangelical certainty in the context of postmodern culture.

Key questions:
- How do you reach a generation that is suspicious of institutions and certainty?
- Is the evangelical church's political alignment with the Republican Party faithful?
- Are the atonement models, hell, and sexual ethics of evangelicalism biblical or culturally constructed?

*A New Kind of Christian* (McLaren, 2001) was the touchstone text.

## The Rob Bell Moment (2011)
**Rob Bell**'s *Love Wins* questioned eternal conscious torment. The Reformed evangelical response was immediate and fierce. Bell left his megachurch, moved to Hollywood, eventually left the faith as an institutionalized religion while maintaining spiritual practice.

His trajectory became a template for many: from evangelical megachurch pastor to post-evangelical.

## Rachel Held Evans (1981–2019)
The most influential progressive evangelical voice of her generation. *A Year of Biblical Womanhood* (2012); *Inspired* (2018). Questioned complementarianism; moved toward egalitarianism and eventually progressive Christianity.

Her early death (from a medical emergency at 37) was mourned across the theological spectrum — she embodied a faith that questioned but didn't abandon.

## Peter Enns
NT Wright's former colleague at Westminster Theological Seminary; fired for *Inspiration and Incarnation* (2008). Became a leading voice for a non-inerrancy evangelical approach to Scripture.

## The Deconstruction Movement (2015–present)
"Deconstruction" — deconstructing one's inherited faith:

**Common triggers**:
- Disillusionment with evangelical political alignment (especially Trump)
- LGBTQ+ issues — either personally gay or having LGBTQ+ family members
- The abuse scandals (Ravi Zacharias, Bill Hybels, Mars Hill)
- Intellectual doubts (evolution, inerrancy, historical criticism)
- Racial justice issues

**Destinations** (highly varied):
- Progressive Christianity (still Christian, different)
- Mainline Protestantism
- Catholicism or Eastern Orthodoxy (surprisingly common)
- Spiritual but not religious
- Atheism/agnosticism

## Progressive Christianity
The institutionalized form: **The Progressive Christian Network**, **The Center for Progressive Christianity**.

Characteristics:
- Affirming of LGBTQ+ relationships
- Emphasis on social justice
- Open to doubt and questioning
- Less emphasis on doctrinal boundaries
- Often Universalist or inclusivist on salvation
- Strong emphasis on the historical Jesus over the Christ of faith

**The critique from the right**: Progressive Christianity has abandoned the Gospel; it's just liberal politics baptized.

**The internal critique**: Progressive Christianity retains the cultural markers of Christianity without the substance; it's better at deconstruction than construction.

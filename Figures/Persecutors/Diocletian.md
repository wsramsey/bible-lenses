# Diocletian
*244–311 · Roman Emperor · The Great Persecution*

> **Why he's here:** He launched the last and most severe Roman persecution of Christianity — and it failed completely. The Great Persecution (303–311) demanded the surrender of Scriptures (*traditores* — those who handed over books; root of "traitor"), the demolition of churches, and the execution of Christians who refused to sacrifice. Constantine reversed it within two years of Diocletian's retirement.

## What He Did
- **Administrative reform** — divided the empire into East and West with co-emperors (*tetrarchy*); capable administrator
- **The Great Persecution (303–311)** — four edicts against Christians; the most systematic imperial attack on Christianity
- **Retired (305)** — unprecedented; the only emperor to voluntarily retire; lived to see his persecution policy reversed
- **The Donatist legacy** — the question of what to do with *traditores* (clergy who surrendered Scriptures under persecution) split the North African church for a century; the Donatist controversy that [[Augustine of Hippo]] spent his career fighting was Diocletian's indirect legacy

## Significance
The failure of the Great Persecution proved Christianity was too embedded to be destroyed by force. Constantine's reversal three years later was not coincidental — it was a political reading of reality.

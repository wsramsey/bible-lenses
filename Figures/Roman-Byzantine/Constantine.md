# Constantine I
*272–337 · Roman Emperor · The Constantinian Shift*

> **Why he's here:** No single figure changed the external circumstances of Christianity more dramatically. Before Constantine: illegal, persecuted. After Constantine: legal, favored, then official. Whether this was salvation or corruption is the most contested question in church history.

## What He Did
- **Edict of Milan (313)** — ended persecution; granted religious toleration to all, including Christians
- **Called the Council of Nicaea (325)** — first ecumenical council; settled the Arian controversy (provisionally); produced the Nicene Creed
- **Built Constantinople** — a Christian capital city; moved the empire's center east
- **Funded church building** — including Old St. Peter's and the Church of the Holy Sepulchre
- **Baptized on his deathbed (337)** — delayed baptism was common; not unusual for the era

## Was He Actually Christian?
Genuinely uncertain. He:
- Continued to use pagan imagery on coins
- Kept the title *Pontifex Maximus* (chief pagan priest)
- Executed his own son Crispus and wife Fausta (motives unclear)
- Favored Christianity politically before he personally committed

The honest answer: probably a genuine (if confused) convert who understood Christianity imperfectly and used it politically. The two are not mutually exclusive.

## The Constantinian Shift Debate
- **Pro:** Christianity needed legal space to flourish; the Fathers largely welcomed it
- **Con:** [[John Howard Yoder]], [[Stanley Hauerwas]] — Constantinianism corrupted the church by identifying it with state power; the persecuted church was more authentic
- **[[N.T. Wright]]** — the shift was neither pure gain nor pure loss; the church had to learn to be powerful, and largely failed

## Influenced
- Every subsequent Christian political theology
- [[Eusebius of Caesarea]] (theologized Constantine as God's instrument)
- The entire medieval "Christendom" project
- [[Augustine of Hippo]]'s *City of God* (written partly in response to Constantinianism's failure)

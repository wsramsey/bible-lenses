# Julian the Apostate
*331–363 · Roman Emperor · The Pagan Reaction*

> **Why he's here:** His failure is as theologically significant as Constantine's success. Julian was raised Christian, secretly converted to Neoplatonism, became emperor in 361, and spent 20 months systematically dismantling Constantinian Christianity — reopening temples, removing Christian privileges, banning Christians from teaching classical literature. Then he died in battle in Persia. Christianization resumed immediately and permanently.

## What He Did
- Reopened pagan temples and restored sacrifices
- Banned Christians from teaching rhetoric and grammar (they were using pagan texts they didn't believe)
- Removed state subsidies from the church
- Tried to build a "pagan church" modeled on Christianity's organizational success
- Died June 363, reportedly saying "Thou hast conquered, O Galilean" (probably apocryphal)

## Significance
His reign proved that Christianization was not merely an imperial imposition — it had taken genuine root. When the pagan alternative was offered, the population didn't return to it. His attempt to copy Christian charity and community organization is a backhanded tribute to Christianity's social power.

## Influenced
- [[Gregory of Nazianzus]] (wrote two orations against Julian)
- All subsequent analysis of Christianization as social vs. merely political process

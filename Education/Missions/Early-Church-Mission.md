# The Apostolic and Early Church Mission (30–500 AD)

## Paul's Missionary Journeys (46–57 AD)
→ See [[Paul's Missionary Journeys]] for full account

The defining template:
- **Strategy**: target urban centers; synagogue first; then Gentiles
- **Method**: preaching + community formation + correspondence
- **Result**: churches in Ephesus, Corinth, Philippi, Thessalonica, Rome — the spine of the Roman Empire

## Other Apostolic Traditions

**Thomas in India** (tradition)
The Mar Thoma Christians of Kerala, India trace their origin to the Apostle Thomas (c. 52 AD). Historical evidence is indirect but the community is ancient — present by at least the 3rd century. The *Acts of Thomas* (early 3rd c.) describes a missionary journey to India.

**Mark in Alexandria** (tradition)
The Coptic Church traces its founding to Mark the Evangelist (c. 42 AD). Alexandria became the intellectual center of early Christianity.

**Peter and Paul in Rome** (tradition, largely confirmed)
Both died in Rome under Nero. The Roman church was established by the 50s AD — Paul's letter to the Romans addresses an existing community he hadn't founded.

## The Spread Within the Roman Empire (100–313 AD)
Christianity spread primarily through:
- **Networks**: merchants, soldiers, slaves, women
- **No professional missionaries**: ordinary Christians moving through the empire
- **Caring for the sick**: during plagues, Christians stayed when pagans fled — Rodney Stark's *The Rise of Christianity* argues this was decisive
- **The appeal to women**: Christianity gave women higher status than Roman society; the sexual ethics protected women
- **The appeal to slaves**: "Neither slave nor free" was a radical social message

## Key Missionaries to Germanic and Celtic Peoples

**Ulfilas / Wulfila (c. 311–383)**
Apostle to the Goths. Created the Gothic alphabet specifically to translate the Bible. Christianized the Visigoths — as Arians, which complicated their later relations with Nicene Rome.

**Patrick of Ireland (c. 385–461)**
Born in Roman Britain; kidnapped as a teenager; enslaved in Ireland; escaped; became a bishop; returned to Ireland as a missionary. His *Confessions* is one of the earliest Christian autobiographies.
- **What he found**: Iron Age Celtic paganism; Druids; a tribal society
- **What he did**: converted the high kings; established monasteries; worked within tribal structures rather than imposing Roman models
- **Result**: Celtic Christianity — one of the most creative and distinctive expressions of the faith; Irish monks later re-evangelized much of Western Europe

**Columba (521–597)**
Irish monk; founded Iona (Scotland, 563); from there re-evangelized northern Britain and helped preserve classical learning during the Dark Ages.

**Augustine of Canterbury (d. 604)**
Sent by Pope Gregory I to England (597). Converted King Æthelberht of Kent. The beginning of the English church in its Roman form — in tension with the existing Celtic Christianity from Ireland.

## The Coptic Mission to Ethiopia (4th century)
Frumentius — a Syrian Christian shipwrecked in Ethiopia, became tutor to the royal court, converted King Ezana of Aksum (c. 330). Athanasius of Alexandria consecrated Frumentius as the first Bishop of Ethiopia. The Ethiopian Orthodox Church (now 45 million members) traces its origin here.

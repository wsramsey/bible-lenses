# Christian Missions — Complete Overview
*The most honest account of Christianity's most complex export*

> Did missionaries help the people they went to? Did they hurt them? The honest answer: **both, often simultaneously, sometimes inseparably.** This overview attempts to tell both stories without minimizing either.

## The Question Your Friend Asked: Did They Help or Hurt?

### What They Helped
- **Medical care**: hospitals, clinics, treatment of leprosy, smallpox vaccination, maternal health — saved millions of lives
- **Education**: schools where none existed; literacy in languages that had no written form; primary education for girls and boys equally (revolutionary in many contexts)
- **Translation**: hundreds of languages received their first written form through missionary linguists; without this, those languages might have been lost
- **Legal protection**: missionaries often defended indigenous populations against colonial violence and slavery (Las Casas, Jesuits in Paraguay, Livingstone against the slave trade)
- **Social reform**: opposing foot-binding in China, widow-burning (*sati*) in India, female genital mutilation in Africa — controversial but real interventions against suffering
- **Economic development**: agricultural training, fair trade networks, craft cooperatives

### What They Hurt
- **Cultural destruction**: indigenous languages suppressed; traditional practices called "demonic"; traditional art, music, and dress condemned
- **Colonial complicity**: missionaries legitimized conquest by providing a moral language for it; "civilization and Christianity" was a colonizing ideology
- **Residential schools**: the worst outcome — children forcibly removed from families to be "civilized"; physical, sexual, and cultural abuse; Canada still discovering graves
- **Political dependency**: churches that were arms of colonial governments rather than indigenous movements
- **Theological imperialism**: Western Christianity presented as the only valid form; African, Asian, Latin American expressions dismissed or suppressed
- **Community disruption**: conversion created divisions within families and communities; social fabric torn

### The Honest Synthesis
The missionary movement was not monolithic. The same tradition produced:
- Bartolomé de las Casas AND the conquistadors he accompanied
- The Jesuit Reductions (protecting indigenous people from slavers) AND the Goa Inquisition
- William Wilberforce (against slavery) AND missionaries who accepted colonial structures
- Ida Scudder (founded a medical school in India for women) AND missionaries who burned traditional religious objects

The question is not "was it good or bad overall?" but "which missionaries, in which contexts, doing what?" — and then learning from both the good and the terrible.

## The Timeline

### 1. Apostolic Mission (30–100 AD)
- Paul's journeys across the Mediterranean
- Thomas tradition to India
- The church reaches Rome, Alexandria, Antioch within one generation

### 2. Roman Empire Mission (100–500 AD)
- Christianity spreads within the Empire
- Constantine's conversion changes the dynamic
- Ulfilas and the Goths; Patrick and Ireland

### 3. Medieval Mission (500–1500 AD)
- Benedictines in northern Europe
- Boniface in Germany
- Cyril and Methodius in Slavic territories
- Francis Xavier and the beginning of global mission
- The Crusades as a failed "mission" by force

### 4. The Great Century (1792–1914)
- William Carey; the modern missions movement
- The "scramble for Africa" context
- Medical missions (Livingstone, Grenfell)
- Women missionaries — the majority by 1900

### 5. The 20th Century (1900–2000)
- Edinburgh 1910 and the ecumenical movement
- The "indigenous church" principle
- Liberation theology's critique of mission
- The shift to partnership

### 6. The Reverse Mission (2000–present)
- African, Asian, and Latin American missionaries now come TO Europe and America
- The Global South is the new mission center

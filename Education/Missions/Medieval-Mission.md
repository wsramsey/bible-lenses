# Medieval Mission (500–1500 AD)

## Benedictine Mission in Northern Europe

**Gregory I and Augustine of Canterbury (597)**
Gregory the Great's famous letter to Augustine: don't destroy pagan temples — purify and repurpose them. Don't destroy pagan festivals — give them Christian content. One of the earliest articulations of **inculturation** (adapting the Gospel to local culture).

**Boniface "Apostle of Germany" (675–754)**
Anglo-Saxon monk; the great missionary to Germania.
- **The Felling of Donar's Oak (723)**: cut down the sacred oak of the thunder god at Geismar — expecting to be struck dead; wasn't; mass conversions followed
- Organized the German church; established dioceses; tied it to Rome
- Martyred by pagan Frisians 754
- His approach: direct confrontation with paganism; the power encounter model

**The Carolingian Mission (8th–9th century)**
Charlemagne's forced conversions of the Saxons — the dark side of medieval mission. Baptism at sword-point; Alcuin's protest that "faith is a free act of the will."

## Cyril and Methodius: The Apostles to the Slavs (860s)

**The Mission**
Two Greek brothers sent from Constantinople to Moravia (modern Czech Republic) in 863.

**Their innovation**: They created a new alphabet (Glagolitic, later Cyrillic) to write the Slavic languages, then translated the Bible and liturgy into Slavic (*Old Church Slavonic*). This was revolutionary — the Roman tradition insisted on Latin; the Eastern tradition respected vernaculars.

**The Cyrillic alphabet** (named for Cyril) became the script of Russian, Bulgarian, Serbian, Ukrainian, and dozens of other languages.

**The result**: Slavic Christianity — Bulgaria, Serbia, Russia, Ukraine all trace their Christianization to the Cyrillo-Methodian mission.

**The lesson**: When missionaries work in the vernacular and create indigenous scripts, the faith takes deep root. This contrasts with the Latin-only approach that kept Western European laypeople dependent on clerical intermediaries.

## The Franciscan and Dominican Missions (13th century)

Both mendicant orders engaged in mission:
- **Ramon Llull (1232–1316)**: Catalan Franciscan; the first systematic missiologist; learned Arabic; proposed founding language schools to train missionaries; martyred in North Africa; 265 books
- **John of Montecorvino (1247–1328)**: First Catholic missionary to China; translated NT and Psalms into Chinese; established a church in Beijing; reported 100,000+ baptisms

## The Jesuits and Global Mission (16th century)

**Francis Xavier (1506–1552)**
- Sailed with the Portuguese to India (1542); Goa
- Sri Lanka, Malacca, Moluccas (Indonesia), Japan
- Died trying to reach China
- Estimated 30,000+ baptisms; probably exaggerated but he was remarkably productive
- **Assessment**: effective communicator; worked within Portuguese colonial structure; the Goa Inquisition he requested was established after his death

**Matteo Ricci (1552–1610) — China**
The greatest example of missionary inculturation:
- Learned classical Chinese; dressed as a Confucian scholar
- Engaged Chinese philosophy on its own terms
- Brought Western science (mathematics, astronomy) as a "gift"
- Never directly attacked ancestor veneration; tried to find points of contact
- Reached the imperial court in Beijing
- His method: become Chinese to win the Chinese
- **The Chinese Rites Controversy**: After Ricci's death, Dominican missionaries complained that his accommodation of ancestor rites was idolatrous. Rome condemned the accommodated practices (1715). The mission largely collapsed. Many historians see this as one of history's greatest missed opportunities.

**The Jesuit Reductions of Paraguay (1609–1767)**
- 30 mission settlements (*reducciones*) among the Guaraní people
- Protected indigenous people from Spanish and Portuguese slave traders
- Developed a mixed economy; taught European crafts and music alongside Christianity
- The Guaraní had genuine agency; the missions were not simply paternalistic
- **Baroque music**: the Guaraní became accomplished musicians; recordings survive
- **Destruction**: the Jesuits were suppressed (1773) and the Reductions destroyed; the Guaraní were enslaved or dispersed
- **Assessment**: the most successful example of indigenous-centered mission in history; also destroyed by the colonial powers the Jesuits had tried to protect the Guaraní from

## The Crusades as Failed Mission

The Crusades were also framed as missionary enterprise — bringing the Holy Land "back" to Christianity. This is mission by conquest and it failed:
- Created permanent hostility between Christianity and Islam
- The Fourth Crusade's sack of Constantinople (1204) permanently damaged Christian-Orthodox relations
- The word "Crusade" remains toxic in Muslim-majority contexts today

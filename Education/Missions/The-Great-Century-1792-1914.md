# The Great Century of Missions (1792–1914)
*Kenneth Scott Latourette's name for the explosion of Protestant missions*

## Why This Period Was Unique
Convergence of:
- **Evangelical awakening** (First and Second Great Awakenings) produced missionary zeal
- **The British Empire** provided transport, security, and access to most of the world
- **Steamships** made global travel possible
- **Medical advances** allowed missionaries to survive tropical diseases (quinine for malaria, 1820s)
- **The printing press** enabled Bible translation at scale
- **Telegraph** allowed communication; coordination of global networks

## William Carey (1761–1834) — "The Father of Modern Missions"

**Background**: Baptist cobbler; self-taught linguist; read Captain Cook's voyages and became convinced that ordinary Christians had an obligation to evangelize the world

**The Enquiry (1792)**: *An Enquiry into the Obligations of Christians to Use Means for the Conversion of the Heathens* — the founding document of the modern missions movement

**His slogan**: "Expect great things from God; attempt great things for God"

**India (1793–1834)**: Arrived in Bengal; spent his first seven years with no converts; learned Bengali, Sanskrit, Marathi; translated the Bible into 6 languages; founded Serampore College (1818)

**What he did well**:
- Learned the languages deeply and produced serious linguistic scholarship
- Opposed the caste system and *sati* (widow-burning) — worked with Indian reformers to have it banned (1829)
- Founded the first vernacular newspaper in India (*Friend of India*)
- Never sought to impose English Christianity; worked in Indian languages

**What he didn't do well**:
- Operated under the protection and patronage of British colonialism
- The "civilization and Christianity" link was present in his thought
- His success enabled later missionaries who were far less culturally sensitive

## Adoniram Judson (1788–1850) — Burma/Myanmar

**The first American foreign missionary**

- Sailed to Burma (Myanmar) in 1812
- Spent 6 years with no converts; his wife Ann died; he was imprisoned for 21 months by the Burmese during the Anglo-Burmese War
- Eventually: first Burmese Bible (1837); founded the Karen church (the Karen became predominantly Christian)
- The Burmese church today (1.5 million+ Baptists) traces directly to Judson

**Assessment**: genuine cross-cultural engagement; stayed despite enormous personal cost; the Karen church is an indigenous church, not a colonial import

## David Livingstone (1813–1873) — Africa

**The most famous missionary of the Victorian era**

**His vision**: "Christianity, Commerce, and Civilization" — the three C's. End the slave trade by opening Africa to legitimate commerce. The Gospel and British trade as a package.

**Explorations**:
- First European to see Victoria Falls (1855)
- Discovered the source of the Nile (debated)
- His exploration opened central Africa to European contact

**Against the slave trade**: His revelations about the Arab slave trade in East Africa galvanized British public opinion. The slave trade was finally suppressed in Zanzibar in 1873 — the year Livingstone died.

**The Reckoning**:
- He was a genuinely compassionate man who died in Africa trying to end slavery
- But his explorations opened Africa to colonization; his "three C's" were explicitly a colonizing program
- The Congo (not his direct field) became the worst colonial atrocity in history (Leopold II)
- Africa's Christianization was genuine; Africa's colonization was devastating; they came together

## Hudson Taylor (1832–1905) — China

**Founder of the China Inland Mission (1865)**

**His innovation**: the *faith mission* model — no guaranteed salaries; no appeals for funds; trust God to provide; no denominational affiliation

**Cultural innovation**: dressed Chinese; wore a queue (pigtail); ate Chinese food; deeply opposed to the cultural imperialism of other missionaries

**The CI principle**: go to where no missionary has gone; the unevangelized interior; not the coastal cities where Western merchants were

**Result**: 800+ missionaries; 125,000 Chinese Christians by 1905

**Assessment**: genuine cultural respect; his Chinese dress was controversial among other missionaries but principled; the faith mission model has been influential and also enables underfunded, poorly trained missionaries

## Amy Carmichael (1867–1951) — India

**The model of total identification**

- Dyed her skin brown to blend in (early on)
- Learned Tamil deeply
- Founded the Dohnavur Fellowship (1901) — rescued children (especially girls) from temple prostitution (*devadasi*)
- Never returned to England after arriving in India in 1895
- *If* (1938) — her devotional book; a spiritual classic
- Died in India

**Assessment**: genuine love for Indian people; deep cultural sensitivity; but also worked within a framework that saw Indian religion as demonic and was rescuing children from their own religious tradition — the line between rescue and cultural imposition is complex here

## The Role of Women in 19th Century Mission

By 1900, **women constituted the majority of Protestant missionaries**. This was revolutionary:
- Women could work with women and children in cultures where men had no access
- The *zenana* missions in India: reaching women in purdah (secluded from men)
- Women's missionary societies were among the first organizations where women held organizational leadership
- Missionary service was one of the few professional careers available to educated women

**Key figures**: Charlotte Moon (Lottie Moon, China); Isabella Bird; Amy Carmichael; Ida Scudder (founded Women's Christian Medical College, Vellore, India)

## The Downside: Colonialism and Mission

**The structural problem**: Protestant missions in the 19th century were almost entirely dependent on European colonial power for:
- Security (colonial police and armies)
- Access (colonial treaties with local rulers)
- Prestige (association with the dominant power)
- Infrastructure (colonial roads, telegraph, steamships)

This dependency produced:
- **The "Scramble for Africa"** (1880s): missionaries often preceded colonizers and opened territories; some actively lobbied for colonial takeover of regions they were working in
- **Cultural imperialism**: "civilization" meant European civilization; African and Asian cultures were "heathen" to be replaced, not engaged
- **Residential schools**: exported from North America to Africa and Asia; the model of removing children from families to "civilize" them
- **Dependence**: churches that relied on foreign funding and leadership couldn't develop indigenous leadership or theology

## Indigenous Responses: The Independent Church Movement

By the late 19th century, African and Asian Christians began forming their own churches:
- **African Independent Churches (AICs)**: thousands of churches that broke from Western missions and developed distinctly African expressions of Christianity
- **The Ethiopian Movement** (South Africa, 1892): one of the first; protested Western cultural imperialism in Christianity
- **Simon Kimbangu** (Congo, 1921): founded the Kimbanguist Church; prophetic ministry; persecuted by Belgian colonials; 5 million members today
- **William Wade Harris** (Liberia, 1913–1914): one-man revival; reportedly 100,000 converts in 18 months; created churches that lasted after him

The message: African and Asian people were not passive recipients of the Gospel. They took it, made it their own, and often stripped out the Western cultural packaging.

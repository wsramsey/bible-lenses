# 20th Century Missions (1900–present)
*From Western export to global movement*

## Edinburgh 1910: The High-Water Mark and the Turning Point

The **World Missionary Conference, Edinburgh (1910)** — 1,200 delegates from Protestant missions worldwide — was simultaneously:
- The greatest gathering of Western missionary confidence
- The beginning of its critique

**What was there**: Optimism; "the evangelization of the world in this generation" (John R. Mott's slogan); confidence that Western Christianity could and should spread globally

**What was absent**: Almost no African, Asian, or Latin American delegates. The subjects of mission were not at the table.

**What it produced**: The modern ecumenical movement (leading to the World Council of Churches, 1948); missiology as an academic discipline

## The Indigenous Church Principle

Rufus Anderson and Henry Venn (mid-19th century) had already articulated the "three-self" principle:
- **Self-governing**: the indigenous church governs itself; no foreign control
- **Self-supporting**: the indigenous church funds itself; no foreign subsidy
- **Self-propagating**: the indigenous church evangelizes itself; no dependence on foreign missionaries

By the mid-20th century this was becoming normative theory, even if practice lagged:
- **Roland Allen**, *Missionary Methods: St. Paul's or Ours?* (1912): Paul's method was to plant churches and leave immediately; Western missions stayed and created dependence; prophetic critique
- **Donald McGavran** (1955): Church Growth movement; studied the sociological dynamics of how churches grow; "people movements"

## The Critique of Mission: Liberation Theology (1960s–)

**Gustavo Gutiérrez** and Latin American liberation theology turned the missions question inside out:
- The poor of the Global South are not objects of mission; they are subjects of their own liberation
- The "preferential option for the poor" means siding with the oppressed against their oppressors — even when the oppressors are "Christian"
- Much Western mission had served colonial power rather than the poor
- **The Medellín Conference (1968)**: CELAM (Latin American Bishops) embraced liberation theology

The critique was sharp: mission that doesn't address structural injustice is not Gospel mission. This produced enormous controversy in ecumenical circles.

## The Lausanne Movement (1974)

**The International Congress on World Evangelization, Lausanne, Switzerland (1974)** — Billy Graham and John Stott convened evangelical missionaries from around the world.

**The Lausanne Covenant** — affirmed:
- Evangelism *and* social responsibility are both integral to Christian mission
- "Mission" is not just saving souls but addressing the whole person and society
- Indigenous leadership must be prioritized
- The church in the Global South is a full partner, not a recipient

**The Wheaton Declaration (1966) and subsequent evangelical statements**: evangelical missions increasingly moved away from the cultural imperialism model

## Residential Schools: The Darkest Chapter

The residential school system in Canada, the United States, Australia, and elsewhere was missions' most catastrophic failure:
- **Canada**: 130+ residential schools operated by Catholic, Anglican, United, and Presbyterian churches with government funding (1831–1996 — the last closed in 1996)
- **Goal**: "Kill the Indian in the child" (Duncan Campbell Scott, Canadian official)
- **Method**: Remove children from families; forbid indigenous languages; teach European customs and Christianity
- **Abuse**: Systematic physical, emotional, and sexual abuse documented by the Truth and Reconciliation Commission
- **Deaths**: Over 6,000 deaths confirmed; many buried in unmarked graves (revealed by ground-penetrating radar, 2021)

**The Apologies**:
- **Pope Francis in Canada (July 2022)**: "I humbly beg forgiveness for the evil committed by so many Christians against the Indigenous peoples"
- **Anglican Church of Canada**: formal apology 1993
- **United Church of Canada**: formal apology 1986

This cannot be minimized. It is the clearest example of mission as harm — not a distortion of Christian mission but its institutionalization in a system that caused generational trauma.

## The Reverse Mission (2000–present)

**The most significant development in contemporary mission**: the Global South is now sending missionaries to the West.

- **African missionaries to Europe**: Nigerian Pentecostal churches; the Redeemed Christian Church of God has hundreds of congregations in the UK, Germany, and across Europe
- **Korean missionaries**: South Korea sends more missionaries per capita than any country except the US; Korean missionaries in China, Central Asia, the Middle East
- **Brazilian missionaries**: the Universal Church of the Kingdom of God; Brazilian missionaries across Africa and Europe
- **Filipino missionaries**: Catholic and Protestant; enormous diaspora church planting

**The implication**: Christianity is no longer a Western religion being exported to the world. It is a global religion whose center of gravity has shifted to the Global South — and it is now flowing back toward the secularizing West.

## Key Missionaries of the 20th Century

**Jim Elliot (1927–1956)**: Martyred by the Aucas (Huaorani) of Ecuador; his journal quote: "He is no fool who gives what he cannot keep to gain what he cannot lose"; *Through Gates of Splendor* (Elisabeth Elliot)

**Gladys Aylward (1902–1970)**: Cockney maidservant who became a missionary to China; refused by mission boards; went anyway; remarkable story

**Bruce Olson (1941–)**: Alone among the Motilone of Colombia; no organizational backing; extraordinary story of cultural immersion

**Jackie Pullinger (1944–)**: Hong Kong; drug addicts in the Walled City; Pentecostal; still there

**Lesslie Newbigin (1909–1998)**: The missiologist-theologian; served in India; returned to find Britain more "pagan" than India; wrote the foundational texts for mission in the post-Christian West (*The Gospel in a Pluralist Society*, 1989)

## The Unfinished Conversation

The missionary movement is still working through its relationship with colonialism. Key ongoing questions:
1. Can an apology for residential schools be adequate without structural change?
2. How do you distinguish "the Gospel" from "Western culture" when missionaries themselves often couldn't?
3. Who has the authority to define what "authentic" Christianity looks like in a given culture?
4. What does "partnership" actually mean when resources are so unequal?
5. Is the rapid growth of Christianity in Africa, Asia, and Latin America — despite its colonial origins — evidence that the Gospel transcended its carriers? Or evidence that indigenous people took it on their own terms?

# Major Seminaries and Christian Universities of the United States

## The Colonial Foundations: Training Puritan Ministers

**Harvard College** (1636, Cambridge, Massachusetts)
- Founded by the Massachusetts Bay Colony; named for minister John Harvard
- *New England's First Fruits* (1643): "After God had carried us safe to New-England... one of the next things we longed for and looked after was to advance learning and perpetuate it to posterity, dreading to leave an illiterate ministry to the churches"
- Original seal: *Veritas* (Truth) — originally with Christian imagery
- **Secularization**: Became Unitarian by the 1800s; secular research university by early 20th century; no longer trains ministers as primary mission; has a Divinity School (non-denominational, liberal)

**Yale University** (1701, New Haven, Connecticut)
- Founded by Congregationalists who found Harvard too liberal
- Jonathan Edwards graduated here 1720; George Whitefield preached here
- **Secularization**: Became liberal Protestant; secular by mid-20th century; Yale Divinity School remains (non-denominational, liberal)

**Princeton University** (1746, Princeton, New Jersey)
- Founded as the College of New Jersey by New Side Presbyterians
- Jonathan Edwards was briefly its president (died of smallpox 3 weeks in)
- **Secularization**: Princeton Theological Seminary separated as an independent institution (1812); the university became secular; PTS remains one of the leading mainline Protestant seminaries

**College of William & Mary** (1693, Williamsburg, Virginia)
- Anglican foundation; oldest in the South
- Episcopal; gradually secularized

**Dartmouth** (1769, Hanover, New Hampshire)
- Founded to educate Native Americans and English youth; Congregationalist
- Secularized; Ivy League

## The 19th Century Explosion: Denominational Colleges

Almost every American denomination founded colleges across the country:

### Presbyterian
- **Princeton Theological Seminary** (1812) — conservative Reformed; J. Gresham Machen left to found Westminster
- **Westminster Theological Seminary** (1929, Philadelphia) — Machen's conservative alternative after the fundamentalist-modernist split
- **McCormick Theological Seminary** (1829, Chicago)
- **Union Theological Seminary** (1836, New York) — became liberal flagship; Karl Barth lectured here; Paul Tillich taught here

### Baptist
- **Andover Newton** (1807) — Congregationalist/Baptist; oldest seminary in America
- **Southern Baptist Theological Seminary** (1859, Louisville) — the flagship SBC seminary; Al Mohler president since 1993; conservative resurgence
- **Southwestern Baptist Theological Seminary** (1908, Fort Worth)
- **Gordon-Conwell Theological Seminary** (1969, merger) — evangelical; Billy Graham involved in founding

### Methodist
- **Boston University School of Theology** (1839) — oldest Methodist school; liberal tradition; Edgar Brightman, L. Harold DeWolf (MLK's mentor)
- **Garrett-Evangelical Theological Seminary** (1853, Chicago)
- **Duke Divinity School** (1926) — major mainline Protestant school; Stanley Hauerwas taught here

### Lutheran
- **Lutheran Theological Seminary** (1864, Gettysburg) — oldest Lutheran; ELCA
- **Luther Seminary** (1869, St. Paul) — largest Lutheran; ELCA
- **Concordia Seminary** (1839, St. Louis) — LCMS; the "Walkout" of 1974 when 45 faculty left over inerrancy

### Catholic
**Saint Mary's Seminary** (1791, Baltimore) — oldest Catholic seminary in the US; founded by Sulpicians
**Notre Dame** (1842) — founded by Congregation of Holy Cross; maintained Catholic identity; became major research university; Catholic intellectual life
**Georgetown** (1789) — Jesuit; oldest Catholic university in US
**Catholic University of America** (1887, Washington DC) — the pontifical university; directly under Rome; major theological controversy center
**Boston College** (1863) — Jesuit; major Catholic research university

### Reform/Evangelical
**Moody Bible Institute** (1886, Chicago) — D.L. Moody's training school; non-denominational evangelical; practical ministry training
**Dallas Theological Seminary** (1924) — dispensationalist; trained generations of evangelical leaders; John Walvoord, Charles Ryrie, Howard Hendricks
**Fuller Theological Seminary** (1947, Pasadena) — "neo-evangelical" flagship; Harold Ockenga, Charles Fuller; attempted serious scholarship while maintaining evangelical orthodoxy
**Gordon-Conwell Theological Seminary** (1969) — evangelical; Billy Graham influence
**Trinity Evangelical Divinity School** (1897/1963, Deerfield IL) — Evangelical Free; D.A. Carson taught here
**Westminster Theological Seminary** (1929, Philadelphia) — conservative Reformed; Cornelius Van Til, J. Gresham Machen
**Reformed Theological Seminary** (1966, Jackson MS, multiple campuses) — conservative Reformed; PCA
**Regent College** (1968, Vancouver) — interdenominational evangelical graduate school; J.I. Packer taught here; "marketplace theology"

## The Secularization Pattern
Most originally Christian colleges followed a predictable arc:
1. **Founding** (17th–19th c.): train ministers; Christian confession required of trustees and faculty
2. **Expansion** (19th c.): add liberal arts and professional programs; grow enrollment
3. **Liberalization** (early 20th c.): faculty credentials matter more than faith; doctrinal requirements drop
4. **Secularization** (mid-20th c.): religious identity becomes ceremonial; chapel optional; theology marginalized
5. **Post-Christian** (late 20th c.): the founding religious identity is historical, not operational

**The Benne/Marsden thesis** (Robert Benne, George Marsden):
- Marsden's *The Soul of the American University* (1994): tracked the secularization of elite American universities
- Benne's *Quality with Soul* (2001): only 6 colleges maintained genuine Christian identity at research university level (Notre Dame, Valparaiso, Calvin, Wheaton, Baylor, St Olaf)

## Schools That Maintained or Strengthened Identity
**Wheaton College** (1860, Illinois) — "the Harvard of evangelical Christianity"; Billy Graham alumnus; required statement of faith for faculty; maintained evangelical identity
**Calvin University** (1876, Grand Rapids) — Christian Reformed; rigorous integration of faith and learning; Plantinga, Wolterstorff trained here
**Baylor University** (1845, Waco TX) — Baptist; became research university while maintaining Baptist identity; controversial
**Notre Dame** (1842) — Catholic; major research university maintaining Catholic identity

# Seminaries and Christian Universities
*From the medieval cathedral school to the secular research university*

## The Arc: Cathedral School → University → Seminary → Secular University

### Phase 1: Cathedral Schools (6th–12th century)
Every cathedral was required to maintain a school (Council of Rome, 1179). These were the intellectual centers of medieval Europe:
- Taught the seven liberal arts (trivium + quadrivium)
- Trained clergy for the church
- Open to laymen who could pay

Key cathedral schools: **Notre-Dame (Paris)**, **Canterbury**, **York**, **Chartres**, **Cologne**

### Phase 2: The Medieval Universities (12th–15th century)
Cathedral schools evolved into universities — self-governing communities of scholars with papal charters.
- [[University of Bologna (1088)]] — law
- [[University of Paris (c. 1150)]] — theology; the model for northern Europe
- [[University of Oxford (c. 1167)]] — grew from English students expelled from Paris
- [[University of Cambridge (1209)]]
- [[University of Salamanca (1218)]] — became the center of Counter-Reformation theology
- [[University of Cologne (1388)]] — Thomas Aquinas taught here

### Phase 3: Post-Tridentine Seminaries (1563–present)
The Council of Trent (1563) required every diocese to establish a **seminary** (from *seminarium* = seedbed) specifically for training priests:
- The Jesuit **Collegium Romanum** (1551) — prototype; became the Pontifical Gregorian University
- **Douai College (1568)** — English Catholic seminary in exile
- **Saint-Sulpice Seminary, Paris (1641)** — Sulpician model spread globally

### Phase 4: Protestant Academies (16th–17th century)
- **Geneva Academy (1559)** — Calvin's school; trained Reformed pastors sent across Europe
- **Wittenberg** (Luther's university) — became the first Protestant faculty of theology
- **Leiden University (1575)** — Dutch Reformed; the most Calvinist university
- **Harvard College (1636)** — "After God had carried us safe to New-England..."; trained Puritan ministers

### Phase 5: Secularization (18th–20th century)
Most originally Christian universities gradually secularized:
- Harvard: Unitarian by 1800s; secular research university by 1900s
- Yale: liberal Protestant by 1800s; secular by mid-20th century
- Oxford/Cambridge: formally Anglican but effectively secular
- Berlin (1810): Wilhelm von Humboldt's model of the research university — secular, scientific, professional; the template for all modern research universities
- Princeton: Presbyterian → secular research university; Princeton Theological Seminary separated 1812

### Phase 6: Denominational Schools Maintain Identity (20th–21st century)
Many Christian schools maintained or strengthened their religious identity:
- **Notre Dame** (Catholic, Indiana) — maintained Catholic identity through 20th century; now one of the leading research universities in the world
- **Wheaton College** (evangelical, Illinois) — "the Harvard of evangelical Christianity"
- **Calvin University** (Reformed, Michigan) — rigorous integration of faith and learning
- **Baylor University** (Baptist, Texas) — became a research university while maintaining Baptist identity

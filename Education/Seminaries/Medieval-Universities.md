# The Medieval Christian Universities (1088–1500)
*Christianity invented the university — and here is the full record*

## Why Christianity Produced Universities
The medieval university emerged from two specifically Christian needs:
1. **Theological training** — understanding Scripture, defending orthodoxy, training preachers
2. **Legal training** — canon law required scholarly expertise; the church needed lawyers

The university's structure (self-governance, degree-granting, faculties) was uniquely Christian — no equivalent existed in Islamic madrasas (which lacked degree-granting authority) or Chinese academies (which lacked institutional autonomy).

## What These Universities Did
Every medieval university was fundamentally a theological enterprise:
- **Theology** was the queen of sciences; all other disciplines served it
- The **liberal arts** prepared students for theology and law
- The **rector** was typically a clergyman
- The **papal charter** gave them independence from local bishops and kings
- **Disputations** were the primary pedagogical form — learning through argument

## The Transformation: Berlin (1810)
Wilhelm von Humboldt's founding of the University of Berlin (1810) created a new model:
- Research as the primary purpose (not training clergy)
- Academic freedom (*Lehrfreiheit* and *Lernfreiheit*)
- Professional faculties — law, medicine, theology all equal
- Secular in orientation

This model spread globally and is the prototype of all modern research universities. Most medieval universities gradually adopted it, secularizing in the process.

# Major Seminaries of Europe
*From Trent's mandate to the present*

## The Tridentine Mandate
Council of Trent (1563), Session XXIII, Decree on Reform: every diocese must establish a seminary to train priests. Before Trent, clergy training was haphazard — often apprenticeship with a local priest or university study without specific ministerial formation.

## Catholic Seminaries — Europe

### Rome (The Center)
**Pontifical Gregorian University** (1551)
- Founded by Ignatius of Loyola as the Collegium Romanum
- The Jesuit intellectual flagship; became the "greg" under Gregory XIII (1584)
- Hundreds of popes, cardinals, and bishops educated here
- Faculty included: Suárez, Bellarmine, Clavius

**Pontifical Biblical Institute** (1909)
- Founded by Pius X
- The world's leading center for Catholic Scripture studies
- Faculty: scholars like Augustin Bea, Roland Murphy

**Pontifical Lateran University** (1773)
- The "Pope's University"; theology and canon law

**Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas (Angelicum)** (1222/1908)
- Dominican; founded on the site where Thomas Aquinas taught
- Josef Pieper and John Paul II connected to its tradition

### France
**Séminaire Saint-Sulpice, Paris** (1641)
- Founded by Jean-Jacques Olier; the Sulpician model
- The most influential Catholic seminary model globally
- Sulpician Fathers exported the model to the Americas

**Institut Catholique de Paris** (1875)
- Major French Catholic research university
- Alfred Loisy taught here (before condemnation)

### Germany
**University of Tübingen** — Catholic and Protestant faculties
- The most important German theological institution
- Catholic faculty: Johann Adam Möhler, Hans Küng (before censure), Joseph Ratzinger taught here
- Protestant faculty: Ferdinand Christian Baur, Ernst Käsemann

**University of Münster** — Major Catholic theology
- Johann Baptist Metz (political theology) taught here
- Karl Rahner taught here

### England
**Douai College** (1568, Douai France; now Ushaw, England)
- English Catholic seminary in exile during Protestant Reformation
- Produced the Douay-Rheims Bible (1582–1609)
- Trained priests sent secretly into Protestant England; many were martyred

**Allen Hall / St. Edmund's College** (1568)
- Cardinal Allen's seminary; continued after Douai

### Netherlands
**University of Louvain (Leuven)** (1425)
- Most important Catholic university in northern Europe
- Erasmus lived here; Jansenist controversy originated here
- Split into Flemish (KU Leuven) and French (UCLouvain) in 1968

## Protestant Theological Schools — Europe

**Geneva Academy** (1559)
- Founded by Calvin and Theodore Beza
- The training center for Reformed pastors across Europe
- Graduates sent to France (Huguenots), Scotland (Knox), Netherlands, England
- Became the University of Geneva

**Wittenberg University** (1502, became Lutheranism's theological center)
- Luther and Melanchthon taught here
- The first Protestant university; set the template for Lutheran theological education
- Merged with Halle in 1817

**University of Marburg** (1527)
- First Protestant university founded *as* Protestant (Wittenberg pre-dated Reformation)
- Founded by Landgrave Philip of Hesse
- Luther-Zwingli debate on the Eucharist held here (Marburg Colloquy, 1529)

**University of Leiden** (1575)
- Founded by William the Silent after the Siege of Leiden
- The most Calvinist university; produced the Synod of Dort (1618)
- Arminius taught here; the Calvinist-Arminian controversy developed here
- Still a leading Dutch university

**University of Edinburgh** (1583)
- Presbyterian foundation; major Scottish theological center
- David Hume studied here (irony)

**Oxford and Cambridge**
- Both remained formally Anglican after the Reformation
- Kept theological faculties and chapel requirements until the 20th century
- Secularized gradually but retain theological schools
- Many heads of denominations trained here

# Poverty, Wealth, and Economic Justice
*"You cannot serve both God and Money" — and yet most churches try*

## Jesus on Wealth
Jesus says more about money than about any other topic except the Kingdom of God:
- "Blessed are the poor" (Luke 6:20) — not "poor in spirit" as in Matthew; the actual poor
- The Rich Young Ruler: "Go, sell everything you have and give to the poor" (Mark 10:21)
- Lazarus and the Rich Man (Luke 16) — the rich man in hell for ignoring the poor at his gate
- The Sermon on the Mount: "You cannot serve both God and Money" (Matthew 6:24)
- "It is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich man to enter the kingdom of God" (Matthew 19:24)

## The Theological Responses

### Catholic Social Teaching
The most systematic Christian approach to economic justice:
- **Leo XIII, *Rerum Novarum* (1891)**: private property is a right; but labor has rights; the state must protect the poor; condemned both laissez-faire capitalism and Marxist socialism
- **Subsidiarity**: decisions at the lowest possible level; the state should not do what families and communities can do
- **The Universal Destination of Goods**: the earth's goods are intended for all; private property is subordinate to this
- **The Preferential Option for the Poor** (John Paul II, following liberation theologians): the poor have a claim on our first attention

### The Social Gospel
Walter Rauschenbusch (*A Theology for the Social Gospel*, 1917): the kingdom of God requires transformation of social structures, not just individual conversion; capitalism is a social sin; the church must address systemic injustice.
**Status**: associated with liberal Protestantism; criticized for neglecting personal salvation

### Liberation Theology
[[Gustavo Gutiérrez]], [[James Cone]]: God's preferential option for the poor is the hermeneutical key to Scripture; doing theology begins with the experience of the oppressed; praxis (action for justice) is the primary theological act.
**Vatican response**: cautious critique (Ratzinger); some elements affirmed; Marxist analysis rejected

### Prosperity Gospel
God rewards the faithful with material wealth; poverty is a sign of insufficient faith; tithing produces financial return. Kenneth Copeland, Joel Osteen, Creflo Dollar.
**Theological assessment**: universally condemned by serious theologians; exploits the poor; contradicts the NT directly

### Evangelical Individualism
Personal charity and individual conversion are the Christian response to poverty; systemic/political solutions are not the church's business.
**Critique**: addresses symptoms but not causes; the OT prophets address systems

## What the Scholars Say
**[[Amos]]** — "I hate, I despise your religious festivals... but let justice roll on like a river" (5:21–24); worship without justice is an abomination; the most direct biblical statement

**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — the natural law right to property; but the poor have a right to take what they need for survival; property rights are not absolute; *usury* (lending at interest) is sinful

**[[Dorothy Day]]** — voluntary poverty as solidarity; the Catholic Worker; the poor are Christ; the works of mercy as primary Christian response

**[[Ron Sider]]** — *Rich Christians in an Age of Hunger* (1977): challenged evangelical indifference to global poverty; caused enormous controversy; "structural sin" concept

**[[Wayne Grudem]]** — *Business for the Glory of God*: wealth creation honors God; capitalism is the best poverty-reduction mechanism in history; the evangelical defense of free markets

**[[Walter Brueggemann]]** — the prophetic imagination; the alternative consciousness that refuses the royal consciousness of empire; the church must speak prophetically about economic arrangements

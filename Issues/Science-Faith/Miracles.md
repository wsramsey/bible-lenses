# Miracles
*Can the laws of nature be suspended? Should we believe reports that they have been?*

## The Classic Skeptical Argument (Hume)
David Hume (*An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*, 1748, Section X): "A miracle is a violation of the laws of nature; and as a firm and unalterable experience has established these laws, the proof against a miracle, from the very nature of the fact, is as entire as any argument from experience can possibly be."

In other words: the evidence for the regular operation of natural laws is always stronger than the evidence for any reported miracle. Therefore, we should never believe miracle reports.

## Christian Responses to Hume
**[[C.S. Lewis]]** — *Miracles* (1947): Hume's argument assumes naturalism; if God exists and created nature, miracles are not "violations" but special acts of the Creator within his own creation; the question of miracles is primarily the question of theism

**[[N.T. Wright]]** — miracles are not "violations" of natural law; they are actions of the God who is the ground of natural law; the resurrection is the paradigm case

**[[John Polkinghorne]]** — physicist and theologian; quantum indeterminacy opens space for divine action; miracles don't require "breaking" laws but acting through the regularities and indeterminacies already present

**[[Rudolf Bultmann]]** — "demythologizing": modern people cannot accept a miraculous world-view; the miracles must be reinterpreted existentially; the resurrection is not a resuscitation but an event of faith

## The Cessationism vs. Continuationism Debate
**Cessationism**: the miraculous gifts (tongues, healing, prophecy) ceased with the apostles.
- Key scholars: [[John MacArthur]], [[B.B. Warfield]] (*Counterfeit Miracles*, 1918)

**Continuationism**: the gifts continue throughout church history.
- Key scholars: [[Wayne Grudem]], [[Jack Deere]], virtually all Pentecostal and charismatic theologians

## The Healing Question
Does God heal miraculously today? The Pentecostal/charismatic tradition answers yes emphatically. The cessationist tradition says extraordinary miracles ceased; God heals through medicine. The majority evangelical view: God can heal but doesn't always; prayer for healing is appropriate; "health and wealth" claims are false.

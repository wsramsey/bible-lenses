# Biblical Inerrancy and Inspiration
*What kind of book is the Bible?*

## The Spectrum of Views

### Verbal Plenary Inspiration / Inerrancy
Every word of Scripture (in the original manuscripts) was divinely inspired; Scripture is without error in everything it affirms — including history and science.
- **Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy (1978)**: the definitive evangelical formulation; drafted by [[J.I. Packer]], [[R.C. Sproul]], [[Norman Geisler]]
- **Key scholars**: [[B.B. Warfield]], [[J. Gresham Machen]], [[Carl Henry]], [[Wayne Grudem]]

### Biblical Infallibility (without strict inerrancy)
The Bible is infallible in matters of faith and practice but may contain errors in historical and scientific matters.
- **Key scholars**: [[I. Howard Marshall]], some British evangelicals
- **Critique from inerrancy camp**: this distinction is untenable; if the Bible errs in history, how can it be trusted in doctrine?

### Inspired but Human
The Bible is genuinely inspired by God but produced through thoroughly human processes; it reflects the limitations of human knowledge; it can err; its authority is functional, not propositional.
- **Key scholars**: [[Peter Enns]] (*Inspiration and Incarnation*), [[John Goldingay]], much liberal Protestant scholarship
- **Evangelical critique**: this empties "inspiration" of meaningful content

### Canonical / Barthian
The Bible is the witness to God's revelation in Jesus Christ; it becomes the Word of God as the Spirit uses it; inerrancy is the wrong category because the Bible is personal address, not information deposit.
- **Karl Barth**: Scripture is the *witness* to revelation, not revelation itself; the three forms of the Word: the living Christ, the proclaimed Christ, the written Scripture

## The History of the Debate
- **Pre-critical church**: virtually universal assumption of divine authorship; errors were harmonized
- **Historical criticism (18th–19th c.)**: the Bible as a human document subject to scholarly analysis
- **Old Princeton** (Warfield, Hodge): responded to criticism by tightening the doctrine of inerrancy; the original manuscripts (autographs) are inerrant
- **Fundamentalist-Modernist controversy (1920s)**: inerrancy as a fundamentals criterion
- **The Battle for the Bible (1976)** (Harold Lindsell): accused non-inerrantists of apostasy; launched the evangelical inerrancy debate
- **Peter Enns** (2008): fired from Westminster Seminary for *Inspiration and Incarnation*; inerrancy vs. incarnational model of Scripture

## The Practical Questions
- What do we do with the apparent contradictions in the Gospels?
- What do we do with the violent passages in the OT?
- Does inerrancy require a historical Adam?
- How do we understand the development of the canon?
- Does inerrancy require young-earth creationism?

## Key Debates
**[[Bart Ehrman]] vs. evangelical scholars**: Ehrman left faith partly because of textual variants and apparent contradictions; his *Misquoting Jesus* argues the text is too uncertain to be the ground of faith

**[[N.T. Wright]]**: "inerrancy" is not the best category; "authority" is; Scripture is authoritative as the story that creates the community; but this doesn't require inerrancy in the technical sense

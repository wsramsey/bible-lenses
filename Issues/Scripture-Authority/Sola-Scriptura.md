# Sola Scriptura
*Scripture alone — or Scripture and Tradition?*

## The Reformation Principle
Luther's foundational principle: Scripture alone (*sola scriptura*) is the supreme authority for Christian faith and practice. No tradition, council, pope, or church father can override Scripture.

**The Diet of Worms (1521)**: "Unless I am convinced by the testimony of the Scriptures or by clear reason... my conscience is captive to the Word of God. I cannot and will not recant anything."

## The Catholic Response
Scripture and Tradition together form the single deposit of faith. The Church has the authority (through the teaching office, the Magisterium) to interpret both. Scripture is not self-interpreting.

**The Council of Trent** (1546): "the written books and the unwritten traditions" are received "with equal devotion and reverence."

**Vatican II** (*Dei Verbum*): Scripture and Tradition "flow from the same divine wellspring"; the Church's Magisterium is "not above the word of God, but serves it."

## The Orthodox Response
Scripture, Tradition, and the living experience of the Church form an organic whole. The Scriptures are *part* of Tradition — they emerged from and are interpreted by the community that produced them.

## What the Scholars Say
**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — Tradition is authoritative; the church's teaching authority (*Magisterium*) is necessary to settle disputed interpretations of Scripture

**[[Martin Luther]]** — Scripture alone; but the Church Fathers and councils are valuable when they agree with Scripture; they are not authoritative *per se*

**[[John Calvin]]** — *sola scriptura*; Scripture is "self-authenticating" (*autopiston*); the internal testimony of the Holy Spirit convinces the believer of Scripture's authority

**[[J.I. Packer]]** — *sola scriptura* does not mean "Scripture only, tradition never"; it means Scripture has supreme authority; tradition is valuable as a guide to interpretation but not equal to Scripture

**[[N.T. Wright]]** — *Scripture and the Authority of God*: Scripture exercises authority as the story that creates the community; *sola scriptura* can become an excuse for ignoring 2,000 years of theological wisdom; tradition matters

**[[Keith Mathison]]** — *The Shape of Sola Scriptura*: distinguishes *sola scriptura* (Scripture is the supreme norm) from *solo scriptura* (Scripture alone, ignoring tradition) — the latter is the problem of American evangelicalism, not the Reformation principle

## The Practical Tension
American evangelical Christianity often operates with *solo scriptura* in practice — "the Bible says it, I believe it, that settles it" — ignoring 2,000 years of interpretation. This produces both the freedom of individual conscience and the fragmentation of 30,000+ denominations.

# Ordination of Women
*Should women serve as pastors, priests, and bishops?*

## The Current State
- **Catholic**: No women's ordination; John Paul II declared in *Ordinatio Sacerdotalis* (1994) that this is "definitively held" — not technically infallible but close
- **Eastern Orthodox**: Same; no women's ordination
- **Anglican**: Divided; Church of England ordains women priests (1994) and bishops (2014); GAFCON/Global South opposed
- **Lutheran**: Most bodies ordain women; LCMS and WELS do not
- **Reformed/Presbyterian**: PCUSA ordains women; PCA and many Reformed bodies do not
- **Methodist**: Full equality since early Wesley
- **Baptist**: Divided; CBF affirms; SBC restricts pastoral roles to men
- **Evangelical**: The complementarian-egalitarian debate is the defining issue

## The Two Camps

### Complementarianism
Men and women are equal in dignity but have different roles (*telos*); male leadership in church and home is God's design, not cultural accident.
- Key texts: 1 Timothy 2:12 ("I do not permit a woman to teach or assume authority over a man"); 1 Corinthians 14:34–35 ("women should remain silent in the churches")
- Key scholars: [[John Piper]], [[Wayne Grudem]], [[Thomas Schreiner]]
- Organizations: Council on Biblical Manhood and Womanhood (CBMW)

### Egalitarianism
The texts restricting women reflect specific cultural situations, not timeless commands; the trajectory of Scripture (Galatians 3:28) points toward full equality; the restriction texts must be interpreted in light of women clearly exercising leadership elsewhere in the NT.
- Key texts: Galatians 3:28 ("neither male nor female"); Romans 16 (Phoebe as *diakonos*, Junia as apostle); Acts 2:17–18 (daughters will prophesy)
- Key scholars: [[N.T. Wright]], Gordon Fee, Scot McKnight, [[Phyllis Trible]]
- Organizations: Christians for Biblical Equality (CBE)

## What the Scholars Say
**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — women cannot be ordained because they lack the "state of pre-eminence" (*status eminentiae*) required for priestly representation; follows Aristotle's biology

**[[Karl Barth]]** — order in creation implies male leadership; but Barth's reasoning is from the covenant structure of male-female relations, not from Aristotle

**[[N.T. Wright]]** — 1 Timothy 2:12 is about a specific situation (Ephesian women spreading false teaching); the principle of Galatians 3:28 is normative; women should be ordained

**[[John Piper and Wayne Grudem]]** — *Recovering Biblical Manhood and Womanhood*: the most thorough complementarian case; the restrictions are permanent and theological, not cultural

**[[Junia Debate]]** — Romans 16:7: "Andronicus and Junia, outstanding among the apostles" — was Junia a woman? (Almost certainly yes — the name is feminine.) Was she an apostle? (The text says so.) This verse is the strongest single piece of evidence for women in apostolic leadership.

## The Hermeneutical Stakes
This issue is really about hermeneutics: how do you handle apparent tensions within Scripture? Which texts are the controlling ones? How do cultural context and canonical trajectory interact?

Those who restrict women's ordination: the specific commands override the general principle of Galatians 3:28
Those who affirm: the general principle interprets the specific commands; the specific commands are contextually bound

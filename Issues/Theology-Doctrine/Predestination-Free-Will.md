# Predestination and Free Will
*Does God choose us, or do we choose God? Can both be true?*

## The Historical Arc
This question has been fought in every century since Augustine:
- **Augustine vs. Pelagius** (5th c.) — grace vs. free will; the original formulation
- **Semi-Pelagianism condemned at Orange (529)** — humans cannot initiate salvation
- **Luther vs. Erasmus (1524–25)** — *Bondage of the Will* vs. *Free Will*; Luther: the will is enslaved; Erasmus: some cooperation is necessary
- **Calvin and Reformed theology (16th c.)** — double predestination; TULIP
- **Arminius and Arminianism (17th c.)** — conditional election; prevenient grace; resistible grace
- **The Synod of Dort (1618–19)** — affirmed Calvinism; condemned Arminianism
- **Wesley and Methodism (18th c.)** — Arminianism popularized; free will central to revivalism
- **Molinism (16th c., Jesuits)** — middle knowledge; God knows what free creatures would do in any possible situation

## The Calvinist Position (TULIP)
- **T**otal Depravity — the will is enslaved; humans cannot choose God without grace
- **U**nconditional Election — God chooses the elect based on his sovereign will, not foreseen faith
- **L**imited Atonement — Christ died specifically for the elect (debated even within Calvinism)
- **I**rresistible Grace — the elect cannot ultimately resist God's grace
- **P**erseverance of the Saints — the elect will persevere; "once saved, always saved"

**Key scholars**: [[John Calvin]], [[Jonathan Edwards]], [[John Owen]], [[John Piper]], [[R.C. Sproul]]

## The Arminian Position
- Conditional election — God foreknew who would freely believe; elected on that basis
- Prevenient grace — God's grace enables all to respond; removes inability without forcing belief
- Unlimited atonement — Christ died for all
- Resistible grace — grace can be finally rejected
- Conditional perseverance — apostasy is possible

**Key scholars**: [[Jacob Arminius]], [[John Wesley]], [[Roger Olson]]

## What the Major Scholars Say
**[[Augustine of Hippo]]** — the founder of Western predestinarian thinking; Romans 9 is his central text; God's election is not based on foreseen merit; grace is efficacious and irresistible; free will is real but subordinate to grace

**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — predestination is real; God's providence moves creatures according to their natures; free will is real and predestination doesn't destroy it; how they coexist is mystery

**[[Martin Luther]]** — *The Bondage of the Will*: his most systematic work; the will cannot turn toward God without grace; Erasmus is wrong; this is "the heart of the matter"

**[[John Calvin]]** — double predestination; some are elected to salvation, others to damnation; God's glory is served in both; the "horrible decree" he acknowledged but insisted on

**[[Karl Barth]]** — *Church Dogmatics* II/2: election is not about individuals being sorted into saved/damned; election is Jesus Christ himself, the elected and the rejected; all humanity is elected in him; universalist implications (debated)

**[[N.T. Wright]]** — election in Paul is primarily corporate (Israel, then the church), not individual; predestination is about God's purposes in history, not individual eternal destinies

## Key Questions
1. Does Romans 9 teach individual predestination or national election?
2. Are Calvinism and libertarian free will compatible? (Compatibilism vs. incompatibilism)
3. Does Molinism solve the problem? (Middle knowledge)
4. What are the pastoral implications of double predestination?
5. Can you preach the Gospel effectively if some are not elected?

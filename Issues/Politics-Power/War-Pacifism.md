# War and Pacifism
*"Blessed are the peacemakers" vs. "the sword-bearer does not bear it in vain"*

## The Three Positions

### Pacifism
Christians should not participate in war; violence is never justified.
- **Biblical basis**: Sermon on the Mount ("turn the other cheek"); "love your enemies"; "those who live by the sword die by the sword"; Jesus's non-resistance at his arrest
- **Early church**: most pre-Constantine Christian writers opposed military service
- **Traditions**: Quakers, Mennonites, Church of the Brethren, Amish
- **Key scholars**: [[John Howard Yoder]] (*The Politics of Jesus*), [[Stanley Hauerwas]], [[Dale Brown]]

### Just War Theory
War is sometimes justified when it meets specific criteria.
- **Augustine's original criteria**: just cause (defense/redress of wrong), right intention, proper authority, last resort
- **Aquinas added**: proportionality, discrimination (non-combatant immunity)
- **Traditions**: Catholic, mainline Protestant, most evangelicals
- **Key scholars**: [[Augustine]], [[Aquinas]], [[Reinhold Niebuhr]], [[Oliver O'Donovan]]

### Holy War / Crusade
God commands and endorses specific wars.
- **Biblical basis**: the Conquest narratives; the ban (*herem*); God commanding the destruction of enemies
- **History**: the Crusades; Jihad (Islamic parallel); Christian nationalism
- **Status**: no serious Christian theologian defends crusading theology today; the question of the Conquest narratives remains

## What the Scholars Say
**[[Augustine of Hippo]]** — the founder of just war theory; pacifism is irresponsible love of self; the neighbor who can be protected by force must be protected; love of enemy doesn't require passivity toward their violence

**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — systematized just war criteria; the conditions are strict; most wars fail to meet them; but war in defense of the innocent is obligatory charity

**[[Martin Luther]]** — "A soldier can be a Christian"; the magistrate's sword serves God; pacifism is only for private individuals, not for those responsible for others; but Luther's two-kingdoms framework contributed to German Lutheran quietism

**[[John Howard Yoder]]** — *The Politics of Jesus*: Jesus's nonviolence is not a counseled ideal but a social strategy; the cross is the model; "the Constantinian heresy" began when the church started approving violence; the Mennonite tradition is the faithful remnant

**[[Stanley Hauerwas]]** — Christianity is inherently pacifist; just war theory is a tool of liberal ideology that makes Christians available for imperial wars; the church's task is to be the church, not to manage the world's violence

**[[Reinhold Niebuhr]]** — *Moral Man and Immoral Society*: groups cannot be held to the same moral standards as individuals; nuclear deterrence is tragically necessary; Hauerwas is naive; love in a fallen world requires force sometimes

## The Nuclear Question
Nuclear weapons changed everything. Can any use of nuclear weapons satisfy just war criteria? (Almost certainly not — discrimination is impossible.) Should Christians support nuclear deterrence? Niebuhr said yes; many Catholic bishops said no (*The Challenge of Peace*, 1983).

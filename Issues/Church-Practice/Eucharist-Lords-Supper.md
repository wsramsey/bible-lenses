# The Eucharist / Lord's Supper
*"This is my body" — the four words that divided Christianity*

## The Four Major Positions

### Transubstantiation (Catholic)
At the consecration, the substance of bread and wine is converted into the body and blood of Christ, while the appearances (accidents) of bread and wine remain.
- **Defined at**: Fourth Lateran Council (1215); Council of Trent (1551)
- **Philosophical basis**: Aristotelian substance/accident distinction (*Aquinas*)
- **The Real Presence**: Christ is truly, really, and substantially present — body, blood, soul, and divinity

### Consubstantiation / Real Presence (Lutheran)
Christ's body and blood are truly present "in, with, and under" the bread and wine — but the bread and wine are not changed. Both the bread and Christ's body are truly present.
- **Luther vs. Zwingli at Marburg (1529)**: Luther wrote "This is my body" on the table in chalk and refused to move from the literal meaning; the Protestant Reformation split here
- **The real presence**: genuine, not symbolic; but not transubstantiation

### Spiritual Presence (Reformed/Calvinist)
Christ is truly present in the Supper, but spiritually — his body is at the right hand of the Father; the elements remain bread and wine; faith receives the spiritual benefits of Christ's body and blood.
- **Calvin** vs. both Luther (too physical) and Zwingli (too merely symbolic)
- The faithful receive Christ "spiritually but truly"

### Memorialism (Baptist/Zwinglian)
The Lord's Supper is a memorial — a commemoration of Christ's death, not a means of grace. "Do this in remembrance of me" (1 Cor 11:24) — in memory, not in repetition.
- **Zwingli** at Marburg vs. Luther
- The most common position in evangelical non-denominational churches

## What the Scholars Say
**[[Ignatius of Antioch]]** (c. 107): "The Eucharist is the flesh of our Savior Jesus Christ, the flesh which suffered for our sins" — earliest post-NT affirmation of Real Presence

**[[Thomas Aquinas]]** — transubstantiation; the most philosophically sophisticated defense; the Aristotelian categories of substance and accident; Christ is wholly present under either species

**[[Martin Luther]]** — real presence; "This is my body" means what it says; the Zwinglian/Reformed view is a human subtlety imposed on the plain text; Marburg was the tragedy of the Reformation

**[[John Calvin]]** — the true body is at the Father's right hand; the Spirit lifts us up to receive it spiritually; both Luther (too physical) and Zwingli (too bare memorial) are wrong

**[[Alexander Schmemann]]** — *The Eucharist* (Orthodox): the Eucharist is the church becoming what it is; the meal *is* the kingdom; it is both sacrifice and meal; liturgy is theology enacted

**[[Henri de Lubac]]** — *Corpus Mysticum*: the shift from "mystical body" meaning the Eucharist to "mystical body" meaning the church is the key to understanding medieval sacramental theology

## The Ecumenical Problem
The Eucharist is the center of Christian worship — and the point where Christians cannot commune together. Catholics and Orthodox cannot receive communion with Protestants; Lutherans and Reformed historically cannot commune together. The broken table is the most visible sign of Christian disunity.

# Alasdair MacIntyre
*1929– · Catholic · Virtue Ethics*

> **Why he's here:** His *After Virtue* (1981) is the most important book in moral philosophy of the last 50 years — and its conclusion is that only an Aristotelian-Thomistic framework can rescue ethics from the incoherence of modernity. MacIntyre's trajectory (Marxist → Aristotelian → Thomistic Catholic) is one of the great intellectual conversion stories of the 20th century. [[Rod Dreher]]'s Benedict Option is directly inspired by *After Virtue*'s closing image of a "new St. Benedict."

## Historical Context
Born in Scotland, educated in Britain, spent most of his career at American universities (Notre Dame, Duke). Converted to Catholicism in 1983, two years after *After Virtue* was published.

## Key Concepts
- **The Enlightenment project has failed** — moral philosophy since the 17th century has tried to provide a rational foundation for morality without reference to human nature or *telos*; it cannot succeed; the result is emotivism (moral claims express only feelings)
- **Virtue ethics** — ethics requires a thick account of human nature (*telos*), the virtues that perfect it, and the practices that cultivate them; only Aristotle (and Aquinas) provides this
- **Tradition-constituted rationality** — there is no rationality outside a tradition; reason is always reason within a tradition; the question is which tradition is most internally coherent and capable of resolving its own crises
- **Practice** — a coherent and complex socially established cooperative activity (chess, architecture, farming, medicine) with internal goods achievable only by participating in it; virtues are necessary for participation in practices
- **The new Benedict** — *After Virtue* closes: "We are waiting not for a Godot, but for another — doubtless very different — St. Benedict"

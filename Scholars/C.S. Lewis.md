## Historical Context
Britain 1898-1963: two World Wars, the collapse of Victorian confidence, the rise of scientific materialism, the BBC as a new mass medium. Lewis fought in WWI at age 19 — saw friends die. The interwar period was deeply skeptical of Christianity in educated British culture. He was writing for people for whom Christianity seemed intellectually impossible.

## Biography — What Shaped This Thinker
Mother died of cancer when he was 9. Sent to boarding school (largely miserable). Wounded at the Somme in 1918. Atheist through his 20s. Converted to theism in 1929 (described as being 'the most dejected and reluctant convert in all England'), then to Christianity in 1931 after a night conversation with Tolkien and Dyson. The Inklings — weekly discussions with Tolkien, Charles Williams, Owen Barfield — were his intellectual community. Late in life married the American poet Joy Davidman, who died of cancer in 1960; *A Grief Observed* is the raw aftermath.

## Written Where & Why
*Mere Christianity* (1942-44) — originally BBC radio broadcasts during WWII, written for people with no religious background. *The Problem of Pain* (1940) — his first Christian apologetic book, written before he'd personally suffered much. *A Grief Observed* (1961) — written in a notebook after Joy's death, never intended for publication. The contrast between *The Problem of Pain* and *A Grief Observed* is itself a theological statement about theory vs. lived experience.

## Contemporaries & Intellectual World
J.R.R. Tolkien (closest friend, instrumental in his conversion), Charles Williams (Inklings, deep influence on Lewis's supernatural fiction), Dorothy Sayers (mutual respect, both Anglican lay apologists), Owen Barfield (lifelong friend, philosophical influence on his view of imagination), G.K. Chesterton (major influence though they never met closely — *The Everlasting Man* was pivotal).

## Writing Style & Influence on Others
Clarity as a moral obligation — Lewis believed obscure writing was intellectual cowardice. Uses analogy and homely example rather than technical vocabulary. Influenced by his training in English literature (he was primarily a medievalist, not a theologian). His apologetics work through the imagination as much as argument — Narnia teaches theology through story.

## Pressures & Controversies
Never fully accepted by academic theology (Cambridge gave him a chair in Medieval Literature, not Theology). His popular success was held against him by the academy. His personal life was unconventional — he lived for decades with the mother of a WWI friend in a quasi-domestic arrangement that puzzled and sometimes scandalized.

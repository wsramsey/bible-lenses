# Hildegard of Bingen
*1098–1179 · Catholic · Visionary Mysticism*

> **Why she's here:** The most polymath of all Christian mystics — theologian, composer, healer, scientist, prophet, and abbess. Her visionary theology centers on *viriditas* (greenness/vitality) as a cosmic force of divine life running through creation. Declared a Doctor of the Church in 2012.

## Historical Context
12th-century Germany, Benedictine reform era. The church was in a period of institutional renewal (Gregorian reform); Bernard of Clairvaux was redefining monastic spirituality. Hildegard's visions were officially approved by Pope Eugenius III — remarkable for a woman in this era.

## Biography
Born to a noble Rhineland family, offered to the church as a child oblate. Spent her life at Disibodenberg and later founded her own monastery at Rupertsberg near Bingen. Despite chronic illness, she composed music, wrote theology, practiced medicine, corresponded with emperors and popes, and undertook preaching tours in her 60s.

## Key Concepts
- **Viriditas** (greenness) — divine vitality/life-force flowing through creation; health is viriditas, sin is its withdrawal
- **Living Light** — her term for divine vision; she described seeing "the reflection of the Living Light," never God directly
- **Feather on the Breath of God** — her self-description; radical dependence and smallness before divine power
- **Cosmic anthropology** — humanity as microcosm; body and soul, reason and nature, all interconnected in God

## Writing Style
Visionary, cosmic, dense with imagery. Her music (*Ordo Virtutum*, antiphons) is arguably her most stunning work — unlike anything else in medieval chant.

## Shaped By
- [[Bernard of Clairvaux]] (correspondence; he endorsed her visions)
- Benedictine Rule and liturgical tradition
- [[Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite]] (indirect)

## Influenced
- Modern feminist theology (reclaimed as major female voice)
- Creation spirituality (Matthew Fox's controversial retrieval)
- Contemporary natural/embodied spirituality

## Primary Works
- *Scivias* (Know the Ways, 1141–1151)
- *Book of Life's Merits*
- *Book of Divine Works*
- *Physica* and *Causae et Curae* (natural science/medicine)
- *Ordo Virtutum* (musical morality play)

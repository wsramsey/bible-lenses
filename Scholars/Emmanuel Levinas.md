# Emmanuel Levinas
*1906–1995 · Jewish · Ethics as First Philosophy*

> **Why he's here:** A Jewish philosopher who has shaped Christian ethics more than almost any Christian thinker of the 20th century. His core claim — that the face of the Other makes an infinite ethical demand before any cognition or choice — became the philosophical foundation for Christian theologies of hospitality, the stranger, human dignity, and the preferential option for the poor. [[Pope John Paul II]]'s personalism is in dialogue with Levinas; [[Henri Nouwen]]'s spirituality of the other is soaked in him.

## Historical Context
Born in Lithuania, educated in Strasbourg and Freiburg (studied under Husserl and Heidegger), survived WWII in a German POW camp while his family was murdered in Lithuania. The Holocaust is the background to everything he wrote.

## Key Concepts
- **The face** (*le visage*) — the face of the Other is the site of infinite ethical demand; it says "thou shalt not kill"; it cannot be reduced to a theme or concept
- **Ethics as first philosophy** — against Heidegger (ontology as first philosophy); the encounter with the Other precedes and grounds all cognition; responsibility comes before freedom
- **Infinite responsibility** — my responsibility for the Other is not limited; "I am my brother's keeper" — infinitely; the Other's need makes a claim I cannot fully discharge
- **The trace of the Infinite** — God is encountered not as presence but as trace — in the face of the Other; negative theology via the Other
- **Totality and Infinity** — against totality (systems that absorb the Other into the Same); infinity is the Other's irreducibility

## Shaped By
- Edmund Husserl (phenomenology; translated him into French)

- [[Franz Rosenzweig]] (*Star of Redemption*)

## Influenced
- **[[Jacques Derrida]]** (his closest dialogue partner; wrote on Levinas throughout)
- **[[Pope John Paul II]]** (personalism and the face)
- **[[Henri Nouwen]]** (hospitality theology)
- Liberation theology (preferential option for the poor)
- All Christian theologies of the Other

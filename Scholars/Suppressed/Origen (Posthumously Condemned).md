# Origen (Posthumously Condemned)
*184–253 AD · Suppressed: 553 AD — 300 years after his death · Method: posthumous council-condemnation (553 AD — 300 years after death)*

> ⚠️ This voice was suppressed. Its inclusion here does not imply endorsement — it reflects the historical reality that the shape of orthodoxy was partly determined by who had institutional power to silence dissent.

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origenism)

## Their Theology
The condemned positions: pre-existence of souls, apokatastasis (universal restoration including Satan), spiritual bodies at resurrection, allegorical interpretation displacing literal, subordinationist Christology.

## Suppressed By
- [[Second Council of Constantinople (553)]]
- [[Emperor Justinian]]

## What Survived Despite Suppression
His allegorical method survived everywhere — Jerome, Augustine, medieval exegesis all use it while officially rejecting 'Origenism.' His universalist tendencies surface in Gregory of Nyssa, Hans Urs von Balthasar's Dare We Hope, Thomas Talbott. His biblical scholarship (Hexapla) is foundational for all subsequent textual criticism.

## Why It Matters
He was condemned for things he speculated about, not taught as dogma. The condemnation happened during a political controversy (Three Chapters Controversy) that had little to do with him. His influence was so pervasive that condemning him was like condemning the air the church breathed.

## Unexpected Connection (The idea didn't die)
Every time a preacher reads a text allegorically — 'the crossing of the Red Sea represents baptism' — they are using Origen's method while standing in a tradition that officially condemned him. He is the most influential theologian most Christians have never heard of.

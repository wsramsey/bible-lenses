# Pelagius
*354–420 AD · Suppressed: 418 AD (Council of Carthage condemned Pelagianism) · Method: council-condemnation / writings mostly destroyed*

> ⚠️ This voice was suppressed. Its inclusion here does not imply endorsement — it reflects the historical reality that the shape of orthodoxy was partly determined by who had institutional power to silence dissent.

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pelagius)

## Their Theology
Humans have genuine free will and can choose good without special divine grace. Adam's sin affected him but not all humanity by inheritance. God's grace is primarily the example of Christ and Scripture's teaching. His ethics were rigorous — he was horrified by the moral laxity of Roman Christians.

## Suppressed By
- [[Augustine of Hippo]]
- [[Council of Carthage (418)]]
- [[Pope Zosimus (reversed)]]

## What Survived Despite Suppression
Arminianism, Methodism, much of popular Christianity's assumption that humans decide for God, Eastern Orthodoxy's rejection of original sin as transmitted guilt, modern therapeutic theology.

## Why It Matters
The Pelagian controversy defined Western Christianity's understanding of grace, free will, and original sin. Augustine's victory here shaped everything from Catholic sacramental theology to Calvinist predestination to evangelical altar calls. Pelagius lost the argument but his intuitions keep returning.

## Unexpected Connection (The idea didn't die)
Every time an evangelical says 'I decided to follow Jesus,' they are being more Pelagian than Augustinian. The theological tradition officially rejected Pelagius; popular Christianity has never stopped practicing him.

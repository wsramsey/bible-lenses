# The Cathars (Albigensians)
*12th–14th century · Suppressed: 1244 AD (fall of Montségur), 1321 AD (last known Cathar) · Method: inquisition / crusade / extermination*

> ⚠️ This voice was suppressed. Its inclusion here does not imply endorsement — it reflects the historical reality that the shape of orthodoxy was partly determined by who had institutional power to silence dissent.

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catharism)

## Their Theology
Dualism: the material world was created by an evil god (the OT God/Demiurge); the spiritual world by a good God. Christ was a spiritual being who appeared to have a body (Docetism). The Catholic church was the church of the Demiurge. Rigorous asceticism (perfecti) alongside ordinary believers (credentes). Strong women's leadership.

## Suppressed By
- [[Innocent III]]
- [[Albigensian Crusade (1209-1229)]]
- [[Inquisition]]

## What Survived Despite Suppression
We know their theology almost entirely through Inquisition records of their interrogation — the Cathars' own voice is largely lost. Their memory survives in southern French culture. Some of their ethical instincts (anti-violence, women's spiritual equality) resonate in modern progressive Christianity.

## Why It Matters
The Albigensian Crusade was the first crusade against European Christians — the precedent that the Pope could call a holy war against Christian heretics. The Inquisition was institutionalized partly to deal with the Cathars. These two institutions shaped Catholic-Protestant relations for centuries.

## Unexpected Connection (The idea didn't die)
The Dominicans (Order of Preachers) were founded specifically to convert the Cathars by preaching. Thomas Aquinas and the entire Dominican intellectual tradition exist partly because the Cathars posed a theological challenge that required a systematic response.

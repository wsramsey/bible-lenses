# Flannery O'Connor
*1925–1964 · Catholic · Southern Gothic Fiction*

> **Why she's here:** The greatest American Catholic fiction writer and one of the sharpest theological minds of the 20th century. Her stories are violent, grotesque, and full of grace precisely because she understood that a secular audience needs large, shocking gestures to see what a religious audience perceives in the ordinary. Her letters (*The Habit of Being*) and essays (*Mystery and Manners*) are essential reading on faith and art.

## Historical Context
Mid-20th century American South. O'Connor lived on a farm in Milledgeville, Georgia, with lupus — the same disease that killed her father and would kill her at 39. She wrote two novels and 32 short stories in 13 years.

## Key Concepts
- **The grotesque as grace** — violent, distorted moments in her fiction are the points where grace breaks through; "to the hard of hearing you shout, and for the almost-blind you draw large and startling figures"
- **The Misfit and the grandmother** — her most famous story (*A Good Man Is Hard to Find*) is a theology of grace under pressure
- **Sacramental vision** — matter is the vehicle of the spiritual; the Catholic imagination sees creation as always already graced
- **The prophet as freak** — her characters who see truly are always outsiders, misfits, grotesques

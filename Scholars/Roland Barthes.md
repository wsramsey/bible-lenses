# Roland Barthes
*1915–1980 · Literary Theorist · Death of the Author*

> **Why he's here:** His 1967 essay "The Death of the Author" is the most cited and most contested piece in modern literary theory — and a direct challenge to the hermeneutical method most evangelical scholars use. Barthes argued that meaning is not deposited in a text by an author and recovered by a reader; it is produced by the reader in the act of reading. The author's intention is irrelevant to meaning. This is the position [[E.D. Hirsch]] and [[Anthony Thiselton]] spent careers refuting.

## Key Concepts
- **Death of the Author** — the author's biographical person and intentions are irrelevant to the text's meaning; once written, the text is cut loose
- **The birth of the reader** — as the Author dies, the Reader is born; meaning is produced in reading, not deposited in writing
- **Mythology** — his early work (*Mythologies*) analyzed how bourgeois culture naturalizes its ideologies through signs; everything from wrestling to steak carries hidden ideological meaning
- **The pleasure of the text** — texts offer both readerly pleasure (*plaisir*, comfortable) and writerly jouissance (*jouissance*, disruptive); great texts disrupt comfortable reading

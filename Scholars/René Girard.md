# René Girard
*1923–2015 · Catholic · Mimetic Theory*

> **Why he's here:** The most original Christian thinker of the late 20th century who wasn't a theologian. Girard began as a literary critic, developed a unified theory of human culture, and ended by arguing that Christianity — specifically the cross — is the unique event in human history that exposes and dismantles the scapegoat mechanism that founds all human civilization. His conversion to Catholicism followed from his intellectual work, not the other way around.

## Historical Context
20th century France and America. Girard spent most of his career at Stanford. His trajectory moved from literary criticism (*Deceit, Desire and the Novel*, 1961) to anthropology (*Violence and the Sacred*, 1972) to theology (*Things Hidden Since the Foundation of the World*, 1978). Each book extended and deepened a single thesis.

## Key Concepts
- **Mimetic desire** — human desire is not autonomous; we learn what to desire by imitating others (*mimesis*); desire is always triangular: subject → model → object
- **Mimetic rivalry** — when two people desire the same object via mimesis, they become rivals; rivalry escalates toward violence
- **Scapegoat mechanism** — communities resolve mimetic rivalry by unanimously directing violence against a single victim; the scapegoat's death restores peace; the community then sacralizes the victim (religion is born)
- **Myth conceals the victim** — all myth and archaic religion hides the arbitrary innocence of the scapegoat; the founding murder is covered up
- **The Bible unveils the victim** — the Hebrew Bible (especially Job, the Psalms, the Servant Songs) begins to take the victim's side; the Gospels complete this by making the scapegoat God himself
- **The cross as revelation** — the crucifixion is not a sacrifice that satisfies God but the definitive exposure of the scapegoat mechanism; God is not the one demanding violence but the victim of it
- **The Paraclete** — the Holy Spirit as the Advocate for victims; the Spirit continues Christ's work of exposing sacred violence

## Shaped By
- Dostoevsky, Cervantes, Shakespeare, Stendhal (literary analysis that generated the theory)
- [[Sigmund Freud]] (mimetic desire as alternative to the Oedipus complex)
- [[Friedrich Nietzsche]] (engaged and refuted)
- [[Claude Lévi-Strauss]] (structural anthropology, critiqued)

## Influenced
- **[[James Alison]]** (Girardian theology)
- **[[N.T. Wright]]** (atonement theology aware of Girard)
- **[[Hans Urs von Balthasar]]** (parallel concerns about sacrifice)
- Theological anthropology broadly
- Peace theology / nonviolence (Girard was a committed pacifist)

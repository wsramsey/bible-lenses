## Historical Context
Europe 1914-1968: Barth's theology was born in the trenches of World War I. As a young Swiss pastor, he watched his liberal theology professors sign the 'Manifesto of the 93' supporting Kaiser Wilhelm's war. That destroyed his confidence in liberal Protestant theology which had made human culture and civilization its measuring rod. World War II, the Holocaust, the Cold War — Barth's theology of God's absolute sovereignty and the 'infinite qualitative difference' between God and humanity was forged in these crucibles.

## Biography — What Shaped This Thinker
Pastored a working-class congregation in Safenwil during WWI. Saw firsthand that liberal theology's optimism about human progress was catastrophically wrong — the civilized nations of Europe were slaughtering each other. His commentary on Romans (1919) was, in Karl Adam's phrase, 'a bombshell on the playground of the theologians.' Had a complex decades-long relationship with Charlotte von Kirschbaum, who lived in his household and collaborated on the *Church Dogmatics* — a source of ongoing controversy.

## Written Where & Why
*Epistle to the Romans* (1919, revised 1922) — written in a country parsonage, drawing on Kierkegaard and Paul to blow up liberal theology. *Church Dogmatics* (1932-1967, 13 volumes, unfinished) — written at Basel over 35 years, the largest systematic theology in Christian history. During the Nazi period, wrote the *Barmen Declaration* (1934) in a single night — the Confessing Church's theological resistance to Nazi ideology.

## Contemporaries & Intellectual World
Emil Brunner (ally turned opponent — the famous 'Nein!' exchange on natural theology), Rudolf Bultmann (lifelong interlocutor — Barth thought Bultmann capitulated too much to existentialism), Paul Tillich (parallel German theologian in conversation and tension), Bonhoeffer (student who both honored and questioned him), Hans Urs von Balthasar (Catholic who wrote the first major book on Barth).

## Writing Style & Influence on Others
Enormously dense, discursive, repetitive by design — circling a subject from multiple angles. He plays music (Mozart) while writing. The *Dogmatics* is not a reference book; it's a sustained argument that must be read in motion. Famous for his refusal of 'system' in one sense while producing the largest systematic theology in history. His prose has a musical quality — theme, variation, development.

## Pressures & Controversies
Expelled from Germany in 1935 for refusing to take the unconditional oath to Hitler. Spent WWII in Basel watching Europe burn. After the war, refused to condemn the Soviet Union as sharply as Nazi Germany, which alienated many American allies. His universalist tendencies (everyone may be elect in Christ) kept orthodox Calvinists suspicious.

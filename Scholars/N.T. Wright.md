## Historical Context
Late 20th century: the fragmentation of mainline Christianity, the rise of biblical scholarship as a secular academic discipline, the New Perspective on Paul debate reshaping NT studies. Wright works in both the academy (Oxford, Cambridge, St. Andrews) and the church (Bishop of Durham 2003-2010), insisting that good scholarship and living faith need not be separated.

## Biography — What Shaped This Thinker
Converted to serious Christian commitment at Oxford. Taught at McGill, Oxford, Cambridge, St. Andrews, and served as a bishop simultaneously. His experience of institutional church — synods, committees, administration — gives his work an unusually grounded, anti-abstract quality. His popular books (Simply Christian, Surprised by Hope) deliberately translate academic work for ordinary readers.

## Written Where & Why
*Christian Origins and the Question of God* series (5 vols, 1992-2021) — a career-spanning argument that the historical Jesus, Paul, and the NT make more sense read within 1st-century Jewish apocalyptic categories than through either conservative or liberal Protestant filters. *Surprised by Hope* (2008) — written after years of bishop's ministry, argues that bodily resurrection has been replaced by Platonic soul-escape in popular Christianity, with devastating practical consequences.

## Contemporaries & Intellectual World
E.P. Sanders (whose *Paul and Palestinian Judaism* 1977 started the New Perspective that Wright extended), James Dunn (fellow New Perspective scholar), Richard Bauckham (ally on historical Jesus), John Piper (major opponent — their debate on justification in *The Future of Justification* 2007 is a landmark Reformed-NPP exchange), Marcus Borg (liberal Jesus Seminar scholar — they co-wrote a book from opposite positions).

## Writing Style & Influence on Others
Prolific and accessible — writes both 800-page academic tomes and 200-page popular books on the same subjects. Uses the metaphor of a five-act play for Scripture. Strong on narrative and story as theological categories. Sometimes criticized for being too confident and too prolific — writes faster than critics can respond.

## Pressures & Controversies
Caught between conservative evangelicals who think his NPP undermines justification by faith and liberal scholars who think he's too committed to orthodoxy. His defense of bodily resurrection and his opposition to certain readings of Paul alienate both camps.

## Reacted Against
- [[Rudolf Bultmann (Demythologization)]] — *Bultmann stripped the apocalyptic and historical skeleton from Paul, leaving only existentialist encounter. Wright insists the bones are the point*
- [[Traditional Protestant Justification]] — *Not rejecting it but reframing it: forensic justification is real but secondary to covenantal inclusion — Paul's 'works of the law' are boundary markers not merit-earning*
- [[Platonic Heaven in Popular Christianity]] — *His central pastoral concern: Christians believe in going to heaven when they die (Plato), not bodily resurrection and new creation (Paul). Surprised by Hope is his intervention*

## Forced By (Crisis / Event / Controversy)
- [[E.P. Sanders — Paul and Palestinian Judaism (1977)]] — *Sanders's book demolished the caricature of Judaism as a works-righteousness religion. Wright had to either integrate this or ignore it — he built his whole Pauline theology around integrating it*
- [[4QMMT Dead Sea Scroll]] — *The discovery that 'works of the law' (ma'ase ha-Torah) is an actual phrase in a Qumran legal document — about boundary markers between communities, not merit accumulation — confirmed the New Perspective reading Wright was developing*

## Enabled By (Technology / Context / Person)
- [[Oxford Historical-Critical Method]] — *Training in rigorous historical scholarship gave Wright tools to read Paul within 1st-century Judaism rather than projecting 16th-century Reformation debates backward*
- [[Bishop of Durham (2003-2010)]] — *The episcopal experience — governing, preaching, administering a diocese — grounds his theology in institutional reality. His popular books are written by someone who has actually pastored, not just professed*

## Parallel Convergent (Independent Similar Conclusions)
- [[James D.G. Dunn]] — *Dunn and Wright developed New Perspective arguments in parallel, sometimes in dialogue, sometimes in tension — they agree on the basic framework but differ on details of justification*
- [[Richard Bauckham]] — *Independent but convergent work on the historical reliability of the Gospels — Bauckham's 'Jesus and the Eyewitnesses' complements Wright's historical Jesus volumes*

# Stanley Fish
*1938– · Literary Theorist · Interpretive Communities*

> **Why he's here:** His concept of "interpretive communities" is the most practically useful and most theologically significant idea from reader-response theory. Fish argues that texts don't have meanings — interpretive communities do. The community's shared assumptions and practices determine what counts as a valid reading. For biblical interpretation this raises the urgent question: which community's reading of Scripture is authoritative? The academy's? The church's? The tradition's?

## Historical Context
American literary theory, 1970s–1990s. Fish's *Is There a Text in This Class?* (the title is a real classroom incident: a student asked a professor this question about a course, meaning "is there a textbook?"; the professor thought the student was asking a hermeneutical question) became the defining reader-response text.

## Key Concepts
- **Interpretive communities** — groups of readers who share interpretive strategies; these strategies produce the "text" they read; there is no text independent of interpretive practice
- **Against the plain sense** — there is no neutral, pre-interpretive reading of a text; every "plain reading" is the product of an interpretive community's conventions
- **Self-consuming artifacts** — his earlier work on 17th century literature (Milton, Bacon); texts that undermine themselves in the act of reading
- **Pragmatism** — Fish is ultimately a pragmatist; he doesn't claim there's no meaning, only that meaning is produced locally by communities

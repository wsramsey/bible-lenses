# Al-Ghazali
*1058–1111 AD · Islamic Philosophy / Theology · Islamic mysticism / Occasionalism*

> **Theological lean:** The Incoherence of the Philosophers — rational philosophy cannot reach God. Mystical experience (Sufi tariqa) as the path. God's direct causation of all events (occasionalism). Revival of religious sciences through spiritual practice.

## Historical Context
The height of Islamic civilization — Baghdad was the intellectual capital of the world. Al-Ghazali had a spiritual crisis at the peak of his fame (sounds like Augustine), left his prestigious professorship, wandered for years as a Sufi, and returned to write his masterwork. His critique of Aristotelian philosophy echoes throughout Scholasticism — Aquinas was responding to Al-Ghazali's arguments.

## Biography — What Shaped This Thinker
Taught at the Nizamiyya madrasa in Baghdad — the most prestigious Islamic academic post. At age 37 had a spiritual breakdown — lost the ability to speak, left everything, wandered for 11 years in Syria and Mecca as a Sufi, returned to write Ihya Ulum al-Din (Revival of Religious Sciences). Died teaching.

## Hermeneutical Approach
Mystical — the heart's direct experience of God transcends rational argument. Allegorical reading of Quran for inner meaning. The outward scholar must become the inward Sufi.

## Historical Context
Germany 1933-1945: Hitler's rise, the Nazification of the German church (Deutsche Christen), the Confessing Church resistance, the Holocaust. Bonhoeffer watched the German church capitulate to Nazi ideology in real time — pastors putting swastikas in their churches, blessing Hitler's armies. The Barmen Declaration (1934) was the Confessing Church's resistance statement. He was involved in the July 20, 1944 assassination plot against Hitler.

## Biography — What Shaped This Thinker
Born into Berlin's educated elite — his father was a psychiatrist, his family was upper-class intellectual secular. Became a theologian partly to shock his family. A year at Harlem's Abyssinian Baptist Church (1930-31) under Adam Clayton Powell Sr. transformed him — he encountered the Black church's fusion of gospel and resistance to oppression, and it radicalized his commitment to costly discipleship. His twin sister Sabine married a Jewish man; watching their persecution made the stakes personal.

## Written Where & Why
*The Cost of Discipleship* (1937) — written as director of an illegal Confessing Church seminary at Finkenwalde (later shut down by Gestapo). *Life Together* (1939) — written after Finkenwalde was closed, describing what genuine Christian community looks like. *Letters and Papers from Prison* — written from Tegel Military Prison (1943-45), smuggled out by his friend Eberhard Bethge. His most fragmentary and prophetic work — concepts like 'religionless Christianity' and 'world come of age' have fueled decades of theological debate.

## Contemporaries & Intellectual World
Karl Barth (teacher and theological father — Bonhoeffer's *Act and Being* engages Barth; they had a complicated mutual respect), Reinhold Niebuhr (offered Bonhoeffer escape to America in 1939 — Bonhoeffer returned to Germany after two weeks, saying he must share the suffering of his people), Hans von Dohnanyi (brother-in-law, co-conspirator), Eberhard Bethge (best friend, biographer).

## Writing Style & Influence on Others
Dense and exact in his academic work; urgent, pastoral, and searingly honest in his prison letters. The *Cost of Discipleship* has a prophetic, preacher's rhythm. The prison letters are exploratory, sometimes fragmented — he's thinking through new territory without a net. His concept of 'cheap grace' vs 'costly grace' became one of the most quoted distinctions in 20th-century Christianity.

## Pressures & Controversies
Constant Gestapo surveillance. The seminary at Finkenwalde was illegal — they lived as a fugitive community. His fiancée Maria von Wedemeyer was 18 when they became engaged; he was in prison most of their engagement. Hanged at Flossenbürg concentration camp, April 9, 1945 — three weeks before Germany surrendered.

## Reacted Against
- [[Deutsche Christen (German Christians)]] — *The Nazi-aligned church movement that put swastikas in churches and blessed Hitler's armies — Bonhoeffer's entire career is a refutation of this capitulation*
- [[Cheap Grace in German Lutheranism]] — *Lutheran 'grace alone' had become permission to live however you liked — Bonhoeffer's 'costly grace' directly attacks this*

## Forced By (Crisis / Event / Controversy)
- [[Nazi Rise to Power (1933)]] — *Hitler's appointment as Chancellor in January 1933 forced every German Christian to decide: accommodation or resistance. Bonhoeffer chose resistance from the first week*
- [[Aryan Paragraph (April 1933)]] — *Nazi law excluding Jewish Christians from the German church — Bonhoeffer immediately and publicly opposed it, putting him outside the mainstream church*

## Enabled By (Technology / Context / Person)
- [[Abyssinian Baptist Church, Harlem (1930-31)]] — *A year studying in New York. The Black church's fusion of gospel and resistance to oppression radicalized his understanding of costly discipleship — he later said this was when Christianity became real to him*
- [[Finkenwalde Seminary (1935-37)]] — *Running an illegal underground seminary gave him the lived laboratory for Life Together — Christian community as resistance structure*

## Biographical Causes (Personal Experience → Theology)
- [[Sabine Leibholz (twin sister)]] — *His twin sister married a Jewish man (Gerhard Leibholz). Watching their persecution was not abstract — it was his family. This made the stakes incarnate*
- [[Return from America (June 1939)]] — *Reinhold Niebuhr got him a safe position in New York. Bonhoeffer returned after two weeks: 'I must share the trials of this time with my people.' One of the most consequential decisions in modern theology*
- [[Tegel Prison (1943-45)]] — *The 18 months in prison — letters smuggled out by Eberhard Bethge — produced his most fragmentary and explosive ideas: 'religionless Christianity,' 'the world come of age,' 'Christ the centre'*

## Controversies / Movements Spawned

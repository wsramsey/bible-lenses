# Charles Taylor
*1931– · Catholic · The Secular Age*

> **Why he's here:** His *A Secular Age* (2007) is the most important account of how the West became secular — and why that matters for Christianity. Not a simple secularization thesis (modernity → religion declines) but a story of how the conditions of belief changed: from a world where faith was the default to one where it is a contested option among many. Every serious Christian engagement with Western culture since 2007 is in conversation with Taylor.

## Historical Context
Contemporary Canada (Montreal). Former Oxford professor, McGill philosopher, winner of the Templeton Prize (2007) and Kyoto Prize (2008). A committed Catholic whose philosophical work is never merely academic.

## Key Concepts
- **The immanent frame** — modernity constructs a self-sufficient natural order that can be understood and operated without reference to the transcendent; this is the "frame" within which secular people experience reality
- **The buffered self** — the modern self is "buffered" against the porous world of spirits, enchantment, and cosmic meaning that pre-moderns inhabited; the self is its own source
- **The porous self** — pre-modern selfhood; permeable to outside forces (demons, spirits, God, nature); meaning comes from outside
- **Cross-pressures** — even secular people experience pressure from transcendence (great art, death, love, moral demand); this is the "cross-pressure" that keeps belief alive as a live option
- **Subtraction stories** — the myth that modernity simply "removed" religious superstition to reveal the rational human; Taylor argues modernity *added* new constructions (the buffered self, the immanent frame) rather than simply subtracting religion

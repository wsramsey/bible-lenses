## Historical Context
Mid-20th century America: Cold War anxiety, nuclear threat, the civil rights movement, Vietnam War, Vatican II opening the Catholic Church. Merton's monastery (Gethsemani, Kentucky) was simultaneously a place of radical withdrawal from the world and, through his writing, an address to its deepest crises.

## Biography — What Shaped This Thinker
Born in France to a New Zealand painter and American Quaker mother; orphaned by 16. Reckless youth at Cambridge (a scandal involving a woman and possibly a child he never acknowledged). Converted to Catholicism at Columbia University under influence of literature professor Mark Van Doren and reading of Étienne Gilson and Gerard Manley Hopkins. Entered the Trappist monastery at Gethsemani in 1941 — his abbot initially forbade him to write, then relented. The tension between contemplative withdrawal and prophetic engagement defines his entire career.

## Written Where & Why
*The Seven Storey Mountain* (1948) — autobiography written at Gethsemani in his early monastic years; became an enormous bestseller, sending hundreds of young men to monasteries. *New Seeds of Contemplation* (1962) — mature statement of contemplative theology. His Asian journal and correspondence with D.T. Suzuki, Thich Nhat Hanh, and the Dalai Lama written in his final years when he was finally allowed to travel; he died in Bangkok in 1968 from accidental electrocution.

## Contemporaries & Intellectual World
Thomas Merton's correspondents are his real contemporaries — he lived in a monastery but wrote letters to everyone: Boris Pasternak, Czesław Miłosz, Dorothy Day, Martin Luther King Jr., Thich Nhat Hanh, the Dalai Lama, Erich Fromm. An enclosed monk with a global address book.

## Writing Style & Influence on Others
Fluid, essayistic, luminous prose. Moves between autobiography, theology, poetry, and social criticism without warning. His journals are among the great American literary journals of the 20th century. He wrote fast and revised little — his superiors sometimes worried about quality control.

## Pressures & Controversies
Censorship by his monastery's superiors — they blocked or delayed many of his most prophetic writings on war and race. His desire to live as a hermit was resisted for years. His late relationship with a student nurse ('M.') was a profound emotional crisis that he eventually resolved in favor of his monastic vows.

# Protestant Canon (66 books)
*66 books · Protestant · Finalized: Reformation era (Luther 1520s, Westminster Confession 1647)*

> 39 OT (Hebrew canon only) + 27 NT. Luther removed the 7 deuterocanonical books from equal status based on Jerome's earlier distinction and because 2 Maccabees 12 supported purgatory. He also questioned Hebrews, James, Jude, and Revelation in the NT (placed them at the back) but kept them. His successors retained all 27 NT books.

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biblical_canon#Protestantism)

## Disputed Books
Luther's 'antilegomena': Hebrews ('who wrote it?'), James ('epistle of straw' — appears to contradict justification by faith), Jude (quotes non-canonical Enoch), Revelation ('Christ is not taught or known in it')

## Theological Impact
The Reformation was partly a canon argument. By removing 2 Maccabees, Luther removed the primary textual basis for purgatory and prayers for the dead. The canon decision drove the doctrinal difference, not just the other way around.

## Key Canon Decisions
- **Luther's German Bible (1534)** — Separated deuterocanon into an appendix labeled 'Apocrypha: not equal to Holy Scripture'
- **Westminster Confession (1647)** — Formally defined the 66-book Protestant canon
- **39 Articles of Religion (Anglican)** — Lists the books of Scripture, following Jerome's Hebrew canon principle

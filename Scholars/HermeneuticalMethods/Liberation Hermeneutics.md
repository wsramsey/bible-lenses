# Liberation Hermeneutics

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberation_theology)

> Scripture read from the perspective of the poor, marginalized, and oppressed. The Exodus is the paradigmatic biblical story: God takes sides with the enslaved against their oppressors. Truth is disclosed from below, not from the academy. Gustavo Gutiérrez, James Cone, and Howard Thurman represent Catholic, Black American, and African-American Protestant streams of this hermeneutic.

## Philosophical Roots
- Marxist analysis (mediated, not adopted wholesale)
- Latin American base communities
- Black church tradition
- Paulo Freire (Pedagogy of the Oppressed)

## How It Works
1. The 'epistemological privilege of the poor' — the underside of history reveals what power conceals
2. Praxis before theory — action for justice shapes theological reflection, not the reverse
3. The Exodus as master narrative: God hears the cry of the oppressed and acts
4. Re-read familiar texts from the bottom: the Beatitudes as addressed to the literally poor
5. Scripture's meaning is distorted when read only from positions of privilege and power

## Primary Sources It Illuminates
- Exodus
- Prophetic literature (Amos, Isaiah, Micah)
- Magnificat (Luke 1)
- Beatitudes
- James

## Strengths
Recovers prophetic tradition. Takes seriously the actual social context of the biblical world. Connects theology to ethics and action.

## Critiques
Can reduce Scripture to a social program. Risk of reading only what confirms a pre-existing ideology. Questions about universality: whose liberation? At whose expense?

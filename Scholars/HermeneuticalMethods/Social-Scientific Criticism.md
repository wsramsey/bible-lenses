# Social-Scientific Criticism

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social-scientific_criticism_of_the_Bible)

> Applies models from cultural anthropology and sociology to the biblical world. Bruce Malina and John Neyrey led this movement. Key insight: the biblical world operated on honor/shame values, patron-client relationships, limited-good worldview, and group-oriented (not individual-oriented) identity — all completely foreign to modern Western readers. Understanding these social dynamics is essential for reading the text correctly.

## Philosophical Roots
- Cultural anthropology (Mary Douglas, Victor Turner)
- Sociology of knowledge (Peter Berger)
- Mediterranean cultural studies

## Key Practitioners
- [[Bruce Malina]]
- [[John Neyrey]]
- [[Jerome Neyrey]]
- [[Ben Witherington III (socio-rhetorical)]]

## How It Works
1. Identify the cultural models operating in the text (honor/shame, purity/pollution, patron-client)
2. A 1st-century reader would have understood these without being told — we must reconstruct them
3. Honor = public reputation managed through challenge-riposte exchanges
4. Limited good: resources are fixed — if you gain, I lose. Explains envy and generosity texts.
5. Group orientation: identity is corporate, not individual. 'I' texts may mean 'we'

## Primary Sources It Illuminates
- Pauline epistles (patron-client, honor-shame)
- Gospel of John (in-group/out-group)
- Acts (household conversions)
- 1 Corinthians (status divisions at Lord's Supper)

## Strengths
Illuminates texts that seem strange to modern readers. Grounds exegesis in actual cultural context. Explains social dynamics in Paul's communities.

## Critiques
Can over-schematize — not every 1st-century person operated identically. Models can become procrustean. Sometimes more interested in the model than the text.

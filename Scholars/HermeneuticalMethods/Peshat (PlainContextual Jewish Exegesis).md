# Peshat (Plain/Contextual Jewish Exegesis)

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peshat)

> The primary Jewish exegetical method — read the text in its plain contextual meaning, attending to grammar, syntax, and the flow of the narrative. Rashi epitomizes peshat. Contrasted with derash (homiletical/midrashic expansion), remez (allegorical), and sod (mystical/Kabbalistic). The four levels are sometimes memorized as PaRDeS (orchard). Peshat is the foundation; the other levels build on it.

## Philosophical Roots
- Rabbinic tradition
- Medieval Jewish rationalism
- Saadia Gaon (biblical grammar)
- Rashi
- Ibn Ezra (grammar-focused)

## Key Practitioners
- [[Rashi]]
- [[Abraham Ibn Ezra]]
- [[Nahmanides (Ramban)]]
- [[David Kimhi (Radak)]]

## How It Works
1. What do the words mean grammatically and syntactically?
2. What is the flow of the narrative — what comes before and after?
3. Are there parallel texts that illuminate this one?
4. What did the Talmudic rabbis say? (Peshat interacts with tradition, not in isolation)
5. Only move to derash, remez, or sod if peshat doesn't exhaust the meaning

## Primary Sources It Illuminates
- Torah (all five books)
- Psalms
- Prophets — especially Isaiah

## Strengths
Grounds interpretation in the actual text. The primary method of most Protestant exegesis (though rarely credited to its Jewish roots). Resists arbitrary spiritualizing.

## Critiques
Even 'plain meaning' is contested. What seems plain in Hebrew may be opaque in translation. Peshat and derash interact in ways that make clean separation impossible.

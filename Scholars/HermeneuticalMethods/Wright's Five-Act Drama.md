# Wright's Five-Act Drama

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N._T._Wright)

> N.T. Wright's framework for understanding how Scripture functions as authority. The Bible is like a Shakespeare play where the final act has been lost — we have Acts 1-4 (Creation, Fall, Israel, Jesus) and the beginning of Act 5 (the early church). We must improvise Act 5's continuation in a way consistent with the earlier acts, not mechanically repeat them. This explains why Christians don't sacrifice animals or stone adulterers.

## Philosophical Roots
- Narrative theology
- Canonical criticism (Childs)
- Virtue ethics (we need character, not just rules)

## Key Practitioners
- [[N.T. Wright]]
- [[Samuel Wells (Improvisation The Drama of Christian Ethics)]]

## How It Works
Act 1: Creation — God and the good world
Act 2: Fall — human rebellion and its consequences  
Act 3: Israel — God's covenant people, the story of Scripture
Act 4: Jesus — the climax of Israel's story, the hinge of history
Act 5: Church — we live here, improvising faithfully in light of Acts 1-4
The resurrection launched Act 5 but we don't have the script — we have the author's character revealed in Acts 1-4

## Primary Sources It Illuminates
- The whole biblical canon as unified narrative
- The tension between OT law and NT ethics
- How to apply ancient texts to modern situations

## Strengths
Explains why we don't simply repeat OT practices. Makes the whole Bible relevant without flattening it. Gives a role to Christian ethics and discernment.

## Critiques
The 'lost act' metaphor implies more discontinuity than some are comfortable with. Who decides what 'faithful improvisation' looks like?

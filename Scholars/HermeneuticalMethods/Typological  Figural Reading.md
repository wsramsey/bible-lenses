# Typological / Figural Reading

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typology_(theology))

> Reads OT persons, events, and institutions as 'types' that foreshadow their 'antitypes' in the NT. Adam is a type of Christ (Romans 5). The Exodus is a type of redemption. The Temple is a type of Christ's body. This is the primary hermeneutic of the NT itself (Hebrews, Paul, Matthew) and of the Patristic tradition. It reads the OT as Christian Scripture without allegorizing it away.

## Philosophical Roots
- The NT's own reading strategy
- Patristic tradition
- Hans Urs von Balthasar (theological aesthetics)
- Erich Auerbach (Mimesis — figural interpretation as historical)

## Key Practitioners
- [[Irenaeus of Lyon (recapitulation)]]
- [[Origen (but tends to allegory)]]
- [[Augustine]]
- [[Matthew's Gospel]]
- [[Hebrews]]
- [[Gerhard von Rad (OT Theology)]]
- [[Christopher J.H. Wright]]

## How It Works
1. A type is a real historical person/event that also prefigures something greater
2. The type doesn't lose its historical reality — it's not just a symbol
3. The antitype fulfills and surpasses the type — Christ > Adam, New Exodus > Exodus
4. Progressive revelation: the OT is genuinely pointing forward, not randomly
5. The whole OT has a Christological direction without every text being a prediction

## Primary Sources It Illuminates
- Adam/Christ typology (Romans 5, 1 Cor 15)
- Exodus/Redemption typology (1 Cor 10)
- Melchizedek/Christ (Hebrews 5-7)
- Temple typology
- Servant Songs (Isaiah 40-55) read as Christ

## Strengths
Reads the OT as the church actually received it. Honors both historical reality and theological meaning. Connects the two Testaments organically.

## Critiques
Risk of imposing NT meaning on OT texts. Can ignore the OT's own theological integrity. Distinction between typology and allegory is sometimes blurry.

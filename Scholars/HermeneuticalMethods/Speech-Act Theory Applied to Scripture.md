# Speech-Act Theory Applied to Scripture

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speech_act_theory)

> J.L. Austin and John Searle distinguished locution (what is said), illocution (what the speaker is doing — promising, commanding, asserting), and perlocution (the effect on the hearer). Kevin Vanhoozer and Nicholas Wolterstorff applied this to Scripture: the Bible doesn't just contain information (locution) but performs divine speech acts (illocutions) — God promises, warns, commands, comforts through the text.

## Philosophical Roots
- J.L. Austin (How to Do Things with Words)
- John Searle
- Paul Ricoeur (distanced speech acts in written texts)

## Key Practitioners
- [[Kevin Vanhoozer]]
- [[Nicholas Wolterstorff]]
- [[Anthony Thiselton]]

## How It Works
1. Ask not just WHAT the text says but WHAT it is DOING
2. A promise is a different kind of speech act than a command or a description
3. God as the ultimate author performs illocutions through the human authors' discourse
4. Hermeneutics = identifying the illocutionary force of texts across historical distance
5. Meaning is stable because illocutions can be replicated across time (unlike perlocutions)

## Primary Sources It Illuminates
- Prophetic utterances (divine illocutions)
- Psalms (laments, praises as speech acts)
- Epistles (commands, promises)
- Narrative (authoritative testimony)

## Strengths
Sophisticated account of how language works. Preserves divine authorship without ignoring human authors. Provides a basis for Scripture's authority beyond inerrancy debates.

## Critiques
Highly technical — can lose the text in the theory. Depends on determining authorial intent, which faces its own challenges.

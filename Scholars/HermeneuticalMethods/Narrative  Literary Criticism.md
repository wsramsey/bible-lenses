# Narrative / Literary Criticism

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biblical_narrative)

> Reads the Bible as literature — analyzing plot, character, point of view, narrator, implied reader, and narrative rhetoric. Developed in biblical studies by Robert Alter (Hebrew narrative) and Hans Frei (the narrative identity of Jesus). Moved away from questions of historical reference to questions of literary meaning. The story shapes the reader, regardless of whether every detail is historically verifiable.

## Philosophical Roots
- New Criticism (literary theory)
- Robert Alter (The Art of Biblical Narrative)
- Frank Kermode
- Hans Frei (The Eclipse of Biblical Narrative)

## Key Practitioners
- [[Robert Alter]]
- [[Hans Frei]]
- [[Meir Sternberg]]
- [[Eugene Peterson]]
- [[N.T. Wright (story as primary category)]]

## How It Works
1. Read the text as a unified literary work, not a collection of sources
2. Analyze narrative technique: type scenes, repetition, gaps, irony, characterization
3. The narrator's point of view — is the narrator reliable? Omniscient?
4. The implied reader — what does the text assume its audience knows and believes?
5. Plot structure and resolution — how does the story create meaning through its shape?

## Primary Sources It Illuminates
- Genesis narratives
- Gospel of Mark (narrative pace and urgency)
- Psalms (as poetry)
- Job (as drama)

## Strengths
Reads what's actually there. Accessible to non-specialists. Connects biblical studies to literary studies. Restores the story's power to shape imagination.

## Critiques
Can ignore historical questions entirely. Sometimes imports modern literary categories anachronistically. 'Story' can be used to avoid hard truth claims.

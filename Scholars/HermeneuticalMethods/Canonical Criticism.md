# Canonical Criticism

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_criticism)

> Reads Scripture in its final canonical form as the theologically normative text for the community of faith. Developed primarily by Brevard Childs as a reaction against historical-critical fragmentation. The text to interpret is not the hypothetical sources behind it, nor the original oral units, but the final shape of the canonical book — which is the form the community received and used for worship and instruction.

## Philosophical Roots
- Reaction against historical-critical atomism
- Karl Barth (Scripture as witness to revelation)
- James Sanders (canon as process)

## Key Practitioners
- [[Brevard Childs]]
- [[James Sanders]]
- [[Christopher Seitz]]
- [[Craig Bartholomew]]

## How It Works
1. The final canonical form is the text — not the hypothetical sources behind it
2. Read each book in light of its canonical context (Isaiah 40-55 illuminated by Isaiah 1-39)
3. The OT is read as Christian Scripture — it anticipates the NT
4. The community that received the canon shapes the meaning of individual texts
5. Intertextual reading — later texts in the canon interpret earlier ones

## Primary Sources It Illuminates
- Isaiah (canonical unity vs critical division into Deutero-Isaiah)
- Psalter (canonical shape of the 5-book structure)
- OT/NT relationship

## Strengths
Keeps theology central. Addresses the question the historical method can't: what does this text mean for the community of faith TODAY? Respects the final form the church actually received.

## Critiques
Can flatten the historical diversity of the text. Which canon? (Catholic, Protestant, Orthodox, Ethiopian all differ.) Can be used to avoid hard historical questions.

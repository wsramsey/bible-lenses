# Critical Realism

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_realism_(philosophy_of_the_social_sciences))

> N.T. Wright's methodological framework — a middle path between naive realism (just read it plainly) and radical skepticism (we can't know anything). We can know things about the past, but our knowledge is always perspectival and revisable. Forms hypotheses, tests them against evidence, accepts the best explanation.

## Philosophical Roots
- Karl Popper (falsifiability)
- Imre Lakatos (research programs)
- Michael Polanyi (Personal Knowledge)
- Roy Bhaskar (critical realism in social science)

## Key Practitioners
- [[N.T. Wright]]
- [[Ben Meyer (Aims of Jesus)]]
- [[Anthony Thiselton]]

## How It Works
1. All knowledge is perspectival — the knower brings a worldview to the text
2. But real knowledge is still possible — the text pushes back on false readings  
3. Form a hypothesis about meaning → test against all available evidence
4. The best hypothesis explains the most data with the least special pleading
5. Worldview analysis: every community has stories, symbols, praxis, questions
6. Reconstruct the worldview BEFORE asking what individual texts mean

## Primary Sources It Illuminates
- Pauline epistles
- Gospel narratives
- Second Temple Jewish literature
- Dead Sea Scrolls

## Strengths
Avoids both fundamentalist literalism and postmodern nihilism. Takes history seriously. Explains why the same text produces different readings in different communities.

## Critiques
Can privilege the scholar's reconstruction over the text itself. 'Best explanation' is still a judgment call. Wright's own reconstructions are contested.

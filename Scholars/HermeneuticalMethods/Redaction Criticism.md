# Redaction Criticism

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redaction_criticism)

> Studies how the biblical authors (redactors/editors) shaped their sources to communicate theological messages. Goes beyond Form Criticism (which focused on pre-literary units) to ask: how did Matthew or Luke USE Mark? What do their additions, omissions, and modifications reveal about their theology? Each Gospel writer is a theologian, not just a collector.

## Philosophical Roots
- Building on Form Criticism
- Günther Bornkamm (Matthew)
- Hans Conzelmann (Luke — The Theology of St. Luke)
- Willi Marxsen (Mark)

## Key Practitioners
- [[Günther Bornkamm]]
- [[Hans Conzelmann]]
- [[Willi Marxsen]]
- [[Norman Perrin]]

## How It Works
1. Compare parallel passages across Gospels — note changes Matthew and Luke make to Mark
2. Each change reveals the redactor's theological concern
3. Luke's theology of history (Acts as volume 2) — salvation history in three periods
4. Matthew's ecclesiastical concerns — the church and its mission
5. Apply same method to OT: Chronicles rewrites Kings with priestly theological concerns

## Primary Sources It Illuminates
- Synoptic Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke)
- Books of Kings/Chronicles comparison
- Deuteronomistic History

## Strengths
Takes the final author's theology seriously. Shows each Gospel as a unified theological work, not just a collection. Connects to literary criticism.

## Critiques
Depends on solving the Synoptic Problem first (is Mark really first?). Can make too much of small differences. Sometimes ignores the author's use of eyewitness sources.

# Feminist Hermeneutics

[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_theology)

> A hermeneutics of suspicion (Ricoeur's term applied by Schüssler Fiorenza) applied to the patriarchal biases embedded in Scripture and its interpretation. The canon was selected, transmitted, and interpreted primarily by men — what was lost or suppressed? Phyllis Trible recovers overlooked women's stories; Elisabeth Schüssler Fiorenza reconstructs women's leadership in early Christianity; Womanist theology reads from Black women's triple marginalization.

## Philosophical Roots
- Paul Ricoeur (hermeneutics of suspicion)
- Feminist theory (Betty Friedan, Simone de Beauvoir)
- Post-colonial theory

## Key Practitioners
- [[Phyllis Trible]]

- [[Katie Geneva Cannon (womanist)]]

## How It Works
1. Hermeneutics of suspicion: the text may encode patriarchal bias — read against the grain
2. Recovery: find and amplify the women's voices already present but marginalized
3. Remembrance: reconstruct the lost history of women's leadership in early Christianity
4. Re-reading: patriarchal texts (1 Tim 2:12) in their specific contexts, not as universal
5. Womanist addition: race and class intersect with gender — white feminism is insufficient

## Primary Sources It Illuminates
- Texts of Terror (Trible): Hagar, Tamar, Jephthah's daughter
- Proverbs 31 and wisdom literature
- Mary Magdalene (Gospel of Mary)
- Women disciples in Luke 8
- Phoebe, Junia, Priscilla in Paul's letters

## Strengths
Recovers lost voices. Corrects centuries of androcentric reading. Shows how interpretation shapes community practice.

## Critiques
Risk of reading the text only for what it lacks rather than what it contains. Tension between the authority of Scripture and feminist critique of it. Womanist theologians critique white feminism's blind spots.

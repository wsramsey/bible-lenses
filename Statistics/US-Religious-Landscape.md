# US Religious Landscape
*America's dramatic religious transformation — what the data shows*

## The Big Picture: The Rise of the "Nones"

### Adults Who Identify as Christian
| Year | % Christian | Source |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1976 | ~91% | Gallup |
| 1996 | ~86% | Gallup |
| 2007 | 78% | Pew |
| 2014 | 71% | Pew |
| 2019 | 65% | Pew |
| 2023 | 63% | Pew |

**The rate of decline**: approximately 1 percentage point per year since the 1990s.

### Adults With No Religious Affiliation ("Nones")
| Year | % Nones | Source |
|------|---------|--------|
| 1990 | 8% | ARIS |
| 2007 | 16% | Pew |
| 2014 | 23% | Pew |
| 2019 | 26% | Pew |
| 2023 | 28-30% | Pew/PRRI |

**Key data point**: "Nones" are now the largest single "religious group" in America — larger than evangelical Protestants, larger than Catholics.

- Historically Black Protestant: ~8%

### Catholic: ~20%
### Mormon: ~2%
### Orthodox: ~1%
### Other Christian: ~2%

## Mainline Protestant Collapse

The "Seven Sisters" of mainline Protestantism (United Methodist, ELCA Lutheran, Presbyterian USA, Episcopal, American Baptist, United Church of Christ, Disciples of Christ):

| Denomination | Peak Members | 2023 Members | % Decline |
|-------------|-------------|--------------|-----------|
| United Methodist | 11 million (1965) | ~5 million | -55% |
| ELCA Lutheran | 5.3 million (1987) | ~3.1 million | -42% |
| Presbyterian USA | 4.2 million (1965) | ~1.1 million | -74% |
| Episcopal | 3.4 million (1965) | ~1.6 million | -53% |
| United Church of Christ | 2.1 million (1965) | ~730,000 | -65% |
| American Baptist | 1.6 million (1965) | ~1.0 million | -38% |

**The mainline Protestant world has lost roughly half its membership since 1965 — and the rate of decline is accelerating.**

## Catholic Trends

### Catholic Identification
- 1960s: ~24% of Americans identified as Catholic
- 2023: ~20% identify as Catholic
- The numbers have held more stable than mainline Protestant — because of Hispanic immigration replacing departing Euro-Americans

### Catholic Practice
- 1955: 75% of Catholics attended Mass weekly (Gallup)
- 2022: 17% of Catholics attend Mass weekly (Pew/Gallup)
- Only 40% of those who identify as Catholic say religion is "very important" to them

### Catholic Sex Abuse Impact
- 2002: before the Boston Globe scandals, ~24% of Americans identified as Catholic
- 2019: 20%
- An estimated 2-4 million people have left Catholicism since 2002 partly due to the abuse scandal

## Evangelical Protestant Trends

### Self-Identified Evangelicals
- 2000: ~28% of Americans called themselves "born again or evangelical"
- 2023: ~24% (Pew)
- But: the label has become politically charged; some theological evangelicals now reject the label

### Evangelical Practice
- **Weekly church attendance**: 54% of evangelicals in 1972 → 29% in 2023 (General Social Survey)
- **Bible reading** (daily or near-daily): 35% of evangelicals in 2011 → 27% in 2022 (Barna)

### The Political Complication
- 2016: 81% of white evangelicals voted for Trump
- 2020: 76% of white evangelicals voted for Trump
- Result: "evangelical" is increasingly heard as a political identity, not theological
- Surveys show many theological evangelicals now reject the label to avoid political association

## Generational Data (Pew 2023)

### Religious Identification by Generation
| Generation | Christian | None | Other |
|-----------|-----------|------|-------|
| Silent (born 1928–1945) | 84% | 9% | 7% |
| Baby Boomer (1946–1964) | 76% | 17% | 7% |
| Gen X (1965–1980) | 67% | 25% | 8% |
| Millennial (1981–1996) | 49% | 40% | 11% |
| Gen Z (1997–2012) | ~36% | ~52% | ~12% |

**The generational cliff**: Gen Z is the first American generation where non-Christian is the plurality identity.

### Why Young People Leave (Barna Research)
Top reasons young adults (18-29) leave the church:
1. Church seems overprotective / anti-science: 23%
2. Shallow faith formation: 20%
3. Church unfriendly to doubt: 18%
4. Sexual ethics feel repressive/harmful: 17%
5. Exclusive salvation claims: 15%
6. Church unfriendly to LGBTQ+ people: 14%
7. Political alignment (church too politically partisan): 12%

### The Retention Crisis
- Of those raised in evangelical Protestant homes, 65% remain evangelical Protestants as adults (2023, Pew)
- Of those raised Catholic, 52% remain Catholic as adults
- Of those raised mainline Protestant, 45% remain mainline Protestant
- **The "none" is the most stable — 89% of those raised with no religion remain unaffiliated**

## Church Attendance

### Regular Church Attendance (Gallup)
| Year | "Attend church weekly" | "Attend church monthly" |
|------|----------------------|------------------------|
| 1955 | 49% | — |
| 1980 | 40% | — |
| 2000 | 35% | — |
| 2010 | 30% | — |
| 2020 | 20% | — |
| 2023 | 17% | 22% |

**Note**: Gallup research shows people over-report church attendance by roughly 2x when compared with actual headcounts. True weekly attendance may be 8-10% of adults.

### COVID Impact
- Church attendance dropped 20-30% during COVID lockdowns
- An estimated 30-40% of that drop did not return post-COVID (Barna, 2022)
- An estimated 3,500 Protestant churches close permanently per year in the US

### Belief in Heaven and Hell (Pew 2023)
- Heaven: 55% of Americans believe in heaven
- Hell: 40% believe in hell
- Among Christians: 82% believe in heaven; 70% believe in hell

### Prayer
- 55% of Americans say they pray daily (Pew 2023)
- Among Christians: 74% pray daily
- Among "Nones": 20% still pray regularly (spiritual but not religious)

## Racial/Ethnic Patterns (Pew 2023)

### Christian Identification by Race
| Group | % Christian |
|-------|-------------|
| Black Americans | 79% |
| Hispanic Americans | 68% |
| White Americans | 64% |
| Asian Americans | 34% |

### Denominational Patterns
- **Black Americans**: 79% Christian; majority Baptist or nondenominational
- **Hispanic Americans**: historically Catholic (68% in 1970) → now 47% Catholic, 25% Protestant; fastest-growing evangelical population
- **White Americans**: largest group by number; declining fastest
- **Asian Americans**: significant evangelical Korean-American community; but overall less Christian than other groups

## State-by-State Variation
**Most religious states** (% who say religion is "very important"):
1. Mississippi: 63%
2. Alabama: 58%
3. South Carolina: 57%

**Least religious states**:
1. Vermont: 19%
2. New Hampshire: 20%
3. Maine: 20%
4. Massachusetts: 21%
5. Oregon: 22%

**The Bible Belt** remains distinctly more religious; the Northeast and Pacific Northwest are post-Christian in profile.

## The "Nones" in Detail (Pew 2023)

### Who Are the Nones?
- **Atheist**: 4% of US adults
- **Agnostic**: 5% of US adults
- **Nothing in particular**: ~20% of US adults

### What Do They Believe?
- 17% of "Nones" believe in the God of the Bible
- 33% believe in a higher power or spiritual force
- 26% don't believe in any higher power
- 72% say they seldom or never pray
- But: 20% still pray regularly; 18% believe in heaven

### Why Did They Leave?
- "Just gradually drifted away": 49%
- "Stopped believing in religion's teachings": 20%
- "Negative experiences with religion": 19%
- "Not raised in religion": 12%
